# Sengoku Period

**Category**: History  
**Key figures**: Oda Nobunaga (1534–1582), Toyotomi Hideyoshi (1537–1598), Tokugawa Ieyasu (1543–1616), Takeda Shingen (1521–1573), Uesugi Kenshin (1530–1578), Mōri Motonari (1497–1571)

## Summary

The Sengoku (*Sengoku Jidai*, "Warring States period"), spanning roughly 1467 to 1615, was an era of nearly constant civil warfare, feudal fragmentation, and military innovation in Japan. The period began with the Ōnin War (1467–1477), which destroyed the Ashikaga shogunate's governing authority and fragmented Japan into hundreds of semi-autonomous domains controlled by regional warlords (*daimyo*). For over a century, these military strongmen engaged in incessant territorial conflict, upending the medieval feudal order and creating a landscape of fortified castles, standing armies, and shifting alliances. The era witnessed the introduction of European firearms — from Portuguese traders landing at Tanegashima Island in 1543 — and their rapid adoption into Japanese warfare, fundamentally transforming military tactics.

By the late 16th century, three towering military unifiers — Oda Nobunaga, Toyotomi Hideyoshi, and Tokugawa Ieyasu — successively consolidated Japan through conquest, diplomacy, and institutional reform. Nobunaga's assassination in 1582 left the project incomplete; Hideyoshi completed domestic unification by 1590 but squandered resources on two ultimately failed invasions of Korea (1592–1593 and 1597–1598). After Hideyoshi's death in 1598, Tokugawa Ieyasu outmaneuvered rival lords and won the decisive Battle of Sekigahara on October 21, 1600. His appointment as shogun in 1603 and the destruction of the last Toyotomi holdouts at the Siege of Osaka in 1615 ended the Sengoku era and established the [Edo Period](edo-period.md)'s 264 years of peace.

## Origins: The Ōnin War (1467–1477)

The proximate trigger of the Sengoku period was a succession dispute within the Ashikaga shogunate. When Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa (r. 1449–1473) initially lacked an heir, he designated his brother Yoshimi as successor. When Yoshimasa's son Yoshihisa was born shortly after in 1465, rival factions aligned with the Hosokawa and Yamana clans backed competing claimants. Full-scale warfare erupted in Kyoto in January 1467.

The Ōnin War lasted a decade, reducing much of Kyoto to rubble and demonstrating that the Ashikaga shogunate could no longer enforce order. By the war's end in 1477, the nominal conclusion resolved nothing; fighting shifted to the provinces, where local warlords (*kokujin* and *shugo daimyo*) battled for regional supremacy independent of any central authority. The century that followed saw a phenomenon the Japanese called *gekokujō* ("the low overcoming the high"): men of low birth, including the son of a peasant (Toyotomi Hideyoshi) and the son of a minor warrior (Tokugawa Ieyasu), rose to national power entirely through military and political skill.

## Daimyo, Domains, and Castles

The Sengoku period produced roughly 200–300 significant warlord-controlled domains (*han*) at its height, each essentially an independent military state. *Daimyo* competed not only militarily but also economically: those who built efficient administrative systems, taxed commerce, encouraged artisan industries, and attracted merchant networks often outlasted those who relied on military strength alone.

Castle architecture underwent a revolution during this period. Medieval fortifications (*yamashiro*, mountain redoubts) gave way to lowland flatland and hilltop castles (*hirajiro* and *hirayamashiro*) designed for administrative control rather than purely defensive retreat. These complex castle towns (*jōkamachi*) housed samurai retainers, artisans, and merchants, becoming the nuclei of Japan's early modern urban network. Nobunaga's Azuchi Castle (begun 1576 on the shores of Lake Biwa) was a paradigm shift: a massive seven-story stone-based tower keep designed to project power visually across the surrounding plain. It burned in 1582 following the Honnō-ji Incident but its design influenced every subsequent castle. Hideyoshi's Osaka Castle (begun 1583) and Fushimi Castle (begun 1594), and Ieyasu's Edo Castle (substantially expanded from 1603), extended the tradition.

## Military Transformation

### The Firearms Revolution

Portuguese traders introduced the matchlock arquebus (*tanegashima*, named for the island where Portuguese ships landed in 1543) to Japan. The weapon spread with extraordinary speed: within a generation, Japanese gunsmiths were mass-producing arquebuses of equal or superior quality to Portuguese originals. Estimates suggest several hundred thousand muskets were in circulation by the 1580s — more than in any country in Europe at the time.

Oda Nobunaga is most celebrated for the military application of firearms at the **Battle of Nagashino** (June 28, 1575). Against Takeda Katsuyori's cavalry, Nobunaga deployed approximately 3,000 arquebusiers behind wooden palisades in rotating three-rank volleys, neutralizing the Takeda's formidable mounted samurai. The battle decisively ended the era of cavalry-dominated warfare and demonstrated the strategic superiority of disciplined mass infantry with firearms.

### Troop Composition and Scale

Medieval Japanese armies of the Heian and Kamakura periods had numbered hundreds to a few thousand samurai cavalry. Sengoku armies numbered in the tens of thousands and relied on massed *ashigaru* (foot soldiers) armed with pikes (*yari*), bows, and eventually firearms, with samurai cavalry commanding rather than constituting the primary fighting force. Oda Nobunaga's final campaigns involved forces of 100,000 or more; the Battle of Sekigahara in 1600 engaged perhaps 160,000 combatants on both sides — the largest pitched battle in Japanese history.

### Naval Warfare

Control of sea lanes mattered as much as land campaigns. Nobunaga commissioned Japan's first ironclad vessels (*tetsukōsen*, "iron warships") in 1578 — large flat-bottomed ships with iron-plated hulls to protect against incendiary arrows, employed against the Mōri clan's naval forces in Osaka Bay. Mōri Motonari, whose domain controlled the western Seto Inland Sea and much of western Honshū, built his power partly on naval supremacy; his famous 1555 victory at the Battle of Miyajima used deception and maritime mobility to destroy a larger rival fleet.

## The Three Unifiers

### Oda Nobunaga (1534–1582)

Nobunaga emerged from the minor Owari domain (roughly modern Aichi Prefecture) and first achieved prominence by defeating and killing the much-feared warlord Imagawa Yoshimoto at the **Battle of Okehazama** (June 12, 1560) — a surprise ambush with a force estimated at 2,000–3,000 against Imagawa's 25,000. This stunning upset established Nobunaga's reputation for unconventional tactics and ruthlessness.

By 1568, Nobunaga had captured Kyoto and installed his preferred candidate, Ashikaga Yoshiaki, as shogun. When Yoshiaki conspired against him, Nobunaga expelled him in 1573, ending the Ashikaga shogunate. He systematically destroyed the military-religious power of Buddhist institutions, most infamously in 1571 when he ordered the burning of the entire complex of temples and settlements on **Mount Hiei** (*Enryakuji*), killing by some estimates 3,000–4,000 monks, soldiers, and civilians — an act that shocked contemporaries but eliminated a powerful military rival.

Nobunaga's career ended on June 21, 1582, when his general **Akechi Mitsuhide** launched a surprise attack on him at Honnō-ji temple in Kyoto. Caught with only a small escort, Nobunaga died in the burning temple — whether by the attackers' blades or by his own hand (*seppuku*) is debated in historical sources. Akechi's coup lasted less than two weeks before Hideyoshi force-marched his army back from the siege of Takamatsu Castle — his campaign against the Mōri in the Chūgoku region, a feat remembered as the *Chūgoku Ōgaeshi* ("Great Return from Chūgoku") — and defeated and killed Akechi at the Battle of Yamazaki (July 2, 1582).

### Toyotomi Hideyoshi (1537–1598)

Hideyoshi's biography represents the most dramatic social ascent in Japanese history. Born to a peasant farmer in Owari around 1537, he served Nobunaga as an infantry page and rose through sheer competence and personal charisma to become one of Nobunaga's senior commanders. After Nobunaga's death, Hideyoshi outmaneuvered rival successors to become the effective ruler of Japan.

His unification campaigns included the **Siege of Takamatsu Castle** (1582, interrupted by news of Honnō-ji), the **Battle of Shizugatake** (1583, defeating Shibata Katsuie), the construction of Osaka Castle as his power base (1583–1585), and campaigns that reduced the last resisting domains by 1590. He received the title of *kampaku* (imperial regent) in 1585 — a court rank unavailable to the shogun title (which required Minamoto ancestry) but conveying comparable authority — and became *taikō* (retired regent) in 1591.

Hideyoshi's domestic policies reshaped Japanese society. The **Sword Hunt Edict** (*Katanagari*, 1588) confiscated weapons from farmers, fixing the boundary between samurai and agricultural classes. His **cadastral surveys** (*taikō kenchi*, 1582–1598) standardized land measurement and tax assessment across Japan, creating a more uniform national economic basis. His **class separation edicts** (*hei-nō bunri*) prohibited samurai from returning to farming and farmers from becoming samurai, hardening social boundaries that the Sengoku's social mobility had disrupted.

His two invasions of Korea (the **Imjin War**, 1592–1598) were intended as the first stage of conquering Ming China and possibly India. The first invasion achieved rapid initial successes — Japanese forces captured Seoul within three weeks and pushed to the Yalu River — but Korean Admiral Yi Sun-sin's armored "turtle ships" destroyed Japanese supply fleets, Chinese intervention stabilized the front, and guerrilla resistance wore down Japanese forces. Both campaigns ended in withdrawal. The cost in Japanese lives and resources, estimated at over 100,000 dead, significantly weakened Hideyoshi's coalition. He died on September 18, 1598, aged approximately 61, leaving his five-year-old son Hideyori as nominal heir under a council of five regents — of whom Tokugawa Ieyasu was by far the most powerful.

### Tokugawa Ieyasu (1543–1616)

Ieyasu, born to a minor *daimyo* family of Mikawa Province (modern Aichi), spent his youth as a hostage — first to the Oda clan, then to the Imagawa — developing the patient, calculating temperament that would define his career. He became an independent lord in 1560 following Imagawa Yoshimoto's death at Okehazama, allied with Nobunaga from 1562, and steadily built his power base in eastern Japan.

After Hideyoshi's death in 1598, Ieyasu maneuvered against the other regents, formed alliances, and triggered the confrontation at **Sekigahara** (Gifu Prefecture) on October 21, 1600. His Eastern Coalition (~89,000 troops) defeated the Western Coalition (~82,000) in a battle that lasted roughly six hours. Key to the outcome was the defection of Kobayakawa Hideaki and other Western lords who had secretly negotiated with Ieyasu beforehand. Over 30,000 may have died in the battle and subsequent executions.

Ieyasu was appointed shogun by Emperor Go-Yōzei on March 24, 1603, and immediately transferred the title to his son Hidetada in 1605 to establish the principle of Tokugawa hereditary succession. The surviving Toyotomi heir, Hideyori, remained in Osaka Castle. After engineering pretexts for conflict — allegedly reading the inscription on a newly cast bell at Hōkōji temple (1614) as a curse on the Tokugawa name — Ieyasu launched the **Siege of Osaka**: the Winter Campaign (1614) ended in a truce, and the Summer Campaign (May–June 1615) culminated in the fall of Osaka Castle. Hideyori died in the burning castle, and the last significant Toyotomi loyalists were hunted down. The Sengoku period was definitively over.

## Social and Economic Transformations

The Sengoku century, though defined by warfare, paradoxically accelerated Japan's economic development. Commercial networks expanded to supply armies; castle towns became permanent urban settlements that persisted into the Edo period. The spread of currency, credit, and commercial exchange reached rural areas unprecedented in earlier periods. Rice cultivation techniques spread and improved under competitive *daimyo* who understood that agricultural productivity translated directly into military capability (rice being the basis of both taxation and troop supply).

The period also saw significant Christian missionary activity following Francis Xavier's arrival in Kagoshima in 1549 (see [Kyushu](../geography/major-islands.md)). By the 1580s, an estimated 150,000–200,000 Japanese had converted to Christianity, concentrated in Kyushu where Portuguese trade links were strongest. Nobunaga tolerated and even encouraged Christianity as a counterweight to Buddhist military power. Hideyoshi grew hostile, issuing an expulsion edict for missionaries in 1587 (unevenly enforced) and crucifying 26 Christians in Nagasaki in 1597. The Tokugawa would complete the suppression.

## Cultural Life in the Sengoku Era

The Sengoku period was not purely destructive. Warlords competed as cultural patrons as well as military commanders. The tea ceremony reached its aesthetic apogee during the Sengoku: Sen no Rikyū (1522–1591) served both Nobunaga and Hideyoshi as tea master, and the ritual of *chanoyu* became inseparable from the diplomacy and cultivation of warlord elites. Hideyoshi organized the **Grand Kitano Tea Ceremony** in October 1587 — a massive outdoor tea gathering in the grounds of Kitano Tenmangū shrine in Kyoto, open to all social classes regardless of rank, an extraordinary democratic gesture within the otherwise rigidly hierarchical society. Rikyū eventually fell from Hideyoshi's favor and was ordered to commit *seppuku* in February 1591, an act whose exact motivations remain disputed by historians.

Noh theater, patronized since the 14th century by the Ashikaga shogunate, continued to serve as elite entertainment. The Sengoku period also saw the introduction and rapid spread of *samisen* (lute) music from the Ryukyu Islands — cultural exchange even amid the chaos of war. Kabuki's origins trace to the very end of the Sengoku, with Izumo no Okuni's performances beginning around 1603 in Kyoto.

## Significance

The Sengoku period marks Japan's traumatic but generative transition from medieval feudalism to the early modern nation-state. The military innovations of the era — especially the rapid incorporation of firearms, the castle-based territorial control system, and the large disciplined armies — became foundational to the Tokugawa shogunate's administrative model. The social transformations — class separation, urban development, expanded commerce — established the socioeconomic structure of the [Edo Period](edo-period.md).

The three great unifiers personify the era's strategic diversity: Nobunaga's radical ruthlessness and tactical innovation; Hideyoshi's diplomatic genius and social vision alongside catastrophic foreign policy overreach; Ieyasu's patience, institutional foresight, and willingness to wait and consolidate. The Sengoku's upheaval paradoxically created the conditions for Edo's extraordinary peace. The [Samurai & Bushido](../culture-society/samurai-bushido.md) code crystallized precisely as samurai were being turned into administrators rather than warriors — bushido as philosophy emerged most fully once the actual combat it theorized had ended.
