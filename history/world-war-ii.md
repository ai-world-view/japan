# World War II

**Category**: History
**Key figures**: Emperor [Hirohito](../people/hirohito.md), Prime Minister / General Hideki Tojo, Admiral Yamamoto Isoroku, General Douglas MacArthur, Foreign Minister Yosuke Matsuoka, General Tomoyuki Yamashita, Admiral Chester Nimitz

## Summary

Japan's involvement in World War II (1941–1945) began with the attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, and represented the catastrophic culmination of nearly two decades of imperial expansion across East Asia and the Pacific. Japan entered the war as one of the Axis powers and ended it under the world's first nuclear attack, surrendering unconditionally in August 1945 after suffering approximately 2.5–3 million military and civilian dead. The war's aftermath—Allied occupation and a new pacifist constitution—fundamentally remade the Japanese state.

## Imperial Expansion: 1931–1941

Japan's path to the Pacific War traced through a decade of militarist expansion rooted in resource scarcity, ultranationalist ideology, and unresolved Great Depression-era economic pressures. Key milestones:

**1931 — Manchurian Incident**: On September 18, 1931, officers of the Kwantung Army staged the Mukden Incident, a fabricated railway explosion used as a pretext to invade and occupy Manchuria. Japan established the puppet state of Manchukuo (March 1932), placing the last Qing emperor Puyi on the throne. The League of Nations' Lytton Commission condemned the seizure; Japan withdrew from the League in March 1933.

**1937 — Second Sino-Japanese War**: Full-scale war with China began in July 1937 after the Marco Polo Bridge Incident near Beijing. Japanese forces captured Nanjing (December 1937) in an episode of mass atrocity—the Nanjing Massacre—in which an estimated 100,000 to 300,000 Chinese civilians and prisoners of war were killed over six weeks. The war against China consumed enormous Japanese resources and never achieved the decisive outcome Tokyo expected.

**1940 — Tripartite Pact**: On September 27, 1940, Japan formally allied with Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy, positioning itself as an Axis power. Japan simultaneously occupied French Indochina (July 1941), triggering the United States, the United Kingdom, and the Netherlands to impose an oil embargo. Since Japan imported approximately 80% of its oil, the embargo forced a decision: withdraw from China and Indochina, or seize the petroleum-rich Dutch East Indies by force—which meant war with the Western powers.

**1941 — Decision for War**: At an Imperial Conference on December 1, 1941, [Emperor Hirohito](../people/hirohito.md) ratified the decision to go to war with the United States, the British Empire, and the Netherlands. Combined Fleet Commander Admiral Yamamoto Isoroku had argued against the war on strategic grounds, warning: "I shall run wild considerably for the first six months or a year, but I have utterly no confidence for the second and third years." He nonetheless planned and commanded the Pearl Harbor attack.

## The Pacific War: 1941–1942

At 7:48 AM on December 7, 1941 (Hawaiian time), 353 Japanese aircraft launched from six carriers attacked the US Pacific Fleet at Pearl Harbor, Oahu, Hawaii. The strike killed 2,403 Americans, wounded 1,178, sank four battleships (including the USS Arizona), and destroyed 188 aircraft. The United States declared war on Japan the following day; Germany and Italy declared war on the United States on December 11.

Japanese forces moved simultaneously across a vast arc of Southeast Asia and the Pacific:

- **Malaya and Singapore** (December 1941–February 1942): General Yamashita Tomoyuki's 25th Army advanced 1,100 km down the Malay Peninsula in 70 days, capturing Singapore on February 15, 1942—the largest surrender of British-led forces in history (80,000–85,000 troops).
- **Philippines** (December 1941–May 1942): Japanese forces overcame American and Filipino resistance; General Douglas MacArthur was evacuated to Australia, vowing "I shall return."
- **Dutch East Indies** (January–March 1942): Japan seized Dutch colonial oil fields in Borneo and Sumatra, securing the resources that had motivated going to war.
- **Burma** (January–May 1942): British and Indian forces were expelled from Burma; Japan reached the border with India.

By May 1942, Japan controlled a defense perimeter spanning from the Aleutian Islands in the north, across the Pacific, through Southeast Asia, to the border of India in the west—the largest empire in Asia's history.

## Turning Points: Midway to the Philippine Sea

The limits of Japanese expansion became apparent in the spring and summer of 1942:

**Battle of Coral Sea (May 4–8, 1942)**: The first naval battle in which opposing fleets never sighted each other directly, fought entirely by carrier aircraft. American forces prevented the Japanese landing at Port Moresby, New Guinea.

**Battle of Midway (June 4–7, 1942)**: The decisive strategic turning point. US Navy codebreakers had cracked the Japanese naval cipher (JN-25), enabling Admiral Chester Nimitz to position his three carriers in ambush. Japan lost four fleet carriers—Akagi, Kaga, Sōryū, and Hiryū—and their trained aircrews in a single day, June 4, 1942. The United States lost the carrier Yorktown. Japan's offensive capability was permanently crippled; the initiative in the Pacific passed to the Allies.

**Guadalcanal Campaign (August 1942–February 1943)**: American forces landed on Guadalcanal in the Solomon Islands on August 7, 1942, seizing a Japanese airfield. A brutal six-month campaign of ground, air, and naval battles followed. Japan evacuated the island in February 1943, having lost approximately 24,000 troops to combat, disease, and starvation.

**Battle of the Philippine Sea (June 19–20, 1944)**: American carrier aircraft and submarines destroyed three Japanese carriers and approximately 600 aircraft, including newly trained aviators. American pilots called it the "Marianas Turkey Shoot." Japan's carrier-based aviation never recovered.

## Strategic Bombing and Collapse

With the fall of Saipan (June–July 1944) and Tinian (July–August 1944), the United States obtained airbases within range of the Japanese home islands for B-29 Superfortress bombers. From late 1944, the XX Bomber Command (and then the XXI Bomber Command under General Curtis LeMay) systematically attacked Japanese cities.

On the night of **March 9–10, 1945**, LeMay shifted to low-altitude incendiary attacks. A raid of 279 B-29s dropped approximately 1,700 tons of incendiary bombs on [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md), creating a firestorm that killed an estimated **83,000–100,000 civilians** in a single night—the deadliest air raid in history. The raid destroyed 16 square miles of the city and rendered approximately 267,000 people homeless. Similar attacks were carried out against Osaka, Nagoya, Kobe, Yokohama, and 64 other cities through the summer of 1945.

By July 1945, most major Japanese cities had been substantially destroyed, industrial production had collapsed, and Japan faced imminent starvation due to the naval blockade.

## The Atomic Bombings and Soviet Invasion

In August 1945, three overlapping shocks ended the war:

**Hiroshima (August 6, 1945)**: The B-29 "Enola Gay," piloted by Colonel Paul Tibbets, dropped "Little Boy"—a uranium gun-type bomb with an explosive yield of approximately 15 kilotons—on the city of Hiroshima at 8:15 AM local time. An estimated **70,000–80,000 people were killed immediately**; total deaths by end of 1945 including radiation effects are estimated at 90,000–166,000. Hiroshima's built-up area was destroyed over approximately 4.7 square miles.

**Soviet Declaration of War (August 8, 1945)**: The Soviet Union, honoring a commitment made at the Yalta Conference (February 1945), declared war on Japan and invaded Japanese-controlled Manchuria, Korea, and Sakhalin with 1.5 million troops. The rapid Soviet advance annihilated the Kwantung Army and removed the possibility of a negotiated peace through Soviet mediation—one of Japan's remaining strategic options.

**Nagasaki (August 9, 1945)**: The B-29 "Bockscar," piloted by Major Charles Sweeney, dropped "Fat Man"—a plutonium implosion bomb with a yield of approximately 21 kilotons—on Nagasaki at 11:02 AM local time. An estimated **40,000 people were killed immediately**; total deaths through 1945 estimated at 60,000–80,000.

## Surrender and Occupation

On August 15, 1945, [Emperor Hirohito](../people/hirohito.md) delivered a recorded radio address—the first time most Japanese had heard his voice—announcing Japan's acceptance of the Potsdam Declaration and unconditional surrender. The surrender was formally signed aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay on **September 2, 1945**, with the ceremony conducted by General Douglas MacArthur on behalf of the Allied powers.

Allied occupation of Japan (1945–1952) was conducted primarily by the United States under MacArthur's Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers (SCAP). The occupation dismantled the militarist state and rebuilt Japan's institutions:

- **War crimes trials**: The International Military Tribunal for the Far East (1946–1948) tried 28 senior Japanese military and political leaders; seven, including former Prime Minister Tojo Hideki, were executed.
- **Dissolution of the military**: The Imperial Japanese Army and Navy were dissolved; Japan was stripped of all overseas territories.
- **Economic reforms**: The zaibatsu (財閥) industrial conglomerates were broken up; land reform redistributed agricultural property.
- **The 1947 Constitution**: The [postwar constitution](../government-politics/postwar-constitution.md), drafted under American oversight and promulgated May 3, 1947, established parliamentary democracy, guaranteed civil liberties, and enshrined Article 9—which explicitly renounced war and prohibited maintenance of war potential. This constitution remains in force today.

The occupation formally ended with the Treaty of San Francisco, signed September 8, 1951 (effective April 28, 1952), restoring Japanese sovereignty.

## Significance

World War II marked the total collapse of the imperial military state that had dominated Japan since the [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md) of 1868 and governed the country through accelerating militarism from the 1930s. Defeat and occupation became the crucible for modern democratic Japan: the [postwar constitution](../government-politics/postwar-constitution.md), the pacifist national identity, the US-Japan security alliance, and Japan's postwar economic strategy of export-led growth were all direct products of the war's outcome.

The memory of WWII—particularly the atomic bombings—shaped a durable Japanese anti-nuclear and anti-militarist public consensus that outlasted the occupation. Yet the war's legacy remains deeply contested: Japanese politicians' visits to Yasukuni Shrine (which enshrines war dead, including 14 Class-A war criminals), unresolved disputes over "comfort women" with South Korea, and territorial disputes with China and Russia over islands seized in the war's aftermath continue to generate diplomatic friction.

The destruction of [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md) and other major cities in 1944–1945—and their rapid reconstruction in the 1950s and 1960s—accounts for much of Japan's urban landscape today, which replaced structures that would otherwise have preserved a material record of Edo-period and Meiji-era architecture.
