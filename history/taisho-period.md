# Taishō Period (1912–1926)

**Category**: History  
**Key figures**: Emperor Taishō (Yoshihito), Hirohito (regent 1921–1926), Hara Takashi, Yoshino Sakuzō, Hiratsuka Raichō, Ōkuma Shigenobu, Katō Takaaki

## Summary

The Taishō era (July 30, 1912–December 25, 1926) marked fourteen years of social transformation between the Meiji Restoration's rigid modernization and the militarism of the Shōwa period. Named after the reign of Emperor Yoshihito (Emperor Taishō, 1879–1926), who suffered from a progressive neurological illness that gradually incapacitated him, the era saw effective governance increasingly transferred to oligarchs, party politicians, and ultimately his son Hirohito, who assumed the role of prince regent on November 25, 1921.

Japan entered the Taishō era as an established imperial power, having won decisive victories in the Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895) and the Russo-Japanese War (1904–1905) — the latter the first modern military triumph of an Asian nation over a European power. These wars brought colonial territories: Taiwan (1895), the Kwantung Leasehold (1905), and Korea (formally annexed 1910 under Meiji). World War I (1914–1918) proved an unexpected economic windfall: with European manufacturing disrupted, Japan captured export markets in Asia, growing its industrial output dramatically and accumulating significant foreign exchange reserves.

Domestically, the era witnessed Japan's most vigorous experiment with representative democracy before 1945. Party politicians challenged the oligarchic *genrō* (elder statesman) system that had dominated Meiji governance. In 1918, Hara Takashi became Japan's first prime minister drawn from a commoner background and the first to lead a government based on commanding a parliamentary majority (the Seiyūkai party). His assassination by a railway worker on November 4, 1921, was a portent of the political violence to come. The pivotal achievement of "Taishō Democracy" (*Taishō demokurashī*) was the General Election Law of March 29, 1925, which granted universal male suffrage to men aged 25 and over, expanding the electorate from approximately 3 million to 14 million voters. Simultaneously — and ominously — the Peace Preservation Law (Chian Ijihō) of the same year criminalized advocacy for changing the *kokutai* (national polity) or abolishing the private property system, enabling the suppression of socialist, communist, and labor-organizing movements.

The era ended with the death of Emperor Yoshihito on December 25, 1926, and the accession of Hirohito, beginning the Shōwa period.

## Political Developments

### Party Politics and the Taishō Democracy Movement

The "Taishō Democracy" movement was both a political program and an intellectual current. Political scientist Yoshino Sakuzō (1878–1933) formulated the concept of *minponshugi* ("democracy based on the people") in a landmark 1916 essay, arguing that while sovereignty formally resided in the emperor, government should be conducted for the benefit of and responsive to the people — a formulation carefully calibrated to avoid challenging imperial sovereignty directly. Yoshino's ideas galvanized university students and labor activists and provided intellectual grounding for party government demands.

The *genrō* system — through which a small group of Meiji-era elder statesmen (including Yamagata Aritomo and Saionji Kinmochi) informally nominated prime ministers — faced mounting criticism. The Taishō Political Crisis of 1912–1913 erupted when the army's demand for two new divisions was refused by civilian ministers; the resulting cabinet collapse prompted mass protests under the slogan *kensei yōgo* ("protect constitutional government"). The movement forced the resignation of the Katsura Tarō cabinet after just 53 days in what became known as the "Taishō Political Change" (*Taishō Seihen*).

The Seiyūkai and Kenseikai parties alternated in power through the 1920s. Katō Takaaki's Kenseikai cabinet (1924–1926) carried the universal suffrage reform to completion and also undertook significant diplomatic liberalization, reducing the army by four divisions and negotiating the Washington Naval Treaty (1922) compliance framework.

### Assassination and Political Instability

Political violence shadowed the era. Prime Minister Hara Takashi was stabbed to death at Tokyo Station on November 4, 1921, by a young railway worker with vague ultranationalist grievances. Crown Prince Hirohito survived the Toranomon Incident (December 27, 1923), when anarchist Namba Daisuke fired at his motorcade. These episodes revealed the fragility of civilian governance beneath the surface of democratic progress.

## Economic Transformation

### The World War I Boom

The outbreak of World War I in August 1914 removed European competitors from Asian markets and generated enormous demand for Japanese exports: textiles, machinery, and ships. Japan's industrial production roughly doubled between 1914 and 1919. The trade surplus swelled from negligible to over ¥1 billion annually. New industries — chemicals, steel, electrical equipment — expanded rapidly, concentrated in the Osaka–Kobe–Nagoya industrial belt. Japan transformed from a net debtor to a creditor nation during this period.

### Postwar Recession and the Rice Riots

The postwar deflation after 1920 struck Japan hard. Demand for Japanese goods collapsed as European industry recovered, triggering a banking crisis and rising unemployment. Rice prices, which had surged during wartime, provoked the Rice Riots (Kome Sōdō) of August 1918 — the largest popular uprising in modern Japanese history to that point — involving over 700,000 participants in more than 300 locations across the country, demanding lower food prices. The government deployed the army in some areas and the riots contributed to the fall of the Terauchi cabinet.

### Labor Movement

The Yūaikai (Friendly Society), founded in 1912 by Suzuki Bunji with just 15 members, grew into Japan's first major labor federation. By the early 1920s it had reorganized as the Nihon Rōdō Sōdōmei (Japan General Federation of Labor). Textile workers — predominantly young women from rural areas — organized strikes in major spinning mills. The 1921 Kawasaki and Mitsubishi dockyard strike at Kobe involved over 30,000 workers and lasted nearly two months. These organizing efforts faced escalating suppression under the Peace Preservation Law and police infiltration.

## The Great Kantō Earthquake (1923)

At 11:58 a.m. on September 1, 1923, an earthquake of magnitude 7.9 struck the Sagami Bay area, with the rupture propagating directly beneath the Tokyo-Yokohama metropolitan region. The disaster unfolded in three compounding waves: ground shaking destroyed wooden buildings; fires, fueled by overturned cooking stoves at lunchtime, consumed much of the city; and a tsunami struck the Sagami Bay coast. Approximately 142,000 people died — the majority from fire — making it the deadliest natural disaster in Japanese history.

The destruction was nearly total in Yokohama and severe throughout Tokyo's low-lying eastern wards. The Imperial Hotel, designed by Frank Lloyd Wright on a floating foundation, famously survived largely intact. Reconstruction required a decade and reshaped the capital's urban form, though grand plans for a rationalized modern city by Interior Minister Gotō Shinpei were largely overridden by property owners eager to rebuild quickly on original lots.

A dark aftermath accompanied the catastrophe: rumors that Koreans and socialists were poisoning wells and looting spread by word of mouth in the first chaotic days. Vigilante groups (jikeidan) and police massacred an estimated 6,000 Koreans in the Tokyo and Kanagawa area over several days. Anarchist leaders Ōsugi Sakae and Itō Noe were murdered by military police on September 16. These events exposed the violent underside of Taishō social tensions.

## Cultural Modernization

### Taishō Modernity and Mass Culture

The Taishō era saw the flowering of a distinctively modern urban culture, absorbing Western fashions with speed and creativity. Cafés (*kissaten*), department stores (Mitsukoshi expanded its flagship in 1914), and cinemas (*eiga*) proliferated in Tokyo and Osaka. Jazz arrived in the early 1920s and was performed in dance halls and cafés. Silent films attracted enormous audiences, accompanied by *benshi* narrators who voiced the action; the director Makino Shōzō, based in Kyoto, is often called the father of Japanese cinema for building the nascent film industry.

The "modern girl" (*moga*, from *modern gāru*) and "modern boy" (*mobo*) became cultural archetypes of the era: young urban consumers who adopted Western dress, frequented cafés, and challenged traditional gender roles. Magazines such as *Fujin Kōron* (Women's Review, founded 1916) and the popular general-interest press expanded readership dramatically, creating a mass-media culture unprecedented in Japan. Department stores like Takashimaya and Isetan cultivated aspirational consumer identities among the urban middle class.

### Literature and Intellectual Life

The Taishō period produced a rich literary harvest. The *Shirakaba* (White Birch) literary group, founded 1910, championed individualism, humanism, and Western aesthetics; members included Mushakōji Saneatsu and Shiga Naoya. Akutagawa Ryūnosuke (1892–1927) wrote psychological short stories including "Yabu no Naka" ("In a Grove," 1922) and "Rashōmon" (1915), probing moral ambiguity; his suicide in 1927 became emblematic of the era's anxieties. Natsume Sōseki, whose major novels span late Meiji into early Taishō, died in 1916; his work on individual alienation under rapid modernization defined the intellectual mood.

### The Seitō Movement and Women's Liberation

The Seitō (Bluestocking) literary journal, founded September 1911 by Hiratsuka Raichō (1886–1971), became the centerpiece of early Japanese feminist thought. Its inaugural issue declared: "In the beginning, woman was the sun." Seitō published women's poetry, fiction, and political essays and tackled then-taboo subjects including abortion, chastity, and prostitution. The journal ceased publication in 1916 but launched the careers of Itō Noe and others. The "New Woman" (*atarashii onna*) that Seitō represented became a contested cultural figure, celebrated by progressives and condemned by conservatives as a threat to the family system. See also [Women in Japanese Society](../culture-society/women-in-japanese-society.md).

## The End of Taishō and the Authoritarian Turn

The Peace Preservation Law of 1925 — enacted the same month as universal male suffrage — was the fulcrum of the era's ambiguity. Initially targeting communists and anarchists, it was amended in 1928 and subsequently weaponized against all political dissent, including moderate labor organizers and liberal academics. Mass arrests of communist sympathizers in the March 15 Incident (1928) under the incoming Shōwa government signaled the direction of travel.

Emperor Yoshihito died on December 25, 1926. The short Taishō reign had witnessed more rapid social change — urbanization, mass culture, labor organizing, democratic politics — than any comparable period in Japanese history. Yet the institutions of party government proved unable to contain the military's growing autonomous political role, the pressures of economic crisis (the Shōwa Depression of 1927, worsened by the Great Depression after 1929), and a resurgent nationalism that would culminate in the Manchurian Incident (1931) and the militaristic Shōwa era.

## Significance

The Taishō period represents the clearest "road not taken" moment in modern Japanese political history: a functioning multi-party democracy, an organized labor movement, a vibrant civil society press, and a cosmopolitan urban culture all coexisted within a constitutional monarchy. The granting of universal male suffrage in 1925 was a genuine democratic watershed. That the same year also brought the Peace Preservation Law illustrates the deep ambivalence of Taishō liberalism: gains were consistently paired with structural constraints. The era established cultural and economic patterns — department store consumer culture, mass media, industrial unionism — that persisted through militarism and into the postwar period. Understanding Taishō is essential to understanding how Japan transitioned from a modernizing imperial state to a militaristic one within a single generation, and why the postwar democratic project attempted to build more durable institutions than the party politics of the 1920s had managed to sustain.
