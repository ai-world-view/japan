# Meiji Restoration

**Category**: History  
**Key figures**: Emperor Meiji (Mutsuhito), Ōkubo Toshimichi, Saigō Takamori, Iwakura Tomomi, Itō Hirobumi, Kido Takayoshi

## Summary

The Meiji Restoration (明治維新, *Meiji Ishin*), initiated on January 3, 1868, was a sweeping political and social transformation that ended nearly 265 years of Tokugawa shogunate rule and restored direct imperial authority under Emperor Meiji (personal name Mutsuhito, born November 3, 1852). Led by reformist samurai from western domains—primarily Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa, and Hizen—the movement dismantled Japan's feudal order and launched the fastest top-down modernization in recorded history. Within a single generation, Japan transformed from a largely agrarian, sword-bearing feudal society into an industrializing state capable of challenging Western imperial powers.

The restoration's catalyst was the crisis triggered by Commodore Matthew Perry's forced opening of Japan in 1853–1854, which demonstrated the technological gap between Japan and Western powers and discredited the Tokugawa government. The rallying slogan *sonnō jōi* ("revere the emperor, expel the barbarians") evolved, once the reformers took power, into a pragmatic program of selective Westernization summarized by the phrase *fukoku kyōhei* ("enrich the country, strengthen the military").

## Background: End of the Edo Period

The Tokugawa shogunate had maintained a policy of *sakoku* (closed country) since the 1630s, severely restricting foreign trade and contact. When Perry's "Black Ships" arrived in Uraga Bay on July 8, 1853, the shogunate's inability to repel the threat exposed its weakness. A series of unequal treaties—the Convention of Kanagawa (March 31, 1854) and the Harris Treaty (1858)—opened Japanese ports and granted extraterritorial rights to foreigners, further eroding the shogunate's legitimacy.

A coalition of samurai from Satsuma and Chōshū domains, having fought and lost small engagements against Western warships in 1863–1864, drew the opposite conclusion from most conservatives: Japan could only survive by adopting Western military technology and institutional models. They overthrew the last shogun, Tokugawa Yoshinobu, who formally surrendered power on November 9, 1867. Resistance continued in the **Boshin War** (1868–1869), in which pro-shogunate forces were defeated at battles including Toba–Fushimi (January 27–30, 1868) and the naval Battle of Hakodate (May 11, 1869), the final engagement of the old order on [Hokkaido](../geography/major-islands.md).

## The Charter Oath and Foundational Reforms (1868–1873)

On **April 6, 1868**, Emperor Meiji swore the **Five Charter Oath** (Gokajō no Goseimon), committing the new government to deliberative assemblies, unified national effort, abandonment of outdated customs, seeking knowledge worldwide, and rule of law. This document set the ideological frame for subsequent reforms.

Key early measures included:

- **Abolition of the domain system (haihan chiken), August 1871**: The 270-odd feudal domains were replaced by prefectures under centrally appointed governors, consolidating national authority and breaking the power of regional lords (*daimyō*).
- **Emancipation Edict, 1871**: Abolished hereditary outcast status (*burakumin*), though discrimination persisted.
- **Iwakura Mission, December 1871 – September 1873**: A 107-member delegation led by Iwakura Tomomi traveled to the United States, Britain, France, Germany, and ten other nations to study Western institutions. Its findings shaped education, law, and government structure for decades.
- **Land Tax Reform (Chisokaisei), 1873**: Replaced payment in rice with a cash tax of 3% of land value, providing the government a stable revenue base independent of harvest variability.
- **Conscription Ordinance (Chōheirei), January 10, 1873**: Replaced the samurai class as the military arm with a universal male conscript army modeled on the French and Prussian armies. The samurai's monopoly on bearing arms was effectively ended; carrying swords in public was banned in 1876.

## Industrialization and Infrastructure

The Meiji government treated industrial development as a national security priority. State-owned factories, mines, and shipyards were established throughout the 1870s, then sold to private interests at below-market prices in 1880–1884, creating the core of the *zaibatsu* conglomerates—Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo, and Yasuda—that would dominate Japanese industry for decades.

Transportation was central to national integration. Japan's **first railway**, a 29-kilometre line connecting Shimbashi (Tokyo) and Yokohama, was formally opened on **October 14, 1872** (a provisional service over part of the route had begun that June). By 1912 the national railway network had grown to approximately 7,800 kilometres. The first telegraph line opened in 1869; by 1871 Japan was connected to the international telegraph network via Shanghai. Modern banking, including the founding of the Bank of Japan in 1882, provided the financial infrastructure for industrialization. Japan's GDP grew at roughly 3% annually between 1870 and 1910—modest by later standards but remarkable for the era.

## Constitutional Monarchy and Education

Itō Hirobumi, who would become Japan's first Prime Minister under the new system, spent 18 months in Europe in 1882–1883 studying constitutional models, concluding that the Prussian/German model best suited Japan's goals of preserving imperial authority. The **Meiji Constitution** (*Dai-Nippon Teikoku Kenpō*) was promulgated on **February 11, 1889** and took effect on **November 29, 1890**. It established a bicameral legislature (the Imperial Diet), an independent judiciary, and guaranteed certain rights—but sovereignty resided explicitly in the Emperor, and the military was outside civilian cabinet control, a structural flaw with disastrous later consequences.

The **Imperial Rescript on Education** (October 30, 1890) became the ideological companion to the constitution, prescribing Confucian loyalty to the emperor and state as the core of moral education. A national compulsory education system established from 1872 achieved near-universal elementary literacy by the 1900s, rising from roughly 28% male and 3% female literacy in 1868.

## Military Victories and Great-Power Status

The new military system was tested and vindicated in two wars:

- **First Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895)**: Japan decisively defeated Qing China. The **Treaty of Shimonoseki** (April 17, 1895) awarded Japan Taiwan, the Penghu Islands, and an indemnity of 200 million taels. The Liaodong Peninsula was retroceded under French-German-Russian "Triple Intervention" pressure, a humiliation that intensified military ambition.
- **Russo-Japanese War (1904–1905)**: Japan's victory—including Admiral Tōgō Heihachirō's annihilation of the Russian Baltic Fleet at the **Battle of Tsushima** (May 27–28, 1905)—shocked the world. The **Treaty of Portsmouth** (September 5, 1905), brokered by U.S. President Theodore Roosevelt, gave Japan the Liaodong leasehold, the South Manchuria Railway, and the southern half of Sakhalin Island below the 50th parallel. Japan became the first non-Western nation in modern times to defeat a European power, altering global perceptions of racial hierarchy.

Japan annexed Korea on August 22, 1910, completing its emergence as an imperial power on the Asian continent.

## Internal Tensions: The Satsuma Rebellion

The transformation was not without violent opposition. Former samurai resentful of the loss of their privileges staged several uprisings, culminating in the **Satsuma Rebellion (January–September 1877)**, led by Saigō Takamori—ironically one of the Meiji Restoration's architects. An army of 40,000 former samurai was defeated by 60,000 conscript soldiers, demonstrating the new military's effectiveness. Saigō died by ritual suicide (or in battle) on September 24, 1877. His death closed the era of armed samurai resistance and solidified the conscript army's legitimacy.

## Significance

The Meiji Restoration prevented Japanese colonization by Western powers—a fate that befell most of Asia and Africa in the same century—and positioned Japan as a modernizing state within the existing international order rather than outside it. The restoration demonstrated that non-Western societies could achieve rapid industrialization without surrendering to colonial domination, making Japan a model studied by reformers in China, Egypt, Turkey, and elsewhere.

The era's institutional legacy was ambiguous: the Meiji Constitution's insulation of the military from civilian control contributed directly to the militarism of the 1930s–1940s. The emperor-centered ideology, including the elevation of [Shinto](../culture-society/shinto-religion.md) as a state religion in 1868 and subsequent State Shinto doctrine, was weaponized to mobilize the population for imperial expansion before being dismantled by Allied occupation authorities in December 1945.

Emperor Meiji died on July 30, 1912, ending the Meiji era. Japan had, in 44 years, moved from feudal isolation to a position among the great powers—a transformation with no close historical parallel.
