# Buddhist Schools & Traditions

**Buddhism** arrived in Japan in the mid-6th century (traditionally 538 or 552 CE) from the Korean kingdom of Baekje, bringing with it a sophisticated religious philosophy, institutional structures, canonical texts, and aesthetic traditions that would become woven into the fabric of Japanese civilization. Rather than displacing the indigenous Shinto tradition, Buddhism merged with it in a syncretic relationship known as *shinbutsu-shūgō* (神仏習合, "kami-Buddha syncretism"), sustained by the theological doctrine of *honji-suijaku* (本地垂迹): the teaching that Shinto *kami* were local manifestations (*suijaku*, "traces") of universal Buddhas and bodhisattvas (*honji*, "original ground"). This framework allowed Buddhist temples and Shinto shrines to share sacred precincts and ritual calendars for over a millennium. Over fifteen centuries, Japan developed one of the world's most diverse and philosophically sophisticated Buddhist traditions, producing original schools found nowhere else in Asia.

## Introduction of Buddhism and the Nara Period (538–794)

### Arrival and Court Patronage

Buddhism arrived in Japan through Chinese and Korean transmission, carried by monks, traders, and diplomatic missions. The religion quickly gained the patronage of the imperial court and the Yamato nobility, who saw in it both spiritual depth and a tool for state administration and cultural sophistication. The **Soga clan**, one of the most powerful noble houses of the 6th century, championed Buddhism against the more conservative Mononobe and Nakatomi clans who favored Shinto ritual. The Soga victory in a series of court power struggles (climaxing in the killing of Mononobe no Moriya in 587) established Buddhism as the court religion.

**Prince Shōtoku** (574–622), regent to Empress Suiko, issued the **Seventeen-Article Constitution** (604 CE), which opened with "Revere the Three Treasures"—the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha—embedding Buddhism in governance. Shōtoku oversaw construction of Hōryū-ji temple in Nara Prefecture (completed c. 607), the oldest surviving wooden structure in the world.

### The Six Nara Schools

In the **Nara period** (710–794), six major Buddhist philosophical schools were established at the imperial capital of Nara, sponsored by the court and primarily maintained by monks studying Chinese Buddhist scholarship:

- **Kusha** (俱舍宗) — based on the *Abhidharmakośa* of Vasubandhu; analyzed the seventy-five dharmas (basic constituents of existence).
- **Jōjitsu** (成実宗) — based on the *Satyasiddhi-śāstra*; taught that both the self and dharmas were ultimately empty. Often treated as a sub-branch of Sanron.
- **Ritsu** (律宗) — focused on the Vinaya monastic code. The Chinese monk **Jianzhen** (Ganjin in Japanese, 688–763) arrived in 754 after five failed sea voyages to establish proper Vinaya ordination, founding Tōshōdai-ji in Nara.
- **Sanron** (三論宗) — the Three Treatises school, based on Nāgārjuna's *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*; emphasized the emptiness of all phenomena.
- **Hossō** (法相宗) — the Consciousness-Only (Yogācāra) school, examining how the mind constructs reality through the eight consciousnesses (*vijñāna*), centered at **Kōfuku-ji** temple in Nara. Hossō flourished under the monk Dōshō (629–700) who studied in China under Xuanzang.
- **Kegon** (華厳宗) — the Flower Garland school, based on the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*; taught the interpenetration and mutual dependence of all phenomena. Centered at **Tōdai-ji** temple, whose 15-meter bronze Great Buddha (*Rushana Butsu*, a.k.a. Vairocana) was completed in 752 under Emperor Shōmu's patronage. Tōdai-ji's Daibutsuden (Great Buddha Hall) remains the world's largest wooden building.

These schools were primarily monastic and elitist, centered in the great Nara temples and serving the court and nobility. The Emperor Shōmu's initiative to build a network of provincial temples (*kokubunji*) in every province in 741 spread Buddhist institutional infrastructure across Japan. These schools did not yet have mass popular followings.

## The Heian Period and the Rise of Japanese Buddhist Schools (794–1185)

The shift to the Heian period brought a transformation in Japanese Buddhism. As political power moved from the imperial court to the Fujiwara regency, and as Japan's embassy system to Tang China ended in 894, Japanese Buddhism developed its own distinctively Japanese philosophical currents.

### Tendai (天台宗)

**Tendai** was founded by the monk **Saichō** (767–822, posthumous title: Dengyō Daishi), who studied in China and returned to establish a new school on **Mount Hiei** (Hieizan) northeast of Kyoto in 805. Tendai synthesized multiple Buddhist currents — the *Lotus Sutra* philosophy (emphasizing the universality of Buddha-nature and the unity of all teachings), esoteric Tantra, Pure Land devotion, and Zen-style meditation — into a comprehensive system. This synthetic approach, called *enmitsu itchi* ("unity of esoteric and exoteric"), distinguished Tendai from the narrower Chinese Tiantai school on which it was modeled.

Saichō's most significant institutional battle was his campaign to establish an independent Tendai Mahāyāna ordination platform at Mount Hiei, free from the control of the Nara schools. The Nara establishment fiercely resisted, and Saichō died in 822 without winning approval; the ordination platform was granted just one week after his death. This autonomy was foundational to Tendai's explosive growth.

Mount Hiei became an enormous monastic complex — at its height home to over 3,000 buildings and thousands of monks — engaged in study, meditation, ritual, and (controversially) organized military activities through the *sōhei* (warrior monks). The *sōhei* of Enryaku-ji at Hieizan were notorious for marching on Kyoto with portable shrines to press political demands on the court.

Tendai's importance lies partly in what it produced: many founders of later schools — Hōnen, Shinran, Dōgen, Eisai, and Nichiren — all trained at Mount Hiei before establishing their own movements.

### Shingon (真言宗) — Esoteric Buddhism

**Shingon** ("true word") was founded by **Kūkai** (774–835, posthumous title: Kōbō Daishi), a Japanese monk of extraordinary intellectual range who studied esoteric Buddhism in Chang'an under the Chinese master Huiguo from 804–806. Kūkai returned to Japan in 806 with an unprecedented collection of esoteric texts, mandalas, and ritual objects, and gained the favor of Emperor Saga (r. 809–823) and Emperor Junna (r. 823–833).

Shingon introduced to Japan the tantric Buddhist system of *mikkyō* (密教, "secret teachings"): a complex network of ritual, *mantra* (sacred syllables), *mudrā* (symbolic hand gestures), and visualization practices centered on the cosmic Buddha **Mahāvairocana** (*Dainichi Nyorai*), whose body encompasses the entire universe. Kūkai's masterly systematization of this esoteric tradition — articulating the two-realm mandala (*ryōkai mandara*) of the Diamond World (*Kongōkai*) and Womb World (*Taizōkai*) — formed the philosophical core of Shingon practice.

Kūkai established his headquarters on **Mount Kōya** (*Kōyasan*) in Wakayama Prefecture in 816, which became one of Japan's most sacred monastic centers and a major pilgrimage destination with over 100 temples. According to Shingon tradition, Kūkai did not die but entered a state of perpetual meditation (*nyūjō*) in 835 and remains in eternal contemplation on Kōyasan. His *Okunoin* mausoleum — surrounded by over 200,000 lanterns burning continuously — is visited by tens of thousands of pilgrims annually.

Kūkai also exerted profound influence on Japanese secular culture. He is credited (whether accurately or not by later tradition) with inventing or popularizing the *hiragana* syllabary, establishing a public school (*Shugei Shuchi-in*) for commoners, and contributing to poetry, calligraphy, and landscape engineering. His *Jūjūshinron* ("Ten Stages of Mind," 830) is a comprehensive survey of Buddhist and non-Buddhist philosophical systems.

Shingon introduced fire ceremonies (*goma*), detailed esoteric initiations, and elaborate altar rituals (*kaji*, "empowerment") that became central to Japanese court and noble religion. The school maintained close ties to the imperial family, performing protective rituals for the state.

### Pure Land Buddhism (浄土) — The Path of Devotion

As Japan entered the latter Heian period (11th–12th centuries), a growing sense of religious crisis coincided with the Buddhist teaching of *mappō* (末法, "the Latter Days of the Dharma"). According to this doctrine, the Buddha's teaching passes through three ages of decreasing spiritual capacity: the **True Dharma** age (500 years), the **Imitative Dharma** age (1,000 years), and the **Latter Dharma** age (10,000 years of degeneration). Japanese calculations placed the beginning of *mappō* in 1052. This eschatological anxiety made the Pure Land tradition — which promised salvation through faith in *Amitābha Buddha* (Amida in Japanese) — deeply resonant.

**Hōnen** (1133–1212) studied at Hieizan for years before arriving at the conviction that *nenbutsu* recitation — invoking the name "Namu Amida Butsu" (南無阿弥陀仏, "homage to Amida Buddha") — was the single practice capable of ensuring rebirth in Amida's **Pure Land** (*Jōdo*, 浄土; Sanskrit: *Sukhāvatī*), from which enlightenment was certain. Hōnen articulated this in his 1198 treatise *Senchaku Hongan Nembutsu Shū* ("Collection of Passages on the Original Vow of Nembutsu Selected in the Primal Vow"). His teaching was radical: it democratized salvation, making the path to liberation available to women, the illiterate, the poor, and the morally impure. Hōnen was twice exiled (once in 1207, when several disciples were executed for allegedly converting women at court), and died in exile at age 78.

**Shinran** (1173–1263), Hōnen's most important disciple, radicalized the doctrine further. In his masterwork *Kyōgyōshinshō* ("Teaching, Practice, Faith, and Realization"), Shinran argued that *tariki* (他力, "other-power") — reliance on Amida's saving grace — was the only valid spiritual path. He taught that even a single sincere *nenbutsu* was sufficient, and that spiritual "self-power" (*jiriki*) was itself an obstacle to salvation, since it implied doubt in Amida's universal compassion. Shinran also rejected clerical celibacy, married the noblewoman Eshinni, and had six children, establishing that the lay life was as spiritually valid as the monastic. His lineage became the **Jōdo Shinshū** (True Pure Land) school, organized by his great-grandson Kakunyo (1270–1351), which eventually became Japan's largest Buddhist denomination.

A third important Pure Land figure was **Ippen** (1239–1289), founder of the **Ji school** (*Jishū*). Ippen developed *odori-nenbutsu* ("dancing nembutsu"), a distinctive ecstatic practice in which believers danced and chanted simultaneously. A charismatic wandering mendicant, Ippen distributed *fuda* (amulets) inscribed with "Namu Amida Butsu" and reportedly gave amulets to all comers — even (controversially) those who declined faith. His life and journeys were memorably depicted in the 13th-century *Ippen Shōnin Eden* picture scroll.

## Zen Buddhism (禅宗) — The Way of Direct Insight

**Zen** (*chan* in Chinese, *dhyāna* in Sanskrit, meaning "meditation") developed in China and emphasizes direct, non-conceptual realization of Buddha-nature through seated meditation (*zazen*) rather than textual study or ritual. Zen arrived in Japan during the Kamakura period (1185–1333) and became the favored school of the warrior class.

### Rinzai (臨済宗)

**Eisai** (1141–1215, also romanized Yōsai) made two journeys to China, the second returning in 1191 with the **Rinzai** (*Linji*) lineage. Rinzai's core pedagogical tool is the *kōan* (公案): a paradoxical question or anecdote — "What was your face before your parents were born?" "What is the sound of one hand?" — posed by a master to jolt students out of conceptual thinking and into direct experiential insight (*kenshō* or *satori*). Rinzai practice typically combines intensive *kōan* work during *sesshin* (meditation retreats) with *sanzen* (private teacher-student dialogues).

Eisai's 1198 work *Kōzen Gokokuron* ("Propagation of Zen for the Protection of the Country") argued that Zen would strengthen Japan spiritually and militarily. He gained the patronage of the Kamakura Shogunate and established Jufuku-ji in Kamakura (1200) and Kennin-ji in Kyoto (1202), Japan's oldest Zen monastery. Rinzai gained enormous cultural influence through the Gozan ("Five Mountains") system, in which the Kamakura and Muromachi shogunates designated and ranked the five leading Zen monasteries in Kamakura and Kyoto, positioning Zen as the cultural and intellectual center of the warrior aristocracy.

### Sōtō (曹洞宗)

**Dōgen** (1200–1253), widely considered Japan's most original Buddhist philosopher, traveled to China in 1223 and studied under the Caodong (*Sōtō*) master Tiantong Rujing, under whom he achieved what he called "dropping off body and mind" (*shinjin datsuraku*). He returned to Japan in 1227 and eventually settled at **Eihei-ji** temple in Echizen Province (present Fukui Prefecture), founded in 1244.

Dōgen's philosophical vision, articulated in the 95-fascicle *Shōbōgenzō* ("Treasury of the True Dharma Eye"), diverges sharply from Rinzai. Dōgen taught *shikantaza* (只管打坐, "just sitting"): seated meditation not as a *means* to enlightenment but as the direct *expression* of enlightenment itself. His concept of *dōtoku* (道得, "expressing the Way") and his radical rethinking of Buddhist temporality (*uji*, "being-time") have made the *Shōbōgenzō* a central text in comparative philosophy and phenomenology. Dōgen rejected the notion of a separate *kenshō* experience, maintaining that practice and realization were one (*shushō ittō*).

Sōtō developed a strong organizational structure centered on Eihei-ji and the later Sōji-ji (originally in Noto), eventually establishing a vast network of village temples with widespread rural following. Today Sōtō is Japan's largest Zen school by temple count, with approximately 14,000 temples.

### Ōbaku (黄檗宗)

A third Zen school, **Ōbaku** (黄檗, after the Chinese mountain Huangbo), arrived in Japan in 1654 through the Chinese immigrant monk **Yinyuan Longqi** (Ingen Ryūki in Japanese, 1592–1673), who settled at Manpuku-ji temple in Uji, near Kyoto. Ōbaku blended Rinzai-style *kōan* practice with Pure Land *nembutsu* recitation and distinctive Ming-dynasty Chinese architectural and ceremonial styles. Its Chinese cultural character gave it a peculiar status — it remained the smallest of the three Zen schools but exercised disproportionate cultural influence through its printing, calligraphy, and culinary traditions (Ōbaku monks introduced *shōjin ryōri* vegetarian cuisine styles).

### Zen's Cultural Legacy

Zen's aesthetic influence on Japanese culture was immense and distinctive. The tea ceremony (*chanoyu*) as codified by Sen no Rikyū was deeply shaped by Zen principles of simplicity, directness, and *wabi* (rustic imperfection). The dry landscape garden (*karesansui*) — epitomized by Ryōan-ji's 15-stone composition in Kyoto — translates Zen spatial principles into stone and raked gravel. Ink wash painting (*suiboku-ga*) in the Muromachi period, as practiced by Sesshū Tōyō (1420–1506), expressed Zen's preference for emptiness over elaboration. Ikebana (flower arrangement), kyūdō (archery), and kendo all formalized Zen-influenced concepts of mindful presence.

The *dōjō* (training hall) concept — where intensive practice under a qualified teacher brings direct embodied knowledge — is a Zen contribution to Japanese cultural transmission that extends far beyond formal Buddhist practice.

## Nichiren Buddhism (日蓮宗)

**Nichiren** (1222–1282) was a monk from a fisherman's family in Awa Province (present-day Chiba) who, after studying at Hieizan and multiple other centers, became convinced that the *Lotus Sutra* (*Hokke-kyō*) alone was the complete and perfect teaching for the degenerate age of *mappō*. On April 28, 1253, at Seichō-ji in Awa Province, he uttered for the first time the *daimoku* — "Namu-myōhō-renge-kyō" (南無妙法蓮華経, "Homage to the Lotus Sutra of the Wondrous Dharma") — proclaiming it the sole practice for salvation.

Nichiren's polemical tract *Risshō Ankoku Ron* ("On Establishing the Correct Teaching for the Peace of the Land," 1260) argued that the Kamakura shogunate's support for Pure Land Buddhism had brought calamities on Japan (he specifically predicted the Mongol invasions of 1274 and 1281 years in advance). He presented this to the shogunate, earning their enmity. Nichiren suffered:
- **Izu exile** (1261–1263): banished to the Izu Peninsula after the Shogunate found his polemics intolerable.
- **Tatsunokuchi near-execution** (September 12, 1271): arrested and brought to the execution grounds at Tatsunokuchi in Sagami Province; according to Nichiren's own account, a brilliant light appeared and stayed the executioner's hand.
- **Sado exile** (1271–1274): banished to the remote island of Sado in the Sea of Japan, where he wrote the *Kaimokushō* ("Opening of the Eyes") and *Kanjin no Honzon-shō* ("The Object of Devotion for Observing the Mind"), two of his most important doctrinal works.

Despite — or because of — this persecution, Nichiren attracted a devoted following among the *samurai*, merchants, and common people of eastern Japan. He never occupied a position of state patronage and was sharply critical of all other Buddhist schools. After his pardoned return from Sado, Nichiren retired to Minobu in Kai Province (present Yamanashi) in 1274; he died in 1282 at Ikegami (in present-day Tokyo), where he had stopped while traveling for medical treatment.

Nichiren Buddhism subsequently split into dozens of branches. Notable modern movements include:
- **Honmon Butsuryu-shū** (founded 1857 by Nagamatsu Seifu): emphasizes the original teaching (*honmon*) in the latter half of the Lotus Sutra.
- **Sōka Gakkai** (Value-Creating Society): founded 1930 by educator Makiguchi Tsunesaburō; became a mass lay movement especially in postwar Japan, claiming 8 million member families. Its political arm, the **Kōmeitō** party, has been a major force in Japanese coalition politics since the 1970s.
- **Reiyūkai** and **Risshō Kōseikai**: large lay organizations emphasizing ancestor veneration and the Lotus Sutra.

## The Edo Period and Buddhist Consolidation (1603–1867)

During the Edo period, the Tokugawa shogunate, seeking social stability and the suppression of Christianity (which had spread in Kyushu during the late 16th century and was associated with European political intrusion), implemented the **danka seido** (檀家制度, "parishioner-temple system"). Under the 1638 decree that followed the Shimabara Rebellion, every household was required to register as a parishioner (*danka*) of a Buddhist temple. This system tied Buddhist institutions to the state apparatus of population control, making temples responsible for certifying the religious status of households and recording births, deaths, and marriages in *kakochō* (death registers).

The consequences were mixed. Buddhism gained institutional stability, a reliable income from death rites and ancestor memorials, and a captive membership — but also became associated with bureaucracy and "funeral Buddhism" (*sōshiki Bukkyō*), a phrase still sometimes used critically. Sectarian conflict declined as the state suppressed it, but so did theological innovation.

Regional patterns solidified: Pure Land schools dominated in rural areas and among merchant families, Zen among samurai and their descendants, Tendai and Shingon in their mountain strongholds and among court aristocracy. Buddhist scholarship flourished — commentarial literature expanded enormously, and *terakoya* (temple schools) became primary vehicles for popular literacy. The Ōbaku school, arriving in 1654, introduced new energy through Chinese cultural imports including block printing technology and new architectural styles that influenced later Japanese Buddhism.

## The Meiji Restoration and Shinbutsu Bunri (1868)

The **Meiji Restoration** (1868) brought one of Japanese Buddhism's greatest crises. The new Meiji government, seeking to establish State Shinto as the ideological basis of imperial sovereignty, issued a series of **shinbutsu-bunri** (神仏分離, "separation of Buddhism and Shinto") edicts beginning March 17, 1868, mandating that Buddhist elements be removed from Shinto shrines and that Shinto shrine-priests (*kannagi*) be separated from Buddhist monks who had co-administered many shrine complexes.

The immediate result was a wave of anti-Buddhist violence — **haibutsu kishaku** (廃仏毀釈, "abolish Buddhism, destroy Śākyamuni") — in which local government officials and mobs destroyed thousands of Buddhist statues, temples, and texts, particularly in domains where local lords were enthusiastic supporters of State Shinto (notably Satsuma, Mito, and Tsuwano). Some estimates suggest 3,000 to 10,000 Buddhist institutions were destroyed or closed. The government forced the disestablishment of the *danka* system and severed Buddhism's official state role.

Buddhism recovered its social position through decades of reform and adaptation. Buddhist modernists like Inoue Enryō (1858–1919) and the Shin Buddhist reformer Kiyozawa Manshi (1863–1903) worked to justify Buddhism philosophically and socially, framing it as compatible with Western science and national values. New sectarian organizations consolidated governance, established universities (Ryukoku University, Ōtani University, Kōyasan University, and others were founded in the Meiji era), and sent missionaries abroad.

## Modern Era and Contemporary Practice

Today, Buddhism remains embedded in Japanese life, though primarily through mortuary and memorial functions rather than active doctrinal membership. The major school groupings and approximate adherent claims (noting that counting methods vary widely and many Japanese affiliate with multiple traditions) are:

| School | Main Center | Approx. Members |
|---|---|---|
| Jōdo Shinshū (Higashi-Honganji) | Kyoto | ~3 million households |
| Jōdo Shinshū (Nishi-Honganji) | Kyoto | ~3 million households |
| Jōdo-shū (Hōnen's school) | Kyoto (Chion-in) | ~6 million |
| Sōtō Zen | Eihei-ji, Sōji-ji | ~6.5 million |
| Rinzai Zen (multiple branches) | Kyoto (multiple) | ~3.5 million |
| Tendai | Mount Hiei (Enryaku-ji) | ~2.5 million |
| Shingon (Tōji-ha, Kōyasan, etc.) | Kōyasan, Kyoto | ~1.5 million |
| Nichiren-shū | Kuon-ji, Minobu | ~3.5 million |
| Sōka Gakkai | Taiseki-ji, Shizuoka | ~8 million families |

Many Japanese engage in Buddhist practices — *obon* ancestor festivals, *higan* equinox memorial services, *sotoba* grave-post offerings, and funeral rites — without identifying as exclusively "Buddhist," reflecting the enduring *shinbutsu-shūgō* tradition. A majority of Japanese funerals are conducted by Buddhist priests, making the mortuary function still central even in a largely secular society.

Buddhist temples (*tera*) remain vital community institutions. Major heritage sites — Tōdai-ji, Hōryū-ji, Kiyomizu-dera, Eiheiji, Enryaku-ji, Kōyasan — attract millions of visitors annually and function simultaneously as active monasteries, tourist destinations, and living spiritual communities.
