# Shinto Religion

**Category**: Culture & Society  
**Key figures**: No single founder (traditional); Emperor Meiji (State Shinto era); Motoori Norinaga (1730–1801, scholar of Shinto revival); Hirata Atsutane (1776–1843, nativist theologian)

## Summary

Shinto (神道, *Shintō*, "way of the kami") is the indigenous spiritual tradition of Japan, rooted in prehistoric animistic practices and formalized as a distinct body of belief and ritual beginning in the 8th century CE. The term was coined to distinguish native Japanese religious practice from the Buddhism that arrived from the Asian continent via Korea in the mid-6th century (traditionally dated 552 CE). Shinto is not a dogmatic religion with a single founder, sacred scripture, or rigid creed; rather, it is a constellation of rituals, festivals, myths, and ethical values organized around the veneration of *kami*—a vast category of sacred powers inhabiting natural phenomena, exceptional places, ancestors, and deified historical figures.

Japan's approximately **80,000 shrines** (*jinja*) form the primary institutional backbone of Shinto, each typically serving as the home of one or more kami and the center of local community ritual life. About **100,000 priests** (*kannushi*) and shrine maidens (*miko*) maintain these sites. Shinto's defining qualities include an emphasis on ritual purity (*harae*), harmony with nature, communal celebration of seasonal cycles, and the maintenance of a connection between human communities and the sacred forces underlying the world.

## Origins and Sacred Texts

Shinto's mythological framework is preserved in the **Kojiki** (古事記, "Record of Ancient Matters"), completed in **712 CE**, and the **Nihon Shoki** (日本書紀, "Chronicles of Japan"), completed in **720 CE**. Both were commissioned by the imperial court to legitimize the ruling dynasty by tracing its ancestry to the sun goddess **Amaterasu Ōmikami**, from whom the imperial line was said to descend. These texts narrate the creation of the Japanese islands by the primordial couple Izanagi and Izanami, the birth of Amaterasu, her brother Susanoo's storms and misdeeds, the hiding of Amaterasu in a cave (causing darkness to fall on the world), and her eventual emergence through a divine performance that restored light—a myth re-enacted in many matsuri.

The three **Imperial Treasures** (*Sanshu no Jingi*)—the mirror Yata no Kagami (symbol of wisdom), the sword Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi (valor), and the jewel Yasakani no Magatama (benevolence)—are at once Shinto sacred objects and symbols of imperial legitimacy. Their custody is maintained at the Grand Shrine of Ise, the Atsuta Shrine (Nagoya), and the Imperial Palace, respectively, though only priests and emperors may view them.

## Kami and Cosmology

The word *kami* defies a simple translation; "deity" or "spirit" are approximations. Kami may reside in mountains (e.g., Mount Fuji is itself *Fujisan Hongu Sengen Taisha*'s kami), rivers, trees, boulders, ancestors, notable humans, and abstract forces. The Kojiki enumerates specific kami numbering in the thousands, while popular tradition speaks of *yaoyorozu no kami* ("eight million kami")—not literally 8,000,000 but an expression of their countless multitude.

Key kami include:
- **Amaterasu Ōmikami** (sun goddess): supreme kami of the Ise Grand Shrine; ancestor of the imperial family
- **Inari Ōkami** (kami of rice, agriculture, foxes, industry, and worldly success): most widely enshrined kami, with over 30,000 subsidiary shrines
- **Hachiman** (kami of archery and war): patron of the samurai class, enshrined at Usa Shrine (Oita) and Tsurugaoka Hachimangū (Kamakura)
- **Susanoo** (storm kami, brother of Amaterasu): enshrined at Yasaka Shrine (Kyoto)
- **Ōkuninushi** (kami of nation-building and medicine): enshrined at Izumo Taisha

## Major Shrines

Japan's shrine landscape ranges from neighborhood *hokora* (small roadside shrines) to grand complexes that draw millions of visitors annually:

**Ise Jingū** (Mie Prefecture): The most sacred Shinto site, comprising two main shrines—the Inner Shrine (*Naikū*) dedicated to Amaterasu and the Outer Shrine (*Gekū*) to Toyouke (kami of food). The complex is remarkable for the practice of **shikinen sengū**—the complete rebuilding of the shrine structures on adjacent plots every **20 years**, a cycle observed continuously since approximately 690 CE (with occasional interruptions). The most recent rebuilding occurred in **October 2013**, the 62nd such renewal. The forested precinct is off-limits to all but imperial family members and priests; the public venerates from outside the inner gates. Annual visitors exceed 8 million.

**Fushimi Inari-taisha** (Kyoto): Dedicated to Inari, the complex is famous for approximately **10,000 vermilion *torii* gates** (*senbon torii*) donated by businesses and individuals lining paths up the forested Mount Inari (233 m). Founded in 711 CE. The most-visited shrine in Japan, with approximately 2.7 million visitors during New Year's alone.

**Izumo Taisha** (Shimane Prefecture): One of the oldest and most revered shrines in Japan, enshrining Ōkuninushi. The main hall (*honden*), in the *taisha-zukuri* architectural style, is 24 m tall. According to tradition, all kami from across Japan gather here in the tenth lunar month (called *Kannazuki*, "month without gods," everywhere except Izumo where it is *Kamiari-zuki*, "month with gods").

**Meiji Jingū** (Tokyo): Completed in **November 1920**, enshrining the deified Emperor Meiji and Empress Shōken. Located in a forested enclosure of approximately 70 hectares in central Tokyo (the forests were artificially planted after the shrine's construction, now reaching mature canopy). It is Japan's most visited shrine during New Year, typically attracting **3 million+ visitors in the first three days** of January.

**Tsurugaoka Hachimangū** (Kamakura): Founded 1063, expanded 1180 by Minamoto no Yoritomo (founder of the Kamakura shogunate); the spiritual center of the first warrior government.

## Ritual Practices

Core Shinto ritual centers on **harae** (purification), **offerings** (*nusa* or *shinsen*), and **prayer** (*norito*). Approaching a shrine, worshippers first rinse hands and mouth at the *temizuya* (water basin). At the main hall, the standard approach is: bow twice, clap twice, pray, bow once (*nirei-nihakushu-ichirei*). Offerings of rice, salt, water, sake, and seasonal foods are presented by priests, with the act of offering creating a relational bond with the kami.

**Matsuri** (festivals) are the most communal expression of Shinto. Thousands of matsuri take place throughout the year across Japan, typically involving a procession of the kami's portable shrine (*mikoshi*) through the community, performances, and shared meals. Major national matsuri include:
- **Aoi Matsuri** (Kyoto, May 15): Dating to the 6th century, one of Kyoto's three great festivals
- **Gion Matsuri** (Kyoto, July): Month-long festival of Yasaka Shrine; the *Yamaboko Junko* float parade on July 17 and July 24 is among Japan's most spectacular public events
- **Awa Odori** (Tokushima, Shikoku, August): A dance festival of Shinto origins drawing over a million spectators

**Life-cycle rituals** at shrines include:
- **Miyamairi** (first shrine visit for a newborn, typically 30–100 days after birth)
- **Shichigosan** (November 15): Children aged 3, 5, and 7 are dressed in formal attire and brought to shrines
- **Seijin-shiki** (Coming of Age Day): Now a civic holiday (second Monday of January), still often marked with shrine visits
- **Hatsumode** (New Year's shrine visit): Approximately **100 million people**—over three-quarters of Japan's population—visit shrines in the first three days of January, making it the single largest annual movement of people in Japan

## Shinto and Buddhism: Coexistence and Separation

From the Nara period (710–794 CE) onward, Shinto and Buddhism coexisted through a syncretic system known as **shinbutsu-shūgō** (combination of kami and buddhas), in which kami were interpreted as manifestations of Buddhist deities (*honji-suijaku* theory: Buddhist deities are the "original ground," kami their "traces"). Buddhist temples were built within shrine precincts; priests might perform both Shinto and Buddhist rituals; the Ise Shrines had Buddhist temples adjacent to them for centuries.

This syncretism was violently disrupted by the **shinbutsu bunri** (separation of Shinto and Buddhism) decree issued by the new Meiji government in **January 1868**, which ordered the physical separation of Buddhist elements from Shinto sites. The subsequent **haibutsu kishaku** ("abolish Buddhism, destroy Shakyamuni") wave—not ordered by the government but unleashed by nativist sentiment—destroyed thousands of Buddhist artworks, temple buildings, and texts and forced some 17,000 Buddhist temples to close. The damage was concentrated in domains under nativist influence; many shrines and temples elsewhere survived intact.

Today roughly 70% of Japanese people identify with Buddhist customs, approximately 70% with Shinto customs, and only ~3–4% identify as exclusively Shinto. This apparent paradox—totals far exceeding 100%—reflects the traditional Japanese practice of observing both traditions for different life events: Shinto for births and weddings, Buddhism for funerals and ancestral commemoration.

## State Shinto (1868–1945)

The [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md) fundamentally transformed Shinto's political status. The new government elevated Shinto as the ideological foundation of imperial authority, issuing the **Grand Council of State Decree No. 1 (Seitaisho)** in 1868, which established a *Department of Divinities* (*Jingikan*) ranking above the main government ministries. Imperial mythology—particularly the doctrine that the Emperor descended from Amaterasu and was a living *kami*—was instilled through the education system (especially the Imperial Rescript on Education of 1890) and military training.

**State Shinto** (*Kokka Shintō*) was formalized as a civic rather than religious institution—the government claimed shrines were not "religion" but expressions of national identity and loyalty—enabling mandatory participation without violating nominal freedom of religion. The Yasukuni Shrine (Tokyo, founded 1869) became the central memorial for Japan's war dead, enshrining the spirits of those killed in service of the Emperor; the practice of official visits by prime ministers to Yasukuni remains diplomatically contentious with China and South Korea.

State Shinto was formally abolished by the **Shinto Directive** issued by the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers (SCAP) on **December 15, 1945**, severing the connection between the state and Shinto institutions, prohibiting the use of public funds for shrines, and requiring Emperor Hirohito to issue the *Ningen-sengen* (Declaration of Humanity) on January 1, 1946, renouncing claims to divinity.

## Contemporary Shinto

After 1945, Shinto reorganized as a voluntary religious institution. The **Jinja Honchō** (Association of Shinto Shrines), established in 1946, serves as an umbrella organization for approximately 80,000 shrines. Public opinion polls consistently show that many Japanese observe Shinto rituals without identifying as religious believers—reflecting the embedded, habitual character of Shinto practice in Japanese life rather than a propositional faith commitment.

New Shinto-derived religious movements (*shinshūkyō*) emerged in the 19th and 20th centuries, including Tenrikyō (founded 1838), Konkōkyō (1859), and Ōmoto (1892), some of which the government suppressed as threats to State Shinto orthodoxy and which became independent entities after 1945.

## Significance

Shinto is inseparable from the geography, aesthetics, and social life of Japan. Its influence pervades Japanese art (the torii gate as a universal Japanese symbol), architecture (shrine *nagare-zukuri* and *taisha-zukuri* styles), landscape design (the forested shrine precinct as model for the Japanese garden), and environmental ethics (satoyama landscape management rooted in the idea that nature is inhabited by kami). The [major islands](../geography/major-islands.md) themselves—particularly their mountains, forests, and coastlines—are Shinto sacred geography, with Mount Fuji, Ōyama, Nachi Falls, and hundreds of other natural sites enshrined as kami.

The religion's political history—from syncretic medieval coexistence with Buddhism, through Meiji-era weaponization as State Shinto, to postwar voluntary revival—mirrors Japan's broader confrontation with modernity, nationalism, and identity across two centuries.
