# Noh Theater

**Category**: Culture & Society  
**Key figures**: Kan'ami Kiyotsugu (1333–1384), Zeami Motokiyo (1363–1443), Ashikaga Yoshimitsu (1358–1408), Komparu Zenchiku (1405–c. 1471), Kongō Nagatoshi

## Summary

Noh (*能*) is Japan's oldest continuously performed theatrical tradition, emerging in the 14th century from the convergence of popular *sarugaku* (猿楽, "monkey music") entertainment and the refined aesthetics of Zen Buddhism. Pioneered by the actor-playwright **Kan'ami Kiyotsugu** and brought to its classical form by his son **Zeami Motokiyo** under the patronage of Ashikaga Shogun Yoshimitsu, Noh crystallized into a spare, symbolic drama of masked performers, poetic language, and deeply restrained movement. Designated a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2008, it remains an active performing art in contemporary Japan, with hundreds of plays in active repertoire and major performance venues in Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, and Nara.

A typical Noh performance features a single masked protagonist (*shite*, 仕手), a supporting actor who grounds the drama in the present (*waki*, 脇), a child actor (*kokata*), and comedic interlude performers (*kyōgen* actors). The ensemble of musicians (*hayashi*) consists of one flute player (*fue*) and three percussionists playing shoulder drum (*ōtsuzumi*), hip drum (*kotsuzumi*), and stick drum (*taiko*). A six-to-eight member chorus (*ji*) seated at stage left chants in unison, narrating and voicing the protagonist's interior states. All roles were historically performed by men; women's roles are portrayed through masks.

## Origins and Development

### Pre-Noh Traditions: Sarugaku and Dengaku

Noh emerged from two older performance traditions. *Dengaku* (田楽, "field music") was a ritual entertainment tied to rice-cultivation festivals, combining acrobatics, music, and dance; it flourished in the late Heian and Kamakura periods and enjoyed aristocratic patronage in the 12th–13th centuries. *Sarugaku* (猿楽) was a broader category of variety entertainment—juggling, acrobatics, comic sketches, and mimicry—performed by itinerant troupes at temple and shrine festivals throughout Japan.

By the late 14th century, sarugaku troupes in the Yamato region (modern Nara Prefecture) had organized into competing school-companies (*za*) affiliated with major temples and shrines: the Yūzaki-za (later Kanze-za), the Tobi-za (later Hōshō), the Sakato-za (later Komparu), and the Emman-i-za (later Kongō). These four *yamato sarugaku* schools form the origin of the five great Noh schools that continue today.

### Kan'ami and the Breakthrough (1374)

The decisive moment in Noh's formation came in the summer of 1374. **Kan'ami Kiyotsugu** (観阿弥清次, 1333–1384), leader of the Yūzaki-za and an actor of extraordinary range, performed at the Imagumano Shrine in Kyoto before a young Ashikaga Yoshimitsu (r. 1368–1394 as shogun). Yoshimitsu—then 16 years old—was captivated and became Kan'ami's patron, extending court-level prestige and economic support to what had been a popular entertainment form.

Kan'ami's contribution was the synthesis of *sarugaku*'s dramatic energy with *kusemai* (曲舞), a lyrical song-and-dance form derived from Buddhist recitative. He integrated *kusemai*'s rhythmic, melodic chant into the dramatic structure of *sarugaku* plays, producing a musically sophisticated drama with literary poetic texts drawn from the *Man'yōshū*, the *Kokinshū*, and classical Chinese poetry. Surviving plays attributed to Kan'ami include *Matsukaze* ("Wind in the Pines"), *Sotoba Komachi*, and *Kayoi Komachi*, all centering on tragic female protagonists.

### Zeami's Codification (1374–1443)

When Kan'ami died in 1384, his son **Zeami Motokiyo** (世阿弥元清, 1363–1443) inherited the Kanze school and the shogunal patronage. Zeami's relationship with Yoshimitsu—which began when Zeami was approximately eleven years old—was close, personal, and transformative: under Yoshimitsu's aegis, Zeami composed the majority of the Noh canon's most celebrated plays, including *Takasago*, *Atsumori*, *Izutsu*, *Hagoromo*, and *Kinuta*.

More critically, Zeami documented Noh's theory in a series of treatises that constitute the first systematic aesthetics of performance in the world:

- ***Fūshi Kaden*** (風姿花伝, "The Transmission of the Flower through the Style of Performance," c. 1400–1418) — Zeami's most comprehensive treatise, covering actor training from age seven through old age, the qualities of *yugen* (profound grace) and *monomane* (imitation of things), and the principle of *hana* (花, "flower"), the elusive quality that captivates audiences.
- ***Kakyō*** (花鏡, "A Mirror Held to the Flower," 1424) — advanced treatise on actor consciousness and the principle of *riken no ken* (離見の見, "the seeing of the far-sight"), the ability to perceive one's own performance as an audience member would.
- ***Sarugaku Dangi*** (申楽談儀, "Conversations on Sarugaku," c. 1430) — recorded conversations between Zeami and his son-in-law Komparu Zenchiku, preserving detailed accounts of early Noh practice and Kan'ami's techniques.

Zeami defined *yugen* (幽玄, "profound grace") as Noh's supreme aesthetic quality—a quality of mystery, depth, and spiritual resonance that resists literal description. *Yugen* was to pervade every element of performance: the slow glide of the *shite*'s entrance, the tonal quality of the chant, the weight of the mask. This concept linked Noh aesthetics to Zen Buddhist principles of *mushin* (no-mind), *ma* (間, negative space), and the *wabi*-inflected beauty of restraint also central to the contemporaneous [tea ceremony](../culture-society/tea-ceremony.md) tradition.

### Later Schools and Fortunes

Zeami's final years were unhappy: Shogun Yoshinori (r. 1428–1441), who favored rival performers—particularly the *dengaku* actor Zoami—stripped Zeami of official recognition and in 1434 exiled him to the island of Sado, where he composed late treatises including *Kinōsaku* and *Yōkyoku Sanbyakuban Mokuroku*. He was eventually allowed to return to the capital, dying around 1443.

His son-in-law **Komparu Zenchiku** (金春禅竹, 1405–c. 1471) continued Noh's theoretical development, composing treatises that deepened the Zen philosophical dimensions of Noh aesthetics—particularly the concept of *mu* (emptiness) as the ground of dramatic power. A fifth school, the **Kita**, was founded in 1618 under the patronage of the Tokugawa shogunate.

## The Noh Stage and Performance Elements

### The Stage

The classic Noh stage (*noh butai*) is a raised cypress-wood platform roughly six meters on each side (three *ken* square), originally outdoors and now typically within a covered hall. The stage's three most distinctive features are:

1. **The back wall** (*kagami-ita*, mirror board), painted with a single aged pine tree—an image traceable to the sacred pine of the Kasuga Shrine in Nara, traditional patron of the Kanze school.
2. **The bridgeway** (*hashigakari*, 橋掛り), a long narrow walkway extending diagonally from stage left along which the shite makes its entrance and exit—often the most dramatic passage of a performance.
3. **Four pillars** (*hashira*) that frame the playing space; each has a specific relationship to particular actors' positions and is named: the *shite* pillar (downstage left), *waki* pillar (downstage right), *fue* pillar (upstage right), and *metsuke* pillar (upstage left, "eye-fixing post" used by masked actors for spatial orientation).

Pine trees are also planted at intervals along the hashigakari and traditionally across the stage garden. The total lack of scenery—save these natural images—places the imaginative burden on performer and audience together.

### Masks

Noh masks (*omote* or *men*, 面) are carved wooden objects of extraordinary psychological refinement, representing the play's protagonist identity—god, demon, young woman, old man, warrior's ghost, vengeful spirit. The five principal mask categories are:

- ***Ko-omote*** — young woman, idealized and serene
- ***Deigan*** / ***Shakumi*** — middle-aged or grieving woman
- ***Yase-otoko*** — emaciated male ghost or spirit
- ***Hannya*** — jealous or demonic female spirit, with horns and anguished mouth
- ***Okina*** — elderly god, used only in the sacred opening *Okina* ritual

The mask does not move, yet skilled performers animate it through subtle head angle changes: tilting downward (*kumorasu*, "to cloud") expresses grief; tilting upward (*terase*, "to shine") expresses joy or illumination. The audience's perception supplies the affect the static mask does not express—a principle of *yohaku no bi* (余白の美, "the beauty of negative space") central to Noh aesthetics.

### The Five-Play Cycle

A traditional full *one-day Noh* program (*goban-date*, 五番立) presents five plays in a fixed sequence, interspersed with short *kyōgen* comedies:

1. **God play** (*Waki-Noh*, 脇能) — opens with auspicious subject matter; the *shite* typically reveals itself as a Shinto deity (*kami*)
2. **Warrior play** (*Shura-Noh*, 修羅能) — protagonist is the ghost of a warrior, often from the Genpei War
3. **Woman play** (*Kazura-Noh*, 鬘能) — the most elegant category; protagonist is typically a beautiful woman, often a ghost
4. **Mad-person play** (*Yonbanme-Noh*, 四番目能) or Miscellaneous play — the most dramatic and psychologically complex
5. **Demon play** (*Kiri-Noh*, 切能) — supernatural or demonic protagonist; fastest, most kinetically intense

This structure moves from the divine to the human to the uncanny and back—a cosmological arc that mirrors Noh's underlying concern with the boundary between the living world and the realm of spirits, the *this-worldly* and the *other-worldly*.

## Noh and the Samurai Class

From the Muromachi period through the end of the Edo period (1336–1868), Noh functioned as the official entertainment and ceremonial music of the [samurai](../culture-society/samurai-bushido.md) class. Shogunal and daimyo patronage made Noh performance a state ritual: the three Tokugawa shogunal ceremonies (*sanba no shiki*) at which Noh was performed—New Year, the shogun's birthday, and official audience occasions—established Noh as a quasi-constitutional ceremonial form of the [Edo-period](../history/edo-period.md) military government.

Every daimyo maintained a Noh troupe; samurai of elite rank were expected to learn *utai* (chanting) and sometimes to perform. Tokugawa Ieyasu himself studied Noh and reportedly died chanting a Noh lyric. The association between Noh and samurai authority gave the art form an enormous security of patronage but also progressively insulated it from popular audiences—by the 18th century, commoner access to Noh was limited to occasional public performances (*rakan-geichi*) at temple gardens.

When the Meiji Restoration in 1868 abolished the feudal system and eliminated daimyo patronage, Noh faced existential crisis. Its survival was secured partly through the active support of the Meiji imperial household and partly through the organizational restructuring of the schools as professional family companies (*iemoto* systems) offering private instruction.

## The Five Schools

The five *sōke* (宗家, "head-family" schools) that transmit Noh today are:

| School | Founder / Origin | Associated Patron | Plays in Repertoire |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Kanze** (観世) | Kan'ami / Zeami | Ashikaga shoguns | c. 240 |
| **Hōshō** (宝生) | Hōshō Tobi | Kaga domain daimyo | c. 180 |
| **Komparu** (金春) | Zenchiku | Nara Kasuga Shrine | c. 170 |
| **Kongō** (金剛) | Kongō Nagatoshi | Kyoto nobility | c. 150 |
| **Kita** (喜多) | Kita Shichidayū (f. 1618) | Tokugawa bakufu | c. 120 |

Each school maintains its own versions of plays, its own mask traditions, its own authorized chant melodies (*yokyoku*), and its own training lineages. Plays common to multiple schools may differ significantly in musical interpretation and choreographic detail. The five schools together preserve approximately 240 distinct plays, of which roughly 100 see regular performance.

## Aesthetic Principles

### Ma and Yohaku no Bi

*Ma* (間) denotes the intervals—of silence, stillness, or blank space—that carry as much meaning as the sounds and movements around them. In Noh, a prolonged stillness before a line is chanted, or a long pause during which the *shite* stands unmoving in the bridgeway, is not absence but presence: the accumulation of tension, the audience's imagination filling the vacuum. *Yohaku no bi* (余白の美, "the beauty of blank space") extends this principle across all Noh's arts—the undecorated cypress stage, the single painted pine, the sparse instrumentation, the mask's unmoving face.

### Yugen

*Yugen* (幽玄) is the quality that Zeami placed at the apex of Noh aesthetics: a quality of mysterious profundity, ineffable grace, and underlying stillness that moves audiences without they being able to name what has moved them. Zeami described it through analogies—a white cloud over a distant mountain, a boat disappearing into morning mist—all images of partial visibility, of presence partially hidden. *Yugen* is not sadness, not beauty, not spirituality, but something that encompasses all three and transcends each.

### Monomane

*Monomane* (物真似, "imitation of things") was Kan'ami's foundational principle: the actor must deeply imitate not just the surface appearance but the inner essence of what is being portrayed—a woman's movement, an old man's bearing, the spiritual presence of a demon. Zeami refined this into a complex dialectic: pure imitation without *yugen* is mere mimicry; *yugen* without a grounded subject becomes affectation. The synthesis of *monomane* and *yugen* produces *hana*.

## Influence on World Theater

Noh became a significant influence on 20th-century Western theater through several channels. **W.B. Yeats** encountered Noh through Ernest Fenollosa's posthumous manuscripts (edited by Ezra Pound and published in 1916 as *Noh, or Accomplishment*) and wrote four plays explicitly modeled on Noh structure—*At the Hawk's Well* (1916), *The Only Jealousy of Emer* (1919), *The Dreaming of the Bones* (1919), and *Calvary* (1921)—using masks, stylized movement, and two-part structure. Bertolt Brecht's alienation techniques (*Verfremdungseffekt*) drew partial inspiration from Asian theatrical traditions including Noh. Samuel Beckett's *Waiting for Godot* (1953) has been analyzed in structural parallel to Noh, with its sustained stillness, its waiting protagonist, and its refusal of conventional dramatic action.

## Contemporary Practice

Noh is performed today in dedicated theaters (*nōgakudō*) throughout Japan, the most prominent of which is the Kokuritsu Nōgakudō (National Noh Theatre, Tokyo, opened 1983). The [Edo Culture & Arts](../culture-society/edo-culture-arts.md) encyclopedia article notes Noh's status as the entertainment and ceremonial music of the Tokugawa court. Major annual festivals include the outdoor *Takigi Nō* performances at Kasuga Shrine (Nara) and Heian Shrine ([Kyoto](../geography/kyoto.md)), performed by torchlight in open-air settings that recall the tradition's ancient outdoor origins.

The Noh community numbers approximately 1,500 professional performers in Japan today, organized within the five school systems. Transmission remains primarily through family lineages; the head of a school (*iemoto*) holds formal authority over the canon. The Kanze school is the largest, headquartered at the Kanze Nōgakudō in Shibuya, Tokyo. UNESCO recognized Noh (including its sister form of *kyōgen*) on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2008, building on an initial 2001 "Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity" proclamation.
