# Samurai & Bushido

**Category**: Culture & Society  
**Key figures**: Minamoto no Yoritomo (founder of the shogunate), Takeda Shingen, Tokugawa Ieyasu, Yamamoto Tsunetomo, Nitobe Inazō, Saigō Takamori

## Summary

Samurai were Japan's warrior class, a feudal aristocracy that dominated military, political, and cultural life from the 12th century until the Meiji Restoration in 1868. The term "samurai" (侍) originally referred to armed retainers of the court nobility; it derives from the verb *saburau* ("to serve"). Over time, samurai came to designate a distinct hereditary social stratum with formal legal status, land holdings, and political authority. Their rise followed the decline of the imperial court's military capacity and culminated in the establishment of the Kamakura shogunate (military government) in 1185–1192 under Minamoto no Yoritomo, who became the first shogun in 1192. From that point until 1868, successive shoguns governed Japan through a network of daimyo (feudal lords), each commanding samurai warriors bound by loyalty, duty, and honor.

Bushido (武士道), the "way of the warrior," was the ethical and behavioral code that defined samurai culture. Though the term was not standardized until the Edo period, its core principles had guided samurai conduct since the feudal era: loyalty to one's lord (*chū*), personal honor (*meiyo*), martial courage (*yūki*), righteousness (*gi*), benevolence (*jin*), truthfulness (*makoto*), and the acceptance of death. A samurai who faced defeat or dishonor might choose ritual suicide — seppuku (切腹, self-disembowelment) or *hara-kiri* (the colloquial term) — rather than surrender or disgrace. The willingness to face death without flinching distinguished samurai from mercenary warriors and embedded martial virtue within Japanese aesthetic and moral philosophy.

## Historical Development

### Emergence: Heian and Kamakura Periods (794–1333)

The samurai emerged as a warrior class during the late Heian period (794–1185), when the imperial court at Kyoto increasingly relied on regional military clans to suppress rebellions. The Taira and Minamoto clans became the two dominant military houses, fighting the Genpei War (1180–1185) for supremacy. Minamoto no Yoritomo's victory led to the establishment of the Kamakura shogunate in 1185, with formal appointment as shogun in 1192. Under the Kamakura system, samurai received land grants (*shōen*) in exchange for military service, establishing the feudal basis of samurai power.

### Sengoku Period (1467–1615): The Age of Warring States

The Sengoku (Warring States) period produced Japan's most celebrated samurai figures. Daimyo such as Takeda Shingen (1521–1573), Uesugi Kenshin (1530–1578), Oda Nobunaga (1534–1582), Toyotomi Hideyoshi (1537–1598), and Tokugawa Ieyasu (1543–1616) commanded armies of tens of thousands of samurai in near-continuous conflict. The introduction of firearms (arquebus) after 1543, brought by Portuguese traders, transformed battlefield tactics; Nobunaga's use of massed arquebusiers at the Battle of Nagashino in 1575 effectively ended the era of cavalry-dominated samurai warfare. Ieyasu's decisive victory at the Battle of Sekigahara in October 1600 established Tokugawa supremacy, confirmed in 1615 by the Siege of Osaka, after which the Tokugawa shogunate imposed peace over Japan.

### Edo Period (1603–1868): Samurai as Administrators

Under the Tokugawa shogunate, Japan entered over 250 years of largely unbroken peace (Pax Tokugawa). This peace transformed the samurai from active warriors into a hereditary bureaucratic and administrative class. By the 18th century, most samurai had never fought in battle; their martial skills were maintained through formalized practice rather than combat. The shogunate institutionalized a rigid four-class social hierarchy — samurai (士), farmers (農), artisans (工), merchants (商) — with samurai at the apex. Samurai were entitled to wear two swords (the *daishō*, a long *katana* and shorter *wakizashi*) and retained the legal right to cut down commoners who insulted them (*kirisute gomen*), though this right was rarely exercised.

Economically, Edo-period samurai were largely stipend-dependent, receiving rice allowances (*fuchi*) from their daimyo rather than generating income from land. This created a class simultaneously privileged in status and often precarious in finances, leading many samurai into debt to merchant houses, a social irony that generated considerable literary and philosophical commentary.

## Bushido: Codification and Literature

Bushido as a formal philosophy was codified in two landmark Edo-period works:

**Hagakure (葉隠, "Hidden in Leaves")**: Dictated between 1709 and 1716 by the retired samurai Yamamoto Tsunetomo (1659–1719) and recorded by his disciple Tashiro Tsuramoto, the *Hagakure* articulated a pure, uncompromising bushido. Its most famous line — "The way of the samurai is found in death" (*Bushido to wa shinu koto to mitsuketari*) — captures the text's emphasis on total psychological readiness to die in service to one's lord. The *Hagakure* was not widely circulated during the Edo period but became influential in the late 19th and 20th centuries.

**Bushido: The Soul of Japan (1900)**: Written in English by Nitobe Inazō (1862–1933), a Christian scholar and diplomat, and published in Philadelphia in 1900, this work introduced bushido to Western audiences and framed it as Japan's equivalent of European chivalry. Nitobe's interpretation emphasized honor, loyalty, and martial virtue as expressions of a distinctively Japanese moral character. The book was widely read internationally and shaped Western understanding of Japanese culture for generations. Emperor Meiji reportedly sent copies to foreign dignitaries.

Other significant texts include Miyamoto Musashi's *The Book of Five Rings* (*Go Rin No Sho*, written c. 1643), which addressed martial strategy and philosophy, and the *Buke Shohatto* (Laws for Military Houses), a series of edicts issued by the Tokugawa shogunate beginning in 1615 that formalized the behavioral expectations of samurai.

## Martial Arts and Cultural Disciplines

The Edo-period transformation of samurai culture produced a rich tradition of martial arts and aesthetic disciplines:

- **Kendo (剣道)**: Formalized sword practice using bamboo swords (*shinai*) and protective armor, tracing to 18th-century innovations in fencing schools.
- **Judo (柔道)**: Founded in 1882 by Kanō Jigorō (1860–1938), a synthesis of traditional *jujutsu* wrestling that emphasized maximum efficiency and mutual welfare; became an Olympic sport in 1964.
- **Kyudo (弓道)**: The way of the bow, a formalized archery discipline rooted in samurai training.
- **Iaido (居合道)**: The art of drawing and sheathing the sword, emphasizing mental focus and precision.
- **Tea Ceremony (Chado/Sado)**: Though originally a court practice, the tea ceremony was embraced by samurai as an expression of *wabi* (austere beauty), discipline, and mindfulness — Toyotomi Hideyoshi famously patronized tea master Sen no Rikyū (1522–1591) before ordering his ritual suicide in 1591.

Many samurai also practiced calligraphy, poetry, and Noh theater, reflecting the ideal of the *bunbu ryōdō* — "the dual way of civil and martial arts" — that governed elite samurai culture.

## Decline and Abolition

The collapse of the Tokugawa shogunate in 1867–1868, precipitated by Western pressure, internal economic decay, and the rise of the emperor-restoration movement (*sonnō jōi*), ended the shogunal system that had sustained the samurai class. The Meiji government issued a series of reforms that systematically dismantled samurai privilege: the *Haitorei* edict of 1876 prohibited samurai from wearing swords in public; the stipend system was abolished in 1876 via compulsory commutation into government bonds; and a modern conscript army replaced the samurai military order.

Former samurai initially constituted a disproportionate share of Meiji government officials, military officers, and intellectuals. The Satsuma Rebellion (February–September 1877) was the largest armed resistance by former samurai, led by Saigō Takamori (1828–1877), who had been a leading Meiji reformer before becoming disillusioned by the pace and nature of modernization. Some 40,000 rebel samurai were defeated by the new conscript army, and Saigō died at the Battle of Shiroyama in September 1877. His fate crystallized the pathos of the samurai's historical displacement; Saigō was posthumously rehabilitated as a hero and romanticized in culture, most recently in the 2003 film *The Last Samurai*.

## Significance and Modern Legacy

Samurai and bushido are foundational to Japanese cultural identity and global perceptions of Japan. The samurai archetype — the loyal, honorable warrior — has become iconic in global popular culture, from Akira Kurosawa's films (*Seven Samurai*, 1954; *Yojimbo*, 1961) to video games, anime, and manga. Within Japan, bushido represents a cultural continuity connecting medieval feudalism to modern values of duty, discipline, and aesthetic refinement.

The reinterpretation of bushido during the modern era, particularly during the militarist 1930s–1940s, had tragic consequences. State ideologues mobilized the code's emphasis on loyalty to the emperor (*chūkun aikoku*) and acceptance of death to justify imperial expansion, the practice of *kamikaze* (suicide air attacks), and the *Gyokusai* (death before surrender) policy that killed hundreds of thousands of Japanese soldiers and civilians in the Pacific War. The postwar reassessment of this militarized bushido remains a charged subject in Japanese historical memory.

Today, bushido survives in martial arts traditions (kendo, judo, and karate have tens of millions of practitioners worldwide), in Japanese corporate and organizational culture (where hierarchy and collective loyalty remain valued), and in contemporary cultural production. The dual legacy of samurai culture — aesthetic achievement and the dangers of martial romanticism — shapes how Japan and the world understand Japanese history and identity.
