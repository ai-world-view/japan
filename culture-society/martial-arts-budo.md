# Martial Arts & Budo

Martial arts (*budō*, 武道, literally "the way of the warrior") form a distinctive branch of Japanese cultural practice, blending combat techniques with Zen Buddhist philosophy, aesthetic discipline, and moral cultivation. While rooted in samurai military training that crystallized during the [Kamakura period](../history/kamakura-period.md), modern martial arts have become systems of personal development, competitive sports, and cultural exports that have shaped global athletics and self-improvement culture since the late 19th century. An estimated 50 million people worldwide practiced some form of Japanese martial art by 2020.

## Historical Foundations

Martial-arts techniques developed in Japan throughout the medieval and early modern periods as samurai refined methods of combat with swords (*katana*, *tachi*), spears (*yari*), bows (*yumi*), and unarmed fighting. The [samurai *bushido*](samurai-bushido.md) code incorporated martial training (*bugei*, 武芸) as both a practical military necessity and a path to moral and spiritual cultivation. The synthesis of Zen Buddhism with warrior culture during the [Kamakura period](../history/kamakura-period.md) — when Eisai (1141–1215) founded Rinzai Zen and Dōgen (1200–1253) founded Sōtō Zen — gave Japanese martial practice its distinctive philosophical depth.

The formalization of martial arts as systematic, pedagogically structured disciplines emerged during the [Edo period](../history/edo-period.md) (1603–1868), when prolonged peace reduced warfare and samurai sought to preserve and systematize their combat knowledge. By the mid-Edo period hundreds of distinct schools (*ryūha*) existed for swordsmanship alone; the government-licensed schools (*heiho*, "martial strategy") employed specialists who codified techniques in secret transmission documents (*densho*). The Edo peace paradoxically intensified martial sophistication: without real warfare, technique became art.

The **Meiji Restoration** (1868, see [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md)) abolished the samurai class and initially threatened martial traditions. Samurai swords were banned in public in 1876 (the *Haitorei* edict). However, enlightened reformers — most notably Jigoro Kano — recognized that martial disciplines could serve modernizing Japan by fostering national discipline and character development. Rather than being suppressed, martial arts were adapted into the Meiji educational system.

## Judo (柔道)

**Judo** is perhaps the most globally successful martial art to emerge from Japan and exemplifies the transformation of samurai combat techniques into a modern sport and educational practice.

**Origins** — Judo was systematized in **February 1882** by **Jigoro Kano** (嘉納 治五郎, 1860–1938), a young educator who had studied traditional *jūjutsu* (柔術, "gentle art") under masters Fukuda Hachinosuke and Iso Masatomo of the Tenshin Shinyō-ryū school, and Iikubo Tsunetoshi of the Kitō-ryū. He rented a room in the Eishoji Buddhist temple in Shitaya (Tokyo), where his first class had just 9 students on a 12-tatami mat floor. Kano synthesized multiple *jūjutsu* schools with pedagogical rigor, emphasizing:

- **Maximum efficiency with minimum effort** (*seiryoku-zenyo*, 精力善用) — The core physical principle: use an opponent's force and off-balance (*kuzushi*) rather than meeting strength with strength.
- **Mutual welfare and benefit** (*jita-kyoei*, 自他共栄) — The social principle; martial training should benefit both practitioner and society.
- **Safety and accessibility** — Kano removed or restricted the most dangerous *atemi* (striking) and joint-lock techniques, making safe partner training possible at full speed.
- **Progressive belt ranks** (*kyū-dan* system) — Kano introduced the white-belt/black-belt distinction in 1883; the colored intermediate belts were added later in Western countries.

**Kodokan Growth** — Kano moved the Kodokan (講道館, "place for teaching the way") several times as enrollment grew: from 9 students in 1882 to 1,600 by 1911, and over 95,000 registered members by 1945. The Kodokan's current headquarters in Kasuga, Tokyo (a 7-story building opened 1984) has 1,000 tatami mats of practice floor.

**International Spread** — Kano himself promoted judo internationally, becoming in 1909 **the first Asian member of the International Olympic Committee** (IOC). He sent disciples abroad from the 1890s onward: Yoshiaki (Yoshitsugu) Yamashita taught judo to President Theodore Roosevelt at the White House in 1904. The Budokwai in London (1918) became the first judo club in Europe.

**Olympic History** — Judo was demonstrated at the 1932 Los Angeles Olympics and officially included in the **1964 Tokyo Olympics** (the first Olympics held in Asia). Japan swept the gold medals in three weight classes — but was humiliated when the open-weight final was won by the Dutch giant Anton Geesink, signaling judo's true internationalization. Women's judo entered the Olympics in 1992 (Barcelona). By 2024, 204 nations were affiliated with the International Judo Federation (IJF).

The sport emphasizes **throwing** (*nage-waza*, over 60 classified throws in the Gokyo syllabus) and **ground techniques** (*ne-waza*), including pins (*osaekomi-waza*), chokes (*shime-waza*), and joint locks (*kansetsu-waza*). An *ippon* (full point) — awarded for a clean throw, 20-second pin, or submission — ends the match instantly.

## Karate (空手)

**Karate**, meaning "empty hand," is an unarmed striking martial art that developed in Okinawa (the Ryukyu Islands) and was systematized in the 20th century into a global sport and discipline.

**Okinawan Origins** — Karate evolved from indigenous Okinawan combat techniques (*te*, 手, "hand") and adapted influences from Chinese martial arts (*chugoku kenpo*) imported via the Ryukyu Kingdom's historic Chinese trade relations. Okinawa was under Satsuma clan control from 1609, and the prohibition of weapons to Okinawan commoners may have spurred development of empty-hand techniques, though scholars debate this tradition.

**Gichin Funakoshi and Systematization** — **Gichin Funakoshi** (船越 義珍, 1868–1957) brought karate to mainland Japan, giving the first formal demonstration at the **Ministry of Education's First National Athletic Exhibition in May 1922**. He adapted karate to align with Japanese aesthetics and Zen-influenced philosophy, creating a systematic curriculum of *kata* (forms). He founded the Shotokan style (named for his pen name "Shoto") and established Shotokan dojo in Tokyo in 1938. His two books — *Ryukyu Kenpō Karate* (1922) and *Karate-dō Kyōhan* (1935) — standardized the art's techniques and philosophy.

**Modernization** — University karate clubs proliferated from the 1920s. The Japan Karate Association (*Nihon Karate Kyōkai*, NKA) was established in 1949, organizing competition under Master Funakoshi's guidance. During the U.S. occupation (1945–1952), American servicemen stationed in Japan and Okinawa learned karate, initiating its spread to the West — by the mid-1960s, karate schools (*dōjō*) had opened across the United States and Europe.

**Major Styles** (*ryū*) that emerged in the 20th century include:
- **Shotokan** (松濤館流) — Funakoshi's linear, powerful style; most widely practiced worldwide.
- **Goju-Ryu** (剛柔流) — Founded by Chōjun Miyagi (1888–1953) in Okinawa; blends hard (*go*) and soft (*ju*) techniques with breathing exercises (*sanchin*).
- **Shito-Ryu** (糸東流) — Founded by Kenwa Mabuni (1889–1952); emphasizes both *kata* depth and sport kumite.
- **Wado-Ryu** (和道流) — Founded by Hironori Ohtsuka (1892–1982); blends jūjutsu evasion principles with karate strikes.

**Technical Characteristics** — Karate emphasizes striking techniques: punches (*tsuki*), kicks (*geri*), knee and elbow strikes, and open-hand techniques (*shuto*, *nukite*). The philosophy centers on *ikken hissatsu* (一拳必殺, "one punch, certain kill") — the samurai-derived ideal of a single decisive technique. Training cycles through *kihon* (basics), *kata* (patterns), and *kumite* (sparring).

**Olympic Status** — After decades of lobbying, karate was included in the **2020 Tokyo Olympics** (held 2021) as a host-nation showcase, with both *kumite* (sparring, three weight categories per gender) and *kata* (forms) events. Japan's Ryō Kiyuna won gold in men's kata, while Sandra Sánchez of Spain took the women's kata title. However, the IOC did not retain karate as a permanent Olympic sport for Paris 2024 onward, pending a future vote.

## Kendo (剣道)

**Kendo**, the "way of the sword," is a formalized martial art based on the principles and techniques of traditional samurai sword fighting (*kenjutsu*). Practitioners use bamboo swords (*shinai*, 竹刀) and protective armor (*bogu*, 防具 — helmet, breastplate, gauntlets, and hip guard) to engage in full-contact sparring.

**Historical Context** — *Kenjutsu* reached its highest systematization during the Edo period; famous schools included the Ittō-ryū (founded c. 1560), Kashima Shintō-ryū, and Yagyū Shinkage-ryū (patronized by the Tokugawa shoguns). The introduction of the shinai (replacing wooden practice swords, *bokken*) and protective equipment in the 18th century enabled safe full-contact practice. The Meiji government initially discouraged swordsmanship, but included it in police training from 1879; the term *kendo* ("way of the sword") gained currency in the early 20th century.

**Post-War Transformation** — The Allied occupation banned kendo in 1945 as militaristic. Japanese advocates successfully reframed it as sport rather than military training; the **All Japan Kendo Federation** (AJKF, 全日本剣道連盟) was founded in **1952** and kendo was reintroduced to high schools. Today over 1.7 million registered practitioners are affiliated with the AJKF; including unregistered practitioners, estimates reach 8 million.

**Philosophy and Practice** — Kendo emphasizes the unity of mind, sword, and body (*ki-ken-tai-no-itchi*). Advanced practice seeks *mushin* (無心, "no mind") — spontaneous, undeliberated action. The concept of *zanshin* (残心, "remaining mind") — maintained alertness and composure after a strike — is required for valid scoring. Respect (*rei*) permeates all practice: bowing upon entering and leaving the *dōjō*, before and after each bout.

**Competition** — Points (*ippon*) are awarded for strikes to four valid targets: head (*men*, 面), wrist (*kote*, 小手), torso (*dō*, 胴), and throat (*tsuki*, 突き for adults), executed with the correct portion of the shinai and proper spirit. Bouts typically last 3–5 minutes; the first to two *ippon* wins. The **World Kendo Championships** are held every three years (first held 1970 in Tokyo); Japan has dominated but has faced increasing competition from Korean, French, and American teams.

**Cultural Significance** — Kendo remains deeply embedded in Japanese school culture; the Ministry of Education made kendo or judo mandatory for middle-school boys from 2012. Many high schools maintain active kendo clubs (*bukatsudō*) that practice daily and compete in regional and national championships.

## Aikido (合気道)

**Aikido** ("way of harmonious spirit") is a modern martial art developed by **Morihei Ueshiba** (植芝 盛平, 1883–1969), known to practitioners as *Ōsensei* ("Great Teacher"). Ueshiba synthesized techniques from *Daitō-ryū Aiki-jūjutsu* (under master Sōkaku Takeda), *kenjutsu*, and *sōjutsu* (spear) with his own spiritual beliefs rooted in the *Ōmoto-kyō* Shinto sect.

**Development** — Ueshiba began teaching his evolving art formally in 1927 in Tokyo. After relocating to rural Iwama (Ibaraki Prefecture) in 1942, he refined the art's spiritual dimension. The name *aikido* was officially registered with the Dai Nippon Butoku Kai in **1942**. The Aikikai Foundation (合気会) was established in 1948 as the governing organization, headquartered at Aikido World Headquarters (*Hombu Dojo*) in Shinjuku, Tokyo.

**Principles** — Aikido emphasizes *aiki* (blending with an attacker's energy), *tenkan* (turning/pivoting away from attack), and *irimi* (entering directly into attack). The goal is to neutralize an attacker's momentum through redirection rather than direct opposition. Techniques include throws (*nage-waza*), joint locks (*kansetsu-waza*), and pins (*osae-waza*) adapted from jūjutsu. Strikes (*atemi*) exist but are used to create openings rather than as primary techniques.

**Peaceful Philosophy** — Ueshiba taught that aikido's ultimate purpose was peace and reconciliation rather than victory in combat. His famous statement: "The secret of aikido is to become one with the universe." Unlike judo or karate, mainstream aikido (except for the competitive *Shodokan* branch founded by Kenji Tomiki) does not use competitive matches, as Ueshiba believed competition distorted the art's spiritual aims.

**Global Spread** — Kisshomaru Ueshiba (1921–1999), Morihei's son, systematized and internationalized aikido after WWII. Nobuyoshi Tamura, Katsuaki Asai, and other senior students planted the art in Europe; Koichi Tohei established a major U.S. following. By 2020 the Aikikai counted practitioners in over 142 countries. Aikido gained particular appeal through its association with actor Steven Seagal (a legitimate 7th-dan *shihan*), whose action films from the late 1980s sparked worldwide enrollment spikes.

## Kyūdō (弓道) — The Way of the Bow

**Kyūdō** ("way of the bow") is the formalized practice of traditional Japanese archery, derived from the *kyūjutsu* (bow techniques) of samurai warriors. The Japanese asymmetric longbow (*yumi*), traditionally made from laminated bamboo, stands 2.21 meters tall — taller than the archer — and is held at the lower third for a distinctive shooting posture.

**Historical Development** — Archery was foundational to samurai identity; Yabusame (mounted archery) rituals at Shinto shrines date to the Kamakura period. During the [Edo period](../history/edo-period.md), *kyūjutsu* schools proliferated; the Heki-ryū, Honda-ryū, and Ogasawara-ryū became the major classical traditions. The All Nippon Kyūdō Federation (全日本弓道連盟, ANKF) was founded in 1953, unifying the major schools and standardizing practice.

**Philosophy** — Modern kyūdō de-emphasizes accuracy in favor of *shōsha-seichu* ("correct shooting, correct hitting") — the belief that correct form and mental state inevitably produce accurate shots. Practitioners aim to embody *shin-zen-bi* (真善美, "truth, goodness, beauty") in each shot. Shooting ceremonially (*sharei*) or in competition (*kyōgi*) uses specific ritual procedures from entering the range to drawing and releasing. Approximately 500,000 practitioners in Japan, concentrated in high schools and universities.

## Iaidō (居合道)

**Iaidō** is the art of drawing, cutting, and resheathing a sword (*katana*) in a single fluid sequence, practiced as a meditative solo discipline. The word derives from *i* (being), *ai* (harmony), *dō* (way) — "the way of harmonizing being."

**Origins** — Iaidō developed from *iai-jutsu*, a practical samurai technique for drawing and cutting in a single motion against unexpected attacks. The Muso Shinden-ryū and Muso Jikiden Eishin-ryū are the two largest classical schools. Modern iaidō practice uses either real blades (*shinken*) or unsharpened practice blades (*iaitō*). The All Japan Kendo Federation governs iaidō competition alongside kendo.

**Practice** — Iaidō practitioners perform solo *kata* (between 4 and 50+, depending on school and level) simulating combat scenarios — attack from the front, rear, multiple opponents. Competitions are judged on technical precision, spirit, and fluidity rather than athletic performance. Iaidō has a strong following in Europe, particularly France and the Netherlands, where it is practiced as a meditative art.

## Naginata-dō (薙刀道)

**Naginata-dō** is the martial art of the *naginata* (薙刀), a curved pole weapon with a sword-like blade. Historically the weapon of sōhei (warrior monks) and onna-bugeisha (female warriors), naginata practice was systematized as a school subject for girls during the Meiji era.

The All Japan Naginata Federation (全日本薙刀連盟) was established in 1955. Today naginata is practiced predominantly by women in Japan; it is the only Japanese martial art with mandatory gender-segregated competition at the national level. The sport version uses padded naginata (*e-naginata*) and protective armor similar to kendo's. Approximately 60,000 practitioners are registered, with growing international participation in Europe and North America.

## Sumo (相撲)

Japan's ancient wrestling tradition, sumo, occupies a unique position — straddling martial art, Shinto ritual, and professional sport.

**Origins and Shinto Connections** — Sumo's mythological roots reach to the creation of Japan itself; the *Nihon Shoki* (720 CE) describes a legendary wrestling match between deities. Historical sumo was performed at Shinto shrines as ritual (*matsuri*) to entertain the gods and divine the harvest. The ceremony of *dohyō-iri* (ring-entering) and the *shiko* (foot stamping) still evoke these Shinto origins; the *gyōji* (referee) wears Heian-period court dress. Professional sumo was organized during the [Edo period](../history/edo-period.md), with fixed tournament cycles established in the 18th century.

**Structure** — Professional sumo (*ōzumō*) is governed by the Japan Sumo Association (日本相撲協会, JSA), which oversees six honbasho tournaments annually (three in Tokyo, one each in Osaka, Nagoya, and Fukuoka), each lasting 15 days. The 42-rank hierarchy of *rikishi* (wrestlers) runs from *maezumō* (entry) to *yokozuna* (grand champion) — the highest rank, held by only about 73 men in sumo's entire recorded history. The first modern *yokozuna* license was issued in 1789.

**Rules** — A match (*tori-kumi*) is decided when any part of a wrestler's body other than the soles of his feet touches the clay *dohyō* (ring), or when he steps outside its 4.55-meter diameter. Matches typically last seconds; the longest recorded bouts have run just over 4 minutes. The winner is decided by which of the 82 recognized winning techniques (*kimarite*) was successfully executed.

**Contemporary Issues** — From 2003 onward, Mongolian wrestlers dominated the sport: Asashoryu (Dolgorsürengiin Dagvadorj), Hakuho (Mönkhbatyn Davaajargal), and others claimed the majority of *yokozuna* titles and tournament championships. By 2020, Mongolians held 4 of the 5 active *yokozuna* slots. The sport has faced controversies over abusive training and hazing (*kawaigari*) in training stables (*heya*), gambling scandals (2010), and debates about foreign wrestlers adopting Japanese names and customs.

## Budo as Character Development and Social Institution

Across martial arts, a common thread is the concept of **budō as self-cultivation** (*shugyo*, 修行 — ascetic training) rather than merely combative technique. Martial-arts training in Japan is understood to develop:

- **Discipline and perseverance** (*nintai*) — Progressive training cycles, belt examinations, and demanding physical conditioning build patience and resilience applicable beyond the *dōjō*.
- **Respect and hierarchy** (*rei*) — Bowing (*rei*), addressing instructors as *sensei* (先生, "one who came before"), and following strict protocols instill appreciation for social order and earned authority.
- **Spiritual development** — Meditation, focused attention (*zanshin*), and philosophical study connect martial arts to [Shinto](shinto-religion.md) and Zen traditions rooted in the [Kamakura period](../history/kamakura-period.md).
- **Physical health** — Regular training promotes cardiovascular fitness, flexibility, coordination, and postural health across the lifespan.

This educational philosophy led to martial arts' inclusion in Japanese school curricula from the Meiji period. As of 2012, a martial art — most commonly judo or kendo — was made a mandatory element of physical education in Japanese middle schools (grades 7–9), reaching approximately 3 million students annually. University martial-arts clubs (*bukatsudō*) are central to campus culture, with some schools fielding championship kendo, judo, or karate teams that attract national attention.

## International Impact and Cultural Export

The postwar period saw Japanese martial arts become global phenomena. The **1964 Tokyo Olympics** catalyzed judo's worldwide growth; subsequent Olympics (Barcelona 1992, Athens 2004, London 2012) delivered consistently high television audiences for judo competition. As of 2024, the IJF counts 204 national federations.

Popular media was equally crucial: Bruce Lee's films of the early 1970s — while centered on Chinese martial arts — inspired a global martial-arts boom that created mass audiences for Japanese arts. Hollywood films (Ralph Macchio's *The Karate Kid*, 1984) drove karate school enrollments; Steven Seagal's action films of 1988–1995 popularized aikido. The Japanese manga and anime genres produced dozens of martial-arts narratives (*Rurouni Kenshin*, *Baki*, *Hajime no Ippo*) consumed globally and influencing how non-Japanese understand budō philosophy.

**Mixed Martial Arts (MMA)** — Japan was an early MMA hub; the *Shooto* promotion (founded 1985 by wrestler Satoru Sayama as a "shooting art" combining martial arts) predated the UFC (1993) and helped develop the sport. The *K-1* kickboxing promotion (1993–2012) and *PRIDE Fighting Championships* (1997–2007) made Tokyo and Saitama Super Arena major international MMA venues before the sport's center shifted to the United States.

By the early 21st century, martial-arts practitioners vastly outnumber samurai at any point in Japanese history, and the arts are increasingly defined by competitive sport, physical fitness, and spiritual self-improvement. The Japanese government recognized this cultural-export value by establishing the **Japan Budō Association** (日本武道協議会) in 1969, comprising nine member arts: judo, kendo, kyūdō, sumo, karate, aikido, shorinji kempō, naginata, and jūkendo (銃剣道, bayonet art).

## Contemporary Challenges and Evolution

Modern martial arts face several tensions:

- **Commercialization vs. tradition** — The pressure to create spectator-friendly competitions and professional athletes can conflict with traditional emphasis on spiritual development and discipline. The World Judo Tour's rule changes (eliminating leg grabs in 2010, removing ground-work time limits) have been criticized as prioritizing spectacle over classical technique.
- **Safety and liability** — Medical research on chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) and head injuries has prompted kendo and karate organizations to revise protective equipment standards; child-training protocols have been scrutinized after deaths from rough practice.
- **Gender integration** — Women now compete at the Olympics in judo (1992), karate (2020), and are prominent in naginata and kyūdō. However, women's sumo (*joshi sumō*) lacks professional status, and women are officially prohibited from entering the *dohyō* at professional matches — a prohibition defended on Shinto ritual grounds but challenged by feminist advocates (see [Women in Japanese Society](women-in-japanese-society.md)).
- **Globalization and authenticity** — As martial arts spread globally, questions arise about whether non-Japanese practitioners can authentically embody the philosophical and cultural dimensions. Some Japanese masters have criticized the reductive "sport-ification" of arts in overseas contexts, stripped of *rei* and philosophical content.
- **MMA and traditional arts** — The global rise of MMA has prompted debate about the real-world effectiveness of traditional martial arts, drawing practitioners away from classical budō toward pragmatic combat sports.

Despite these tensions, martial arts remain deeply rooted in Japanese society and culture, serving as a symbol of national values that resonates internationally — a living legacy of the samurai tradition (see [Samurai & Bushido](samurai-bushido.md)) transformed for the modern world.
