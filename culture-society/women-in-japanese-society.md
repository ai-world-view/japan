# Women in Japanese Society

**Category**: Culture & Society  
**Key figures**: Murasaki Shikibu, Sei Shōnagon, Empress Meiji, Fukuda Hideko, Hiratsuka Raichō, Ichikawa Fusae, Doi Takako

## Summary

Women's roles and status in Japanese society have undergone profound transformation across centuries, from the aristocratic literacy and cultural patronage of Heian court ladies to the rigid patriarchal structure of the Edo period, through partial Meiji-era emancipation, the democratic reforms of the postwar period, and the complex modern landscape. The arc is neither linear nor uniform: periods of greater women's agency (the Heian aristocratic court, the Buddhist convents of medieval Japan, postwar constitutional equality) alternate with periods of enforced domesticity and legal subordination (the Edo *onna daigaku* tradition, the 1898 Meiji Civil Code, wartime mobilization).

Japan's 1947 Constitution granted women formal legal equality and universal suffrage, eliminating the patriarchal *ie* (household) family code. Yet persistent gaps remain: women earn approximately 75–80% of male wages for comparable roles (2022 data), hold approximately 10% of seats in the national Diet (2024), and constitute roughly 13% of corporate board members (2023) — among the lowest ratios in the developed world. Japan ranked 125th of 146 countries in the World Economic Forum's 2023 Global Gender Gap Index, the lowest among G7 nations. The country's demographic crisis — a total fertility rate of 1.20 (2023 record low) — is widely linked to the structural incompatibility of motherhood with career advancement in Japanese workplaces.

## Heian Period: Literary Agency and Cultural Power (794–1185)

Women of the imperial court during the Heian period (794–1185) exercised significant cultural and intellectual influence unmatched in most of the subsequent millennium. Literacy in classical Chinese was a male aristocratic preserve, but *kana* syllabic script — more flexible and suited to Japanese phonology — became associated with women's writing, paradoxically enabling female authors to produce the major literary works of the era while men wrote formal Chinese.

**Murasaki Shikibu** (c. 973–c. 1014 or 1025), lady-in-waiting to Empress Shōshi, wrote *Genji Monogatari* (*The Tale of Genji*, c. 1010), generally considered the world's first novel and an unparalleled achievement of psychological and literary art. Her contemporary **Sei Shōnagon** (c. 966–c. 1017 or 1025), lady-in-waiting to Empress Teishi, wrote the *Makura no Sōshi* (*The Pillow Book*), a collection of observations, lists, and reflections that remains a masterpiece of Japanese prose. Both women wrote extensively about court life, human nature, and the aesthetics of *mono no aware* (the pathos of things). See [Murasaki Shikibu](../people/murasaki-shikibu.md) for her biography and literary legacy.

These women's agency was real but circumscribed: their influence operated within the emperor's court and through powerful patrons. Marriage politics, which used women as diplomatic instruments, could cut short careers and relocate women to provincial estates far from the capital's cultural life. Daughters of high-ranking *Fujiwara* nobles were positioned as consorts or mothers of emperors, making women (and their fathers) central to court power dynamics.

## Kamakura and Muromachi Periods: Warrior Culture and Changing Status (1185–1573)

The rise of the warrior (*samurai*) class from the late Heian period into the Kamakura shogunate (1185–1333) altered women's property rights and social roles. Under Heian court customs, women of noble families could inherit property and manage estates. The Kamakura legal code (*Goseibai Shikimoku*, 1232) still recognized women's rights to inherit and bequeath land, and samurai widows sometimes managed domain affairs.

The successive political crises of the Nanbokuchō period (1336–1392) and the Ōnin War (1467–1477) progressively eroded women's inheritance rights as the costs of military service drained family resources and male heirs were prioritized to maintain fighting capacity. By the late Muromachi period, daughters' inheritance shares had been effectively eliminated in most warrior households.

Buddhist convents offered women an alternative social role: the convent at Hokkeji in Nara, revived as a formal *bikuni* (nun) institution in the 13th century, provided educated women a path to religious authority and scholarship outside the household system.

## Edo Period: Codified Patriarchy (1603–1868)

The Edo period (1603–1868) enshrined female subordination through Confucian ideology backed by shogunal law. Neo-Confucian thinker Kaibara Ekiken (1630–1714) wrote the *Onna Daigaku* (Great Learning for Women, c. 1710), a conduct manual that became the standard text for female education. Its central precepts — obedience to father before marriage, to husband after marriage, and to son in widowhood — codified the "three obediences" (*sanjū*) that structured women's lives. The *ie* (household) system, formalized in Edo-era law and eventually enshrined in the 1898 Meiji Civil Code, gave the male head of household (*koshu*) near-total legal authority over family members.

Upper-class samurai women received education in classical literature, calligraphy, and household management but were barred from political life and property ownership. Commoner women (*chōnin* and peasant) often participated in household economic activities — running shops, weaving, farming — in ways that gave them practical agency within strict domestic frameworks. The *geisha* institution, emerging in the mid-18th century, provided some women with artistic careers outside domestic roles, though within a regulated entertainment system that itself reflected patriarchal assumptions.

## Meiji Era: Modernization Without Emancipation (1868–1912)

The Meiji Restoration (1868) transformed Japan's political and economic structures while preserving and in some respects intensifying patriarchal family law. The Meiji Constitution (1889) restricted political rights to men. The 1898 Civil Code codified the *ie* household system: women had no right to hold property independently of their household head, could not initiate divorce (though their husbands could), and were classified as legally incompetent alongside minors. The 1890 Rescript on Education emphasized women's role as mothers of loyal imperial subjects (*ryōsai kenbo*, "good wife, wise mother"), a phrase coined in 1875 that became the dominant ideology of women's education.

Nevertheless, Meiji modernization opened limited new paths. Girls' schools (*jogakkō*) expanded: by 1912 there were over 500 higher girls' schools (*kōtō jogakkō*) educating approximately 100,000 students. Professions including nursing, primary school teaching, and some industrial work became acceptable for women. **Empress Meiji** (Haruko, 1849–1914) patronized the founding of the Japanese Red Cross and women's educational associations. **Fukuda Hideko** (1865–1927) was one of the era's most radical figures: imprisoned in 1885 for involvement in a plot to destabilize the Chōsen dynasty in support of Korean independence, she later founded the socialist-feminist journal *Sekai Fujin* (Women of the World) in 1907. The 1900 Public Peace Police Law (Chian Keisatsu Hō) explicitly prohibited women from joining political associations or attending political meetings, criminalizing women's political participation.

See [Empress Meiji](../people/empress-meiji.md) for her role in women's education and philanthropy.

## Taishō New Woman and Early Feminism (1912–1926)

The Taishō era produced Japan's most vibrant pre-war feminist movement. **Hiratsuka Raichō** (1886–1971) founded the literary-feminist journal *Seitō* (Bluestocking) in September 1911, declaring in its inaugural issue: *"In the beginning, woman was the sun. An authentic person. Today she is the moon, living through others."* Seitō published until 1916, featuring women's creative writing alongside essays on reproductive rights, prostitution, and the constraints of marriage. Hiratsuka's later co-founding of the Shin Fujin Kyōkai (New Women's Association, 1920) with **Ichikawa Fusae** (1893–1981) and Oku Mumeo directly lobbied for repeal of the 1900 Public Peace Police Law's ban on women in politics — achieved in 1922.

The "New Woman" (*atarashii onna*) became a contested cultural archetype of urban Taishō Japan: educated, professional or artistic, challenging domestic ideals, visible in cafés and on street-cars. The "modern girl" (*moga*) of the mid-1920s represented a younger, more consumer-oriented version of this figure — shorter hair, Western dress, jazz cafés. Both figures attracted intense cultural commentary, often disapproving from conservative commentators who saw them as threats to the *ie* system and declining birth rates.

Textile factories employing young women (*jokō*) — approximately 600,000 female workers in spinning and weaving by 1920 — became sites of labor organizing. The factory girl literature (*jokō bungaku*) documented harsh conditions: twelve-hour shifts, dormitory confinement, tuberculosis rates several times the national average. Hosoi Wakizō's *Jokō Aishi* (Sad History of Factory Girls, 1925) was a landmark exposé. See [Taishō Period](../history/taisho-period.md) for the broader democratic and labor context.

## Wartime Mobilization and Postwar Reform (1931–1952)

The militarist turn of the 1930s mobilized women in contradictory ways. The Greater Japan Women's Association (*Dai Nihon Fujinkai*, founded 1942) organized 19 million members in war support activities — collecting metal, raising funds, sending off soldiers. Women entered industrial work in unprecedented numbers as men were conscripted, working in munitions factories and the nascent aviation industry. Yet the political and legal constraints of the *ie* system remained intact throughout the war.

American Occupation authorities (1945–1952) imposed sweeping changes. The October 1945 directive required women's suffrage as a democratic reform condition. Japanese women voted for the first time in the April 10, 1946 House of Representatives election, in which 39 women won seats — a proportion not equaled again until 2009. The 1947 Constitution's Article 14 guaranteed equality under law regardless of sex. The revised 1947 Civil Code abolished the *ie* household system, granting women equal inheritance rights, the right to choose their spouse, and independent legal standing. Article 24 specifically guaranteed marriage equality and individual dignity within the family.

## High-Growth Era and the Domestic Ideal (1955–1985)

The economic miracle of 1955–1973 reinforced a new variant of the "good wife, wise mother" ideal within corporate Japan. The *salaryman* (*sarariiman*) model assumed a male breadwinner and a full-time housewife (*sengyo shufu*) managing the household and children's education — the two-income nuclear family was structurally discouraged by tax policies (the ¥1.03 million income ceiling for spousal deductions), corporate norms, and school scheduling. Women were expected to leave the workforce upon marriage or first birth (the so-called "M-curve" in female labor force participation: high among young women, dropping sharply in the 25–35 age range, then rising again as children matured).

Women's labor force participation did remain significant: the "M-curve" showed women returning to part-time work (*pāto*) after childrearing, but this work was structurally inferior — hourly wages 60–70% of full-time rates, no benefits, no promotion track. Women were formally excluded from the core *nenkō joretsu* (seniority-based wage) system that gave male regular workers guaranteed pay increases and job security.

## Contemporary Landscape: Formal Equality, Structural Barriers (1985–present)

The Equal Employment Opportunity Law (Danjo Koyō Kikai Kintōhō, EEOL), enacted June 1985 and effective April 1986, prohibited overt discrimination in hiring and promotion — but provided no enforcement mechanism or penalties, making it largely aspirational in its initial form. Amendment in 1997 strengthened its provisions; further amendments in 2006 extended anti-harassment provisions.

**Doi Takako** (1928–2014) became the first woman to lead a major Japanese political party when she assumed chairmanship of the Japan Socialist Party in September 1986, and later became the first female Speaker of the House of Representatives (1993). Despite this milestone, women's political representation in Japan remained persistently low: approximately 10% of lower house Diet seats (2024), compared to 30–40% in comparable democracies. Japan ranks among the lowest in the G7 for women in national legislature.

Prime Minister Abe Shinzō's "Womenomics" policy (2013–2020) aimed to increase female labor force participation as a response to demographic decline, setting targets of 30% women in leadership positions by 2020 (not achieved) and expanding childcare infrastructure. Female labor force participation rose from 62% (2013) to 73% (2022) — closing much of the gap with European comparators — but the expansion was primarily in part-time, low-wage work rather than regular full-time positions.

The total fertility rate fell to a record low of 1.20 in 2023, the eighth consecutive year of decline. Demographic research consistently identifies the structural incompatibility of career and motherhood — inadequate childcare, workplace inflexibility, husband's limited participation in domestic work (Japanese men average 45 minutes of housework per day vs. 3.5 hours for women, 2021 survey) — as primary drivers of birth rate decline. Policy responses have increased parental leave availability, but male uptake remains low (approximately 17% of eligible fathers took leave in 2022, though rising from under 3% in 2010).

The WEF Global Gender Gap Report 2023 ranked Japan 125th of 146 countries, the lowest among G7 nations, reflecting poor scores on political empowerment (138th) and economic participation (123rd) despite near-parity in educational attainment and health outcomes.

## Significance

The history of women in Japanese society illuminates the variability of women's status across different economic and political configurations, and the complex relationship between formal legal reform and social transformation. Heian literary culture demonstrates women's capacity for extraordinary agency within narrow institutional channels; the Edo *onna daigaku* tradition demonstrates how explicit patriarchal ideology can coexist with women's practical economic participation. The postwar constitutional equality reforms were genuine and consequential — the *ie* system's abolition fundamentally altered family law — yet the persistence of occupational segregation, wage gaps, and political underrepresentation in the 2020s demonstrates that legal reform is necessary but not sufficient for social equality.

Japan's contemporary situation — formal equality combined with deep structural inequality — is a case of particular scholarly interest internationally, relevant to debates about whether economic development automatically produces gender equality or whether specific institutional interventions are required. The demographic crisis, in which women's rational response to structural incompatibilities produces below-replacement fertility, connects women's status directly to national economic and social policy at the highest levels.
