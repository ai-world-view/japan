# Abe Shinzo

**Abe Shinzo** (安倍晋三; September 21, 1954 – July 8, 2022) was Japan's longest-serving prime minister, holding office across two discontinuous terms: September 26, 2006 – September 26, 2007 (first tenure) and December 26, 2012 – September 16, 2020 (second tenure). His combined service totaled approximately 3,188 days — surpassing Katsura Tarō's cumulative record and, in his uninterrupted second term, Eisaku Satō's record for consecutive days in office — and defined Japan's geopolitical posture, constitutional interpretation, and economic strategy for the early 21st century.

## Political Origins and Family Background

Abe was born in Tokyo on September 21, 1954, into one of Japan's most prominent political dynasties:

- **Grandfather**: **Nobusuke Kishi** (Prime Minister 1957–1960), a prewar cabinet minister who served in Tōjō Hideki's wartime cabinet, was imprisoned as a Class A war crimes suspect by the Allied occupation but never tried, and was released in 1948. As PM, Kishi rammed through the revised US-Japan Security Treaty in a controversial midnight Diet session on May 19–20, 1960, triggering the **Anpo crisis** — the largest popular uprising in postwar Japan, with 300,000 protesters surrounding the Diet — before resigning after ratification.
- **Father**: **Shintarō Abe** (Foreign Minister 1982–1986), a senior LDP figure who had been considered a likely prime minister candidate before his death from cancer in 1991.
- **Great-uncle**: **Satō Eisaku** (Prime Minister 1964–1972), the longest-serving PM prior to Shinzo Abe's record, who signed the US-Japan nuclear umbrella understanding and received the 1974 Nobel Peace Prize for the Non-Proliferation Treaty.

Abe studied political science at Seikei University (Tokyo) and interned at Kobe Steel before joining the Foreign Ministry and later entering his father's political office. He was first elected to the House of Representatives in 1993 from Yamaguchi-4th district, the seat his father had held. Within the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) he aligned with the Seiwa-kai — the Fukuda-lineage conservative nationalist faction — and rose rapidly as a protégé of Yoshiro Mori.

## First Tenure as Prime Minister (2006–2007)

Abe succeeded Junichirō Koizumi in September 2006 at age 52, the youngest postwar prime minister. His first cabinet included several "first-timers" and a greater-than-usual number of women. His stated priorities were:

- **Constitutional revision** — explicit commitment to revising Article 9 (the war-renunciation clause), the first sitting PM to make this a cabinet-level platform rather than a long-term aspiration.
- **Education reform** — revised the Fundamental Law of Education (December 2006), restoring references to patriotism and public spirit excised in the 1947 occupation-era version.
- **Diplomatic activism** — his first foreign trip as PM (October 2006) was to Beijing and Seoul, signaling an attempt to repair relations strained by Koizumi's annual Yasukuni Shrine visits; he also convened what he called the "arc of freedom and prosperity" concept with India, Australia, and democratic Southeast Asian states — a precursor to the later Quad and Free and Open Indo-Pacific frameworks.

The first tenure collapsed within a year. The LDP suffered heavy losses in the July 2007 House of Councillors election — winning only 37 of the 121 contested seats and ceding control of the upper house to the opposition Democratic Party of Japan — and Abe faced multiple ministerial scandals. Already suffering from **ulcerative colitis** (a condition he had managed for years), Abe abruptly announced his resignation on September 12, 2007, shortly after opening a new Diet session.

## Return and Second Tenure (2012–2020)

After five years outside government, Abe won the LDP presidency in September 2012, narrowly defeating Shigeru Ishiba in a runoff, and led the party to a landslide House of Representatives victory (294 seats) on December 16, 2012. He returned as Prime Minister on December 26, 2012.

His second tenure was anchored by a new drug regimen (Pentasa and later biologics) that controlled his colitis, and by a more disciplined political strategy learned from the failures of 2006–2007. He won four consecutive national elections — 2013 Councillors, 2014 Representatives, 2016 Councillors, 2017 Representatives — consolidating unprecedented legislative dominance.

## Abenomics

Abe's economic program, branded **"Abenomics"** (*Abenomikusu*), was framed as three "arrows":

### First Arrow: Monetary Expansion
In coordination with Bank of Japan Governor **Haruhiko Kuroda** (appointed April 2013), the BOJ launched **Quantitative and Qualitative Monetary Easing** (QQE):
- Initial target: purchase ¥60–70 trillion in Japanese Government Bonds (JGBs) annually.
- Expanded (October 2014): annual purchases raised to ¥80 trillion.
- **Negative interest rate policy** (January 2016): BOJ set the policy rate at −0.1% on excess reserves.
- BOJ's total assets grew from approximately ¥158 trillion (end-2012) to over ¥700 trillion by the end of Abe's tenure, representing roughly 130% of GDP — the largest central bank balance sheet relative to GDP among major economies.

The yen weakened sharply: from approximately ¥76/$ at the end of 2011 to ¥125/$ by June 2015, before stabilizing around ¥105–115/$. This boosted exporters (Toyota's operating profit rose from ¥1.32 trillion in FY2012 to ¥2.75 trillion in FY2014) and lifted the Nikkei 225 from 8,664 (November 2012) to over 20,000 by 2015.

### Second Arrow: Fiscal Stimulus
The government ran supplementary budgets totaling ¥10–20 trillion in the early years, funding infrastructure, childcare centers, and disaster preparedness. However, two consumption tax increases — from 5% to 8% (April 2014) and 8% to 10% (October 2019) — partially offset stimulus, causing GDP contractions in the quarters following each hike.

### Third Arrow: Structural Reform
The third arrow — deregulation, labor reform, and productivity enhancement — proved hardest to implement against LDP structural interests:
- **Womenomics** — set a goal of 30% women in leadership positions by 2020 (target largely missed); created more childcare places and introduced overtime caps on working hours (Work Style Reform Act, 2018).
- **Specified Skilled Worker visa** (2019) — opened new immigration pathways for up to 345,000 foreign workers in 14 designated sectors over five years, marking a significant departure from Japan's historically restrictive immigration policy.
- **Corporate governance reforms** — introduced the Stewardship Code (2014) and Corporate Governance Code (2015) to encourage institutional investors to engage companies on returns on equity; MSCI inclusion of Japanese stocks increased foreign ownership.
- **Trans-Pacific Partnership** — Abe reversed Japan's initial skepticism of TPP and joined negotiations in March 2013; after the US withdrew under President Trump (January 2017), Japan led the 11-nation **CPTPP** (*Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership*), which entered into force December 30, 2018.

## Security Policy and Constitutional Reinterpretation

Abe's most constitutionally significant act was reinterpreting Article 9 of the postwar Constitution without formal amendment. On July 1, 2014, the Abe cabinet adopted a **Cabinet Decision** reversing Japan's 1960 constitutional interpretation: Japan could now exercise the right of **collective self-defense** — using force if an ally under attack and Japan's survival or fundamental rights were threatened.

Implementing legislation passed the Diet on September 19, 2015, over mass protests including a 100,000-person rally outside the Diet building. The legislation:
- Authorized Japan Self-Defense Forces (JSDF) to provide logistical and combat support to allies in armed conflict overseas.
- Allowed "ship defense" — JSDF naval vessels protecting allied ships in waters near Japan.
- Loosened rules of engagement for JSDF peacekeeping missions.

This represented the most significant shift in Japan's postwar security posture since the 1950 rearmament decision and the 1954 establishment of the Self-Defense Forces. Defense spending, which had been capped at approximately 1% of GDP since 1976, was allowed to drift upward from approximately ¥4.78 trillion (2012) to ¥5.26 trillion (2020).

## Geopolitical Positioning: Free and Open Indo-Pacific

Abe first articulated the concept of a **"Free and Open Indo-Pacific"** (FOIP) in an August 2016 speech in Nairobi, Kenya. The concept — framing the Pacific and Indian Oceans as a unified strategic space requiring democratic guardrails against authoritarian coercion — became the organizing framework for Japanese, US, and Australian regional strategy. Key elements:

- **Quadrilateral Security Dialogue** ("Quad") — Japan, US, India, Australia first convened in 2007 at Abe's initiative; the Quad was relaunched at Abe's initiative in 2017 after a decade of dormancy following Australia's withdrawal in 2008.
- **ODA pivot** — Japan redirected Official Development Assistance toward port infrastructure and capacity-building in Southeast Asia, South Asia, and Africa as a counterweight to Chinese Belt and Road lending.
- **Defense cooperation** — bilateral and multilateral exercises (Malabar, Pacific Vanguard) involving JSDF alongside US, Indian, and Australian forces.
- **South Korea tensions** — despite FOIP framework, historical disputes (comfort women 2015 agreement controversies, wartime labor court rulings) repeatedly disrupted Japan-South Korea security coordination; a 2019 trade dispute cut technology exports, marking a diplomatic low point.

## Domestic Controversies

- **Moritomo Gakuen scandal** (February 2017) — a nationalist elementary school (connected to Abe's wife Akie Abe through honorary positions) received a below-market government land sale; Finance Ministry bureaucrats later admitted to altering official documents to conceal the connection.
- **Kake Gakuen scandal** (May 2017) — a veterinary school obtained government approval for a new campus in a special economic zone in Imabari, Ehime; the school's founder, Kōtarō Kake, was a longtime personal friend of Abe, raising favoritism allegations.
- The Moritomo document-tampering led to the March 2018 suicide of **Toshio Akagi**, an official at the Kinki Local Finance Bureau who had been ordered to falsify records; his death became a symbol of the human cost of the cover-up.

## Resignation and Assassination

Abe resigned on August 28, 2020, citing the recurrence of his ulcerative colitis requiring treatment not compatible with his duties. His successor **Yoshihide Suga** served until October 4, 2021, when **Fumio Kishida** took over.

Abe remained active as the head of the Seiwa-kai faction and as a political power broker. On **July 8, 2022**, while delivering a campaign speech for LDP candidates in the House of Councillors election in **Nara**, Abe was shot twice with a homemade firearm and died from blood loss shortly after. The gunman, **Tetsuya Yamagami** (41), told investigators he targeted Abe because of Abe's promotion of the **Unification Church** (Family Federation for World Peace and Unification), which Yamagami blamed for his mother's ruinous donations.

The assassination triggered a national reckoning. Investigations revealed that dozens of LDP Diet members — including cabinet ministers — had longstanding ties to the Unification Church, attending events, writing congratulatory letters, and receiving organizational support. Prime Minister Kishida ordered an internal review that documented 179 LDP members with church connections, prompting widespread resignations and damage to LDP approval ratings lasting into 2024.

Abe's state funeral was held at **Nippon Budokan** on **September 27, 2022**, attended by approximately 4,000 dignitaries including US Vice President Kamala Harris; it was the first state funeral for a former prime minister since Shigeru Yoshida in 1967. His private burial took place at **Zōjōji Temple** in Tokyo, where the Abe family had long been parishioners.

## Historical Significance

Abe Shinzo's legacy is contested but undeniably transformative:

1. **Constitutional revision without amendment** — by reinterpreting Article 9 rather than amending it, Abe achieved security normalization that had eluded conservative PMs for 60 years while avoiding the political cost of a referendum.
2. **Economic experimentation at scale** — Abenomics constituted the most aggressive test of monetary stimulus combined with fiscal expansion in a major economy, providing empirical data (and cautionary lessons) for central bankers globally.
3. **Indo-Pacific architecture** — the FOIP concept and Quad revival, taken up by the Biden administration in 2021, became the organizing framework for democratic deterrence against China's maritime expansion.
4. **LDP restructuring** — his long tenure consolidated the LDP's rightward shift on security while exposing its organizational vulnerabilities (faction finances, organizational entanglements) that emerged as scandals after his death.
