# Katsushika Hokusai

**Category**: People
**Born**: c. October 31, 1760, Edo (modern [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md))
**Died**: May 10, 1849, Edo, age 88
**Occupation**: Painter, woodblock print artist, illustrator

## Summary

Katsushika Hokusai (葛飾北斎, 1760–1849) was one of Japan's most influential visual artists and the towering figure of the *ukiyo-e* ("floating world") woodblock printing tradition. Over a career spanning seven decades, he produced an estimated **30,000 artworks** across painting, printmaking, and book illustration, working under at least 30 distinct artistic names. His series *Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji* (c. 1830–1832) yielded "The Great Wave off Kanagawa," the most internationally recognized Japanese artwork and among the most reproduced images in the history of art. When his prints reached Europe in the mid-19th century, they catalyzed a transformation of Western art—the movement known as *Japonisme*—influencing Monet, Van Gogh, Degas, Toulouse-Lautrec, and the development of Impressionism and Art Nouveau.

## Early Life and Training

Hokusai was born around October 1760 in the Honjo district of Edo (present-day Sumida, [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md))—a working-class neighborhood of artisans and merchants on the eastern bank of the Sumida River. His birth name was Tokitarō (時太郎); his family surname is uncertain, though some sources suggest he may have been born into the Kawamura family or been adopted by Nakajima Ise, a mirror-polisher who served the shogunate.

At approximately age six, Hokusai was apprenticed to a wood engraver (lending libraries), where he learned to cut blocks for book illustrations. Around age 14 (c. 1774) he apprenticed at a rental-book shop, where he encountered the popular prints and illustrated books of the day. In 1778, at age 18, he entered the Katsukawa school of *ukiyo-e* art under master Katsukawa Shunshō (勝川春章), adopting the name Katsukawa Shunrō (勝川春朗). Shunshō's school was known primarily for portraits of kabuki actors (*yakusha-e*) and sumo wrestlers—the celebrity portraiture of the [Edo period](../history/edo-period.md).

Hokusai was expelled from the Katsukawa school around 1794 after studying privately with Tawaraya Sōri, a master of the rival Rinpa school, demonstrating the eclecticism that would define his career. He subsequently studied Kanō school ink painting, Tosa school traditional Japanese painting, and Western perspective through Dutch and Chinese engravings imported through the port of Nagasaki—the sole point of Western contact permitted under the Tokugawa shogunate's policy of *sakoku* (isolationism).

## Career: Reinvention and Name Changes

Hokusai's practice of changing his artistic name was not mere eccentricity but a deliberate artistic statement—each name change signaled a new creative direction and, in effect, a new artistic persona. Major periods and names include:

| Period | Name | Focus |
|--------|------|-------|
| c. 1779–1794 | Shunrō (春朗) | Actor and wrestler portraits |
| c. 1794–1798 | Sōri (宗理) | Surimono prints, illustration |
| c. 1798–1806 | Hokusai (北斎) | Landscapes, book illustration |
| c. 1807–1819 | Taito (戴斗) | Manga sketchbooks begin |
| c. 1820–1834 | Iitsu (為一) | *Thirty-Six Views* series |
| c. 1834–1849 | Manji (卍) | *One Hundred Views* series; final works |

Hokusai later said he considered his work before age 70 "not worth taking into account," believing he had only truly begun to understand nature and art in old age. He changed his name to "Manji" (卍) at age 74, writing in the postscript to *One Hundred Views of Mount Fuji* (1834): "From the age of 6 I had a penchant for copying the form of things, and from about 50, my pictures were frequently published; but until the age of 70, nothing that I drew was worthy of notice. At 73 years, I was somewhat able to fathom the growth of plants and trees, and the structure of birds, animals, insects, and fish. Thus when I reach 80 years, I hope to have made increasing progress, and at 90 to see further into the underlying principles of things, so that at 100 years I will have achieved a divine state in my art, and at 110, every dot and every stroke will be as though alive."

## Major Works

### Hokusai Manga (北斎漫画, 1814–1878)

Between 1814 and his death (with posthumous volumes continuing to 1878), Hokusai produced 15 volumes of sketch collections—the *Hokusai Manga*—comprising approximately 4,000 drawings of animals, plants, people, landscapes, supernatural beings, and geometric forms. The Manga were printed as instructional books for art students and became enormously popular, going through multiple editions. They reached Europe along with other Japanese woodblock prints and were studied intensively by Western artists. Edgar Degas owned multiple volumes; the influence on his figures in motion is direct and documented.

### *Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji* (富嶽三十六景, c. 1830–1833)

Published by Nishimuraya Yohachi beginning around 1830, this series originally comprised 36 prints and was expanded to 46 as popular demand warranted. The series depicted Mount Fuji—sacred mountain and symbol of Japan—from varying distances and perspectives: from the sea, from rice fields, from the rooftops of Edo, from the grounds of shrines and temples. Hokusai's integration of Western linear perspective (learned from Dutch engravings) with Japanese compositional conventions produced a visual language unlike anything in previous Japanese art.

**"The Great Wave off Kanagawa"** (神奈川沖浪裏, c. 1831): The series' most famous image shows three boats—*oshiokuri-bune* (cargo transport vessels) carrying fish to Edo—threatened by an enormous wave with claw-like foam. Mount Fuji is reduced to a small, snow-capped triangle in the lower center. The image was printed in **Prussian blue** (*berorin-ai* in Japanese)—a synthetic iron-cyanide pigment invented in Berlin around 1704 and introduced to Japan via the Dutch trade in the 1820s. Prussian blue's unusual chemical stability and vivid hue enabled rich, luminous blue gradations impossible with natural Japanese pigments. Hokusai and his contemporary Hiroshige adopted the pigment enthusiastically, transforming the color palette of Japanese printmaking. The wave itself, though often called a "tsunami," is more accurately an *ōkina nami* (great wave) or rogue wave—the whitecap foam is stylized into repeated curved claws, a Hokusai trademark.

**"Red Fuji"** (凱風快晴, "Fine Wind, Clear Weather"): A dawn view of Fuji's reddish summit against a cerulean sky, considered one of the most elegant distillations of Japanese aesthetic restraint.

### *One Hundred Views of Mount Fuji* (富嶽百景, 1834–1835)

A book-format series in three volumes (a fourth was planned but never completed), drawn in the Manji period. More experimental than the prints, these monochrome brush illustrations show Fuji rendered in clouds, reflections, shadows, and conceptual compositions. They demonstrate Hokusai's increasing interest in abstraction and found a devoted following among Japanese art connoisseurs.

### *Thirty-Six Views of the Eastern Capital*, Waterfalls, and Birds-and-Flowers Series

In the same decade, Hokusai produced several parallel series: *Waterfalls in Various Provinces* (諸国瀧廻り, c. 1833), *A Tour of Waterfalls in Various Provinces*, and *Small Flowers* (小花) bird-and-flower prints notable for their combination of scientific observation and compositional dynamism.

## Domestic Influence: Edo Culture

Hokusai operated at the apex of the [Edo period](../history/edo-period.md) cultural world centered in Edo—the sprawling merchant capital that was the largest city in the world by the early 18th century. The *ukiyo-e* tradition in which he worked was a commercial art form serving the urban merchant class (chōnin), produced through a collaborative process: a designer (eshi), a block carver (hōrishi), a printer (surishi), and a publisher (hanmoto) each played distinct roles. Hokusai's extraordinary output—and his willingness to work in almost every genre, from kabuki actor portraits to *shunga* (erotic prints) to history illustrations to children's book—reflected the [Edo period's](../history/edo-period.md) vibrant popular culture.

His depictions of [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md)'s proto-urban landscape—Edo's bridges, pleasure districts, craftsmen's shops, and crowded streets—provide some of the most detailed visual records of everyday life in the Tokugawa city. His students included the printmaker Totoya Hokkei (魚屋北渓) and a daughter, Katsushika Ōi (葛飾応為), who was an accomplished artist in her own right, known especially for interior night scenes using candlelight effects.

## European Influence: Japonisme

When Japan opened to Western trade following Commodore Perry's "Black Ships" mission in 1853 and the [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md) of 1868, Japanese woodblock prints flooded European and American markets. Hokusai's works reached Paris, London, and Amsterdam, where they were avidly collected by artists and connoisseurs. The term *Japonisme* (coined by French art critic Philippe Burty around 1872) described the pervasive influence of Japanese aesthetics on Western art from the 1860s through the early 20th century.

Direct and documented influences include:

- **Claude Monet** collected over 200 Japanese woodblock prints, displayed in his home at Giverny; Hokusai's composition influenced Monet's water-lily paintings and his "Japanese Bridge" series.
- **Vincent van Gogh** directly copied Hiroshige prints in oil paint (1887) and wrote admiringly of Hokusai's work to his brother Theo: "Hokusai makes you cry out in the same way—'My God, those artists are great.'" Van Gogh's The Starry Night (1889) shows Hokusai's influence in its dynamic swirling sky.
- **Edgar Degas** and **Toulouse-Lautrec** absorbed Hokusai's asymmetrical compositions, unexpected cropping, and emphasis on figures in motion.
- **Mary Cassatt**'s aquatint prints of the 1890s directly imitate the flat color areas and graphic line of Japanese printmaking.
- The decorative elongation and organic curves of **Art Nouveau**—in the work of Alphonse Mucha, Gustav Klimt, and others—owe substantial debt to Japanese compositional conventions Hokusai helped codify.

## Legacy

Hokusai died on May 10, 1849, in Edo, at the age of 88, reportedly uttering: "If only Heaven will give me just ten more years... Just a few more years more, then I could become a real artist." His final creative period, working in his 80s after surviving a stroke, demonstrated a sustained artistic vitality without parallel in the history of Japanese art.

In the century and a half since his death, Hokusai's reputation has continued to grow:

- In 1999, *Life* magazine named Hokusai one of the **100 most important people of the millennium**.
- "The Great Wave" has been reproduced on Japanese commemorative banknotes, global consumer goods, and cultural objects ranging from the 2020 Tokyo Olympics to contemporary streetwear—a circulation that makes it plausibly the most reproduced non-photographic artwork in history.
- In 2024, Japan issued a new ¥1,000 banknote featuring "The Great Wave" on its reverse—a formal recognition of Hokusai's role as a national cultural icon.
- The **Sumida Hokusai Museum** (すみだ北斎美術館), opened in 2016 in the Sumida ward of [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md) near Hokusai's birthplace, maintains a permanent collection and rotating exhibitions of his work.

Hokusai represents the apex of the [Edo period](../history/edo-period.md) popular arts and the bridge between Japan's pre-modern visual culture and the global artistic movements of the modern era. His integration of Western perspective, new synthetic pigments, and Japanese compositional traditions prefigured the broader synthesis of Western and Japanese techniques that defined the [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md) era's cultural program—even though Hokusai himself lived entirely within the Tokugawa period and died 19 years before Meiji began. His [Edo cultural milieu](../culture-society/edo-culture-arts.md), with its merchant-class patronage, popular publishing industry, and vibrant mixing of high and low aesthetic traditions, made his extraordinary output possible.
