# Emperor Meiji

**Emperor Meiji** (明治天皇; given name **Mutsuhito**, 睦仁; November 3, 1852 – July 30, 1912) was the 122nd Emperor of Japan whose 45-year reign (r. 1868–1912) presided over Japan's transformation from an isolated feudal state into a modern nation-state rivaling Western powers. His era, the **Meiji period** (明治時代, "period of enlightened rule"), witnessed the abolition of the samurai class, the rapid industrialization of the economy, the adoption of constitutional government, the establishment of a conscript military, imperial expansion in Asia, and Japan's emergence as the first non-Western great power. Though the policies of his era were executed by a cadre of oligarchic advisors known as the *Genrō* ("elder statesmen"), Meiji's personal authority, symbolic centrality, and evident intelligence were indispensable to the legitimacy and coherence of Japan's modernization.

## Early Life and Accession

Mutsuhito was born on November 3, 1852, at the Kyoto Imperial Palace, the second son of Emperor Kōmei (r. 1846–1867). His mother, **Nakayama Yoshiko** (1834–1907), was a *naidaidokoro* (chief lady-in-waiting) of the Nakayama house of court nobles (*kuge*). In court practice, the infant was placed for early childhood in the care of his maternal grandfather, Nakayama Tadayasu, a senior court noble — a convention preserving the health of imperial infants, since palace quarters were considered potentially dangerous for newborns.

When his elder brother, the designated heir apparent, died in infancy in 1858, Mutsuhito was formally recognized as the heir. He received the classical education of a Heian court aristocrat — Chinese poetry and classics, calligraphy, Buddhist ceremony, Shinto theology, and martial disciplines — overlaid with the more rigorous Confucian and Shinto nationalist scholarship that characterized the late Edo imperial court.

### The Crisis of the Bakumatsu

The years of Mutsuhito's childhood coincided with the *bakumatsu* (幕末, "end of the shogunate"), Japan's most turbulent political period since the Sengoku era. Commodore Matthew Perry's Black Ships arrived in Edo Bay in July 1853, followed by Perry's second visit in 1854, which produced the **Kanagawa Treaty** (Convention of Kanagawa, March 31, 1854) — the first breach of Japan's *sakoku* (closed-country) policy, opening the ports of Shimoda and Hakodate to American ships. The subsequent **Ansei Treaties** of 1858, signed under duress by Shogunate officials without imperial approval, opened five more ports including Kobe and Nagasaki, and granted Western powers extraterritorial privileges in Japan.

Emperor Kōmei was an unusual figure among modern Japanese emperors: an active political agent who despised the foreign incursion and used his residual authority to delay and resist the shogunate's treaty negotiations, aligning himself with the *sonnō jōi* (尊王攘夷, "revere the emperor, expel the barbarians") movement. His death on January 30, 1867, at age 36, remains historically contested — the timing (shortly after the Chōshū domain's military revival) and the rapid deterioration from what was initially described as smallpox have prompted speculation, which scholars regard as unresolved.

Mutsuhito was enthroned at age 14 in January 1867. He formally adopted the era name **Meiji** (明治, "enlightened rule") on October 23, 1868, as part of the political settlement following the Boshin War.

## The Boshin War and the Meiji Restoration

The **Boshin War** (戊辰戦争, "War of the Year of the Earth Dragon") erupted between loyalist forces (the *Satchō* alliance of Satsuma and Chōshū domains) and shogunal loyalists in January 1868 following the December 9, 1867 decree proclaiming the restoration of imperial rule (*Ōsei Fukko*). The key battle, the **Battle of Toba-Fushimi** (January 27–30, 1868), south of Kyoto, decisively defeated Tokugawa forces. Subsequent campaigns pushed the conflict north: the **Battle of Ueno** (July 4, 1868) cleared Edo of holdouts, and the **Battle of Hakodate** (October 1868 – May 1869) ended the final Tokugawa-loyalist naval republic in Hokkaido.

The teenage Emperor Meiji rode into Edo — renamed **Tokyo** (東京, "Eastern Capital") in September 1868 — in a ceremonial procession in March 1869, formally transferring the imperial seat from Kyoto to Tokyo for the first time in 1,075 years.

### The Charter Oath (April 6, 1868)

The foundational policy statement of the Meiji government was the **Charter Oath** (*Gokajō no Goseimon*), proclaimed on April 6, 1868 (Gregorian), which articulated five principles:

1. Deliberative assemblies shall be established, and all state affairs shall be decided by public opinion.
2. All classes, high and low, shall unite in actively carrying out the administration of affairs of state.
3. All subjects, civil and military, as well as other people, shall be allowed to fulfill their just aspirations, so that there may be no discontent.
4. Evil customs of the past shall be broken off, and everything shall be based upon the just and equitable principles of nature.
5. Intelligence and learning shall be sought throughout the world, in order to strengthen the foundations of imperial rule.

The oath's fifth point authorized the sweeping program of Western knowledge-gathering that became the hallmark of Meiji foreign policy, climaxing in the Iwakura Mission.

## The Genrō and the Oligarchic System

The Meiji oligarchy was constituted primarily from lower-ranking samurai of the southwestern domains — men who had led the Restoration and now occupied the highest executive positions. The most influential were:

- **Iwakura Tomomi** (1825–1883): A court noble who had coordinated the Satsuma-Chōshū coalition and orchestrated the restoration edict. As head of the **Iwakura Mission** (1871–1873), he led 107 officials and 43 students on an 18-month tour of the United States, Britain, France, Belgium, Netherlands, Germany, Russia, and other nations, studying Western political institutions, industry, and culture. The Mission's official report (*Tokumei Zenken Taishi Bei-Ō Kairan Jikki*, 100 volumes) became a foundational reference for Japan's modernization planners.
- **Ōkubo Toshimichi** (1830–1878): The de facto prime minister during the critical 1870s, architect of land reform, industrialization policy, and the suppression of the Satsuma Rebellion. Assassinated in Tokyo on May 14, 1878, by a samurai opposed to his policies.
- **Yamagata Aritomo** (1838–1922): Father of the modern Japanese Army, twice Prime Minister, and the dominant figure in Japan's military and political life from 1870 until his death.
- **Itō Hirobumi** (1841–1909): Draftsman of the Meiji Constitution, Japan's first Prime Minister under the new constitutional system (December 1885 – April 1888), and Japan's first Resident-General of Korea. Assassinated in Harbin by the Korean independence activist An Jung-geun on October 26, 1909.
- **Inoue Kaoru** (1836–1915): Meiji's Foreign Minister and later Home Minister, architect of treaty revision efforts and Western-style diplomacy.
- **Mori Arinori** (1847–1889): Japan's first Minister of Education (1885–1889), who shaped the modern school system before his assassination by a nationalist on the day of the Meiji Constitution's promulgation.
- **Matsukata Masayoshi** (1835–1924): Finance Minister who engineered the 1881–1885 deflationary fiscal policy (*Matsukata Deflation*) to stabilize the currency and establish the Bank of Japan (1882).

Meiji himself was not a passive actor. He attended cabinet meetings, held private audiences with ministers, and maintained a hand in major decisions, particularly in military matters. Contemporary accounts describe him as politically astute, physically robust (an accomplished equestrian and swimmer), and intellectually serious. He was known for his penetrating questions and his ability to retain information from diverse fields.

## Major Transformations

### Abolition of the Feudal Order

The foundational transformation of the Meiji era was the dismantling of the Tokugawa feudal structure. Key measures:

- **1869**: The daimyo "voluntarily" returned their domains (*hanseki hōkan*) to the Emperor in exchange for retention as prefectural governors with reduced autonomy.
- **1871**: The domains were abolished and replaced with **prefectures** (*ken*) administered by centrally appointed governors (*ken-chiji*). The 300-odd domains became 72 prefectures (later consolidated to 47).
- **1873**: **Universal male conscription** (*chōhei-rei*) established a national army of peasant-soldiers, abolishing the samurai monopoly on military service.
- **1876**: The **Haitōrei** (廃刀令, Edict on Swords, March 28, 1876) prohibited even former samurai from wearing swords in public, ending the sword's role as a marker of social status. The cash stipends (*chitsurokushō*) of former samurai were commuted to lump-sum government bonds, effectively eliminating their privileged economic position.

### The Satsuma Rebellion (1877)

The most serious challenge to Meiji's authority came from the southwest. **Saigō Takamori** (1828–1877), the most celebrated samurai figure of the Restoration and a hero of the Boshin War, had grown disillusioned with the new government's rejection of his proposal for a punitive invasion of Korea (*Seikanron*) and its rapid dismantling of samurai privileges. Saigō returned to Satsuma in 1873, establishing private military academies that became rallying points for disaffected samurai.

The rebellion erupted in **February 1877** when the Meiji government attempted to confiscate weaponry from Satsuma arsenals. Saigō led approximately 40,000 Satsuma samurai in a march on Tokyo, beginning with the siege of **Kumamoto Castle** (February 22 – April 14, 1877), which the government garrison held despite heavy losses. Government conscript forces numbering approximately 65,000 men systematically defeated Saigō's army in a series of engagements from March to September 1877.

The final battle occurred at **Shiroyama** on September 24, 1877: Saigō and approximately 500 remaining followers faced an encircling government force of 30,000. Saigō was wounded early in the engagement and performed ritual suicide (*seppuku*); his head was then severed and buried by a loyal follower to preserve his honor. The rebellion's defeat at the cost of approximately 12,000 government and 20,000+ Satsuma casualties definitively established that no armed challenge to the Meiji order was possible.

Meiji reportedly wept upon learning of Saigō's death. The Emperor's ambivalence toward the man who had been both a great figure of the Restoration and a rebel against his government reflects the complexities of Meiji's own position. Saigō's rehabilitation — the government posthumously pardoned him in 1889, the same year the Constitution was promulgated — was managed carefully to honor the past without legitimizing armed resistance to the state.

### The Iwakura Mission (1871–1873)

The **Iwakura Mission** (岩倉使節団) departed Yokohama on December 23, 1871, and returned on September 13, 1873. The delegation of 107 senior officials — representing virtually the entire Meiji leadership — accompanied 43 scholarship students (including five girls, later famous as "the Five Girls Who Changed Japan"). The mission visited:

- **United States**: January–July 1872 (seven months). The delegation met President Ulysses Grant, toured Congress, and observed industrial sites in Boston, New York, and San Francisco.
- **Britain**: August–December 1872. Observed the House of Commons, toured manufacturing centers in Manchester, Birmingham, and Glasgow.
- **France**: January–March 1873.
- **Belgium, Netherlands, Germany, Russia, and others**: March–September 1873.

The mission discovered that treaty revision — one of its ostensible goals — was impossible without first establishing constitutional governance and Western-style legal institutions that Western powers would recognize as legitimate. This conclusion accelerated Japan's constitutional agenda. The delegation also returned with an understanding that industrialization, not military conquest, was the foundation of Western power.

### Industrialization and Economic Development

Under Meiji, Japan pursued industrialization with the guiding principle ***fukoku kyōhei*** (富国強兵, "enrich the nation, strengthen the military"). The government's approach was directed and interventionist:

- **Strategic industries**: The government established and operated model factories (*kangyō*) in silk reeling, cotton spinning, and cement. The **Tomioka Silk Mill** (Gunma Prefecture, 1872) used French machinery and French technical supervisors to train Japanese workers in mechanized silk reeling. Once the technology was transferred, many government enterprises were sold to private zaibatsu at discounted prices.
- **Foreign advisors** (*oyatoi gaikokujin*): At peak employment (1870s–1880s), over 3,000 foreign specialists worked in Japan — British engineers on railways and lighthouses, French military and legal advisors, German medical professors and army officers, American agricultural experimenters. They were generously compensated but always positioned as temporary transferors of knowledge.
- **Railways**: The first line, Shimbashi (Tokyo) to Yokohama, opened October 14, 1872, with Britain's **Edmund Morel** as chief engineer. By 1912, Japan had approximately 10,000 km of railway track, about half government-owned and half private.
- **Bank of Japan**: Founded October 10, 1882, with Yoshihara Shigetoshi as first governor. The Bank issued convertible bank notes and managed monetary policy, stabilizing Japan's financial system after the inflationary chaos of the early Meiji government-note emissions.
- **Silk and textile exports**: By the 1890s, Japan was the world's largest exporter of raw silk, earning the foreign exchange necessary to purchase military equipment, iron and steel, and industrial machinery.

The 1873 **land tax reform** (*chisokaisei*): replaced the Tokugawa system of rice-based taxation with a cash land tax set at **3% of assessed land value** (reduced to 2.5% in 1877 after rural protests). This created a predictable revenue stream but extracted cash from farmers who had to sell rice to pay, creating dependency on market prices and contributing to tenant-farmer poverty that became a long-term social problem.

### The Meiji Constitution (1889) and the Diet (1890)

Meiji sent Itō Hirobumi on a study tour of European constitutional systems in 1882–1883. Itō studied closely with the German constitutional jurists Rudolf von Gneist (Berlin) and Lorenz von Stein (Vienna), both of whom counseled a strong monarchical constitution rather than British-style parliamentary sovereignty. The resulting **Meiji Constitution** (*Dai Nippon Teikoku Kenpō*) was promulgated by the Emperor himself in a ceremony at the Imperial Palace on **February 11, 1889** (the 2,549th anniversary of the legendary founding of Japan by Emperor Jimmu, calculated by Meiji-era scholars).

The Constitution established:
- The Emperor as "sacred and inviolable" (*shinsei ni shite okasarezaru*), possessing supreme authority in legislation, military command, the appointment of ministers, and foreign affairs.
- A bicameral **Imperial Diet** (*Teikoku Gikai*): the **House of Peers** (*Kizokuin*), composed of members of the imperial family, hereditary peers, peers of the highest rank, and distinguished persons appointed by the Emperor; and the **House of Representatives** (*Shūgiin*), elected by a restricted male franchise initially set at males paying ¥15 or more in direct national taxes (approximately 1.1% of the total population, expanding to 2.2% by 1898).
- A Cabinet responsible to the Emperor rather than the Diet.
- Civil rights including freedom of speech, press, and assembly — subject to the limits of law.

The Diet's first session convened on **November 29, 1890**. The Constitution's ambiguities — particularly over cabinet responsibility and the relationship between the civil and military establishments — would generate chronic political conflict throughout the Meiji, Taishō, and early Shōwa eras.

### The Separation of Buddhism and Shinto

One of the Meiji government's earliest religious acts was the **Shinbutsu Bunri** (神仏分離, "separation of kami and Buddhas") edicts of March–April 1868. Over the preceding millennium, Buddhist temples and Shinto shrines had co-existed syncretically at the same sites, with Buddhist priests administering many shrine rituals. The edicts ordered the removal of Buddhist elements from Shinto shrines, the defrocking of shrine Buddhist priests, and the return of shrine precincts to purely Shinto administration.

The result was a wave of **Haibutsu Kishaku** (廃仏毀釈, "abolish Buddhism, destroy Śākyamuni") violence in some domains — particularly in Satsuma, Mito, and Tsuwano — where local officials interpreted the edicts aggressively, ordering the destruction of Buddhist statues, texts, and temple buildings. Thousands of temples were closed or razed. The violence subsided by 1870 but permanently altered the religious landscape. State Shinto, centering on the Emperor as a divine figure descended from the sun goddess Amaterasu, became the de facto state religion.

### Military Modernization and Imperial Expansion

**Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895)**: The war arose from Japanese and Chinese rivalry over influence in Korea. Japan's modern conscript army (modeled on Prussian organization and tactics) and Imperial Navy (modeled on the British Royal Navy) decisively defeated Qing forces in a series of engagements from August 1894 to February 1895. The **Treaty of Shimonoseki** (April 17, 1895) transferred to Japan:
- Taiwan (*Formosa*), which became Japan's first overseas colony and was administered as such until 1945.
- The Liaodong Peninsula in Manchuria.
- 200 million taels of silver (approximately ¥310 million, equivalent to several years of Japan's total tax revenue).
- Most-favored-nation trading rights in China.

The **Triple Intervention** (April 23, 1895): Russia, Germany, and France jointly "advised" Japan to return the Liaodong Peninsula, arguing that Japanese control would threaten peace in the region. Japan complied but deeply resented the humiliation — resentment that energized Japan's subsequent naval buildup specifically aimed at Russia.

**Russo-Japanese War (1904–1905)**: Tension over control of Manchuria and Korea culminated in Japan's surprise attack on the Russian naval squadron at Port Arthur on the night of February 8–9, 1904. The war involved the largest land battles since the Napoleonic era: the **Battle of Mukden** (February–March 1905) engaged approximately 600,000 men on both sides. The decisive naval engagement was the **Battle of Tsushima** (May 27–28, 1905), in which Admiral Tōgō Heihachirō's Combined Fleet annihilated Russia's Baltic Fleet — 6 battleships, 7 cruisers, and 5,000 men killed in 38 hours of combat, against Japanese losses of 3 torpedo boats and 117 dead. This remains the most one-sided naval victory in modern history.

The **Treaty of Portsmouth** (September 5, 1905), brokered by U.S. President Theodore Roosevelt (who received the Nobel Peace Prize for the mediation), gave Japan:
- Recognition of its paramount interests in Korea (effectively a Japanese protectorate, formally annexed in 1910).
- The southern half of Sakhalin Island (*Karafuto*), transferred from Russia.
- The Russian Kwantung Leasehold in Manchuria (Port Arthur and Dalian).

Japan's victory shocked Western observers and electrified Asian independence movements: for the first time in the modern era, a non-Western nation had defeated a European power in a major war.

### Educational and Cultural Modernization

The Meiji government enacted the **Gakusei** (学制, Educational System Edict) of August 1872, establishing a national framework of **8 university districts**, 32 middle-school districts within each, and 210 elementary-school districts within each middle-school district. By 1880, approximately 40% of school-age children attended school; by 1905, attendance exceeded 95%.

The **Rescript on Education** (*Kyōiku Chokugo*, October 30, 1890), issued in Meiji's name, defined the ethical foundation of Japanese education as loyalty to the imperial throne, filial piety, love of country, and martial virtue. Rescript copies were distributed to every school, where students were required to bow to them. The Rescript's Confucian-imperial synthesis — Western technical education combined with Japanese moral values centered on the Emperor — became the defining cultural formula of Meiji Japan.

Higher education expanded rapidly: Tokyo Imperial University (founded 1877) was joined by Kyoto Imperial University (1897), Tohoku Imperial University (1907), and Kyushu Imperial University (1910). Medical, technical, and military schools proliferated. Thousands of Japanese students were sent abroad — to Germany for medicine and natural science, to Britain for naval training, to France for law and art.

## Personal Life and Character

Emperor Meiji married **Empress Haruko** (later Empress Shōken, 昭憲皇太后; 1849–1914) on February 9, 1869. Haruko, of the Ichijō family of court nobles (*sekke*), was an active public figure who championed women's education and the Japanese Red Cross, adopted Western-influenced court dress for public functions, and composed thousands of *waka* poems. The couple had no surviving children together; five children of imperial consorts were adopted and raised in the imperial household. Emperor Meiji's son by a consort, Yoshihito, became his heir (later Emperor Taishō).

Meiji himself composed approximately **93,000 *waka*** and *tanka* poems over his lifetime — the largest output of any modern Japanese emperor. His verse ranged across seasonal nature imagery, the burdens and aspirations of imperial rule, and personal reflection. He published a selected collection, the *Meiji Tennō Gyoshū*, which remains in print. The poetry was composed in a classically correct style aligned with the Heian-period canon, reflecting his education, though critics note they also reflected a sincere personal engagement with the form.

He was known as an accomplished equestrian who regularly observed army maneuvers personally. He took a keen interest in Western military technology — visiting naval reviews, inspecting new artillery, and receiving briefings on foreign military organization — and in scientific developments including telegraphy, photography, and the early automobile.

## The Later Meiji Years and Death

By the first decade of the 20th century, most of Meiji's early collaborators had died, retired, or been assassinated. Itō Hirobumi was shot at Harbin railway station on October 26, 1909. The Emperor's health declined steadily from 1910, complicated by diabetes. He died on **July 30, 1912**, at age 59, in the Tokyo Imperial Palace, from uremia secondary to diabetes. His personal physician, Erwin von Baelz (one of the most influential of the *oyatoi gaikokujin*), confirmed the diagnosis.

The entire nation observed an intense period of mourning. His state funeral took place on **September 13, 1912**, at Aoyama Parade Ground in Tokyo — the largest state ceremony Japan had then staged. The novelist **Natsume Soseki** memorialized the moment in his novel *Kokoro* (1914). General **Nogi Maresuke** — the hero of the siege of Port Arthur in the Russo-Japanese War, who had served Meiji since the 1870s — committed ritual *junshi* (suicide in loyalty to a departed lord) with his wife on the night of the funeral, the last known *junshi* in Japanese history.

Meiji was enshrined as a *kami* of the Shinto pantheon at **Meiji Jingū** (Meiji Shrine), completed in November 1920 in the forested hills of Yoyogi in Tokyo (now surrounded by the city's urban fabric). The shrine, rebuilt after wartime damage in 1958, receives approximately 3 million visitors on the first three days of the New Year (*hatsumode*) alone, making it Japan's most visited shrine.

## Legacy and Historiographical Assessment

In Japan, Meiji is broadly honored as the wise sovereign who steered Japan through a dangerous era of potential colonization and emerged as a modern state with its sovereignty and cultural identity intact. The Meiji period is associated with a golden age of Japanese national development, and the institutions Meiji's era created — the constitutional framework (superseded in 1947), the railroad network, the university system, the Bank of Japan — remained foundations of Japanese life for much of the 20th century.

Critical historiography emphasizes several less celebratory aspects:

- **Militarism's roots**: The Meiji Constitution's positioning of the military as responsible to the Emperor rather than the civilian cabinet, and the glorification of martial sacrifice established in the Satsuma Rebellion's aftermath, created institutional precedents that made civilian control of the military structurally difficult and politically contested from the 1920s onward.
- **Imperial expansion**: The annexation of Taiwan, Korea, and Karafuto established Japan as a colonial power, with consequences for millions of Asian subjects who experienced Japanese rule as conquest and exploitation.
- **Haibutsu kishaku**: The government's religious policies, which aimed to assert Shinto supremacy over Buddhism, caused lasting institutional damage to Buddhist institutions and established the precedent of state interference in religious affairs.
- **Rural poverty**: While Japan industrialized rapidly, the land tax reform and the shift to a cash economy created chronic rural poverty that the Meiji-era social welfare infrastructure was entirely unequipped to address.

Historians debate whether the outcomes of the Shōwa era — ultranationalism, the Pacific War, the atomic bombings — were inherent to Meiji's modernization project or tragic departures from it. The Meiji era remains one of the most studied periods in modern history and a touchstone for scholars studying late industrialization, state capacity, and the relationship between tradition and modernity.
