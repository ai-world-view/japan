# Ashikaga Takauji

**Category**: People  
**Key figures**: Ashikaga Takauji (1305–1358); Emperor Go-Daigo; Kusunoki Masashige; Nitta Yoshisada; his brother Ashikaga Tadayoshi; his son Ashikaga Yoshiakira

## Summary

Ashikaga Takauji (born August 18, 1305, in Ashikaga, Shimotsuke Province; died June 7, 1358, in Kyoto) was a samurai general and warlord whose seizure of power from Emperor Go-Daigo in 1336 established the Ashikaga shogunate and inaugurated the Muromachi period (1336–1573). Born into the Ashikaga clan—a prestigious branch of the Minamoto lineage tracing descent from Minamoto no Yoshiie, with hereditary estates in the Kanto region of eastern Japan—Takauji rose within the service of the Kamakura shogunate before pivoting to support Emperor Go-Daigo's 1333 Kenmu Restoration. When Go-Daigo's reformist court failed to satisfy warrior expectations, Takauji led a military revolt, defeated the loyalist forces, and established a new military government in Kyoto, claiming the title *Sei-i Taishogun* in 1338—reclaiming the prerogative that [Minamoto no Yoritomo](../people/minamoto-no-yoritomo.md) had established 146 years earlier.

## Early Life and Rise Under the Kamakura Shogunate

Takauji's Ashikaga clan held extensive landholdings in Shimotsuke Province (present-day Tochigi Prefecture) and was recognized as one of the most prominent vassals of the Kamakura shogunate. Like many high-ranking military families of the era, the Ashikaga cultivated connections to the Hōjō regent clan—the true power behind the Kamakura shogunate—while maintaining their separate organizational identity and regional prestige. Takauji served Kamakura loyally through his twenties, receiving appointments and rewards in accordance with the *gokenin* (houseman) system that organized the shogunate's military vassal network.

The political context of Takauji's rise was the chronic weakness of the late Kamakura shogunate under Hōjō regents, who faced mounting fiscal pressure from the failed Mongol invasion defenses (1274, 1281), internal factional disputes, and the rising ambitions of the southern court aristocracy. Emperor Go-Daigo, ascending the Chrysanthemum Throne in 1318, harbored explicit ambitions to restore direct imperial rule by ending the shogunate entirely. After two failed plots (1324 and 1331), Go-Daigo was exiled to the Oki Islands in 1332, but his loyalist supporters—most notably Kusunoki Masashige in the Kinai region—continued to resist Kamakura authority.

## The Kenmu Restoration and Defection (1333–1336)

In 1333, the Kamakura shogunate dispatched Takauji westward with an army to suppress renewed loyalist uprisings in the Kinai region. Once he reached the western provinces, Takauji made the pivotal decision to switch sides: on June 5, 1333, he attacked the Rokuhara Tandai—the Kamakura shogunate's western administrative outpost in Kyoto—and opened the city to Go-Daigo's forces. Simultaneously, Nitta Yoshisada, another eastern warrior, turned against Kamakura itself; on July 4, 1333, Nitta's forces stormed the Kamakura shogunate's capital, and the last Hōjō regent, Takatoki, committed suicide, ending 148 years of Kamakura rule.

The resulting Kenmu Restoration (1333–1336) was Go-Daigo's attempt to restore direct imperial governance. The emperor abolished the shogunal and regent institutions, replacing them with imperial bureaus staffed by court nobles. For warriors like Takauji, the Restoration's rewards were inadequate: land grants were slow, confirmations of existing holdings were uncertain, and court nobles received precedence over military men in administrative appointments. Within two years, nearly all warrior factions had grown disillusioned. Takauji's break with Go-Daigo began in late 1335, when he suppressed a Hōjō loyalist uprising in the east (Nakasendai Disturbance) and distributed land rewards to his followers without imperial authorization—an act of insubordination that Go-Daigo declared treasonous.

## Civil War and Establishment of the Muromachi Shogunate (1336–1338)

In late 1335 Takauji marched east and defeated Nitta Yoshisada at the Battle of Takenoshita (December 1335), then advanced on and occupied Kyoto in early 1336. Loyalist reinforcements under Kitabatake Akiie and Nitta Yoshisada soon drove him from the capital, forcing him to retreat by sea to Kyushu. There he recovered rapidly, receiving reinforcements from local Kyushu warlords. Returning eastward by sea, he landed at Hyogo (present-day Kobe) and won the decisive Battle of Minatogawa on July 4, 1336, against a combined loyalist army commanded by Nitta Yoshisada and Kusunoki Masashige. Kusunoki, the most celebrated loyalist commander, died in this battle—traditionally by ritual suicide after his defeat—and became a posthumous icon of loyalty, later idealized in Meiji-era nationalist mythology.

With Kyoto secured, Takauji installed a new emperor (Kōmyō, of the Northern Court) and compelled Go-Daigo to surrender the imperial regalia. Go-Daigo later escaped to Yoshino, south of Nara, where he established the Southern Court (*Nanchō*) and declared his surrender void. The resulting Nanboku-chō (Northern and Southern Courts) period (1336–1392) saw sustained conflict between Ashikaga-backed Northern Court emperors and the Southern Court, which retained legitimacy claims based on possession of the genuine regalia. On November 7, 1336, Takauji issued the *Kenmu Shikimoku*—a seventeen-article administrative code modeled on the Kamakura shogunate's *Goseibai Shikimoku*—establishing foundational governance principles for the new regime. In 1338, a Northern Court imperial decree formally appointed him *Sei-i Taishogun*, completing the institutionalization of the Muromachi shogunate.

## Co-Governance with Tadayoshi and Internal Conflict

Unlike previous shogunates, Takauji's regime from the outset featured a bifurcated power structure. His younger brother **Ashikaga Tadayoshi** (1306–1352) handled most administrative and judicial governance—managing the shogunate's legal apparatus, land distribution (*ando*), and inter-domain disputes—while Takauji concentrated on military command. This division proved unstable. Takauji's personal retainer corps (led by his powerful chief administrator Kō no Moronao) and Tadayoshi's administrative faction developed into rival power centers, each cultivating separate warrior clienteles.

Tensions escalated into open conflict in the Kannō Disturbance (1349–1352). In 1349, Tadayoshi was forced to retire to a monastery. In 1351, Takauji briefly made common cause with the Southern Court against his own followers—a bizarre reversal that historians call the *Kannō no jōran* ("disturbance of the Kannō era"). Tadayoshi was subsequently captured and died in March 1352 under suspicious circumstances, widely believed to have been poisoned on Takauji's orders. The fratricidal conflict demonstrated the extreme instability of Ashikaga central authority in this formative period, a weakness that would recur throughout the dynasty.

## Administrative and Cultural Legacy

Takauji established the Muromachi shogunate in Kyoto's Muromachi district (giving the period its name), keeping the military government in the same city as the imperial court—a marked departure from Yoritomo's deliberately distant Kamakura administration. This proximity fostered the distinctive blend of courtly refinement and warrior culture that characterized Muromachi civilization. Takauji was a committed patron of Rinzai Zen Buddhism: he established the network of *Gozan* ("Five Mountains") Zen temples—five premier monasteries each in Kyoto and Kamakura—modeled on Song-dynasty Chinese precedents, providing institutional anchors for Zen scholarship, art, and literary culture. He commissioned the construction of the Tenryū-ji temple (1339) in Kyoto's Arashiyama district under the supervision of the Zen master Musō Soseki, funding it partly through revenues from a licensed trade ship (*Tenryū-ji bune*) dispatched to Ming-dynasty China in 1342.

Takauji was also personally accomplished in poetry, and his patronage created an atmosphere in which cultural refinement and martial authority reinforced each other—a synthesis his successors would develop more fully. The cultural institutions his regime established—the Gozan literary tradition, the *dōbōshū* system of cultural advisors—eventually produced the Higashiyama cultural flowering of Ashikaga Yoshimasa's era (mid-15th century), which yielded Noh theater, ink painting, the tea ceremony, and garden design traditions including Ryoan-ji's famous rock garden.

## Death and Succession

Ashikaga Takauji died on June 7, 1358, likely of a skin disease or oral cancer—contemporary records describe a progressive deterioration of his health in his final years. He was buried at Tōji-in temple in Kyoto, which became the official mortuary temple of the Ashikaga shoguns. He was succeeded by his son **Ashikaga Yoshiakira** (1330–1367), who ruled as the second shogun. The dynasty continued for fifteen shoguns in total, lasting until 1573, when Oda Nobunaga expelled the last Ashikaga shogun, Yoshiaki, from Kyoto.

## Significance

Ashikaga Takauji's establishment of the Muromachi shogunate perpetuated the shogunal system as Japan's dominant political form and prevented the restoration of centralized imperial authority—a choice with consequences lasting into the modern era. By defeating Emperor Go-Daigo's Kenmu Restoration, he reinforced the principle that military force, rather than imperial mandate alone, determined political supremacy, a lesson that future samurai leaders—including [Tokugawa Ieyasu](../people/tokugawa-ieyasu.md) three centuries later—would repeatedly act upon. The long conflict between his Northern Court and Go-Daigo's Southern Court entrenched regional warrior networks and accelerated the decentralization of power, inadvertently preparing the conditions for the Sengoku era's full-scale civil warfare.

Culturally, Takauji's court became a seedbed of the distinctly Japanese arts that would flourish under his successors: Noh theater, ink painting (*sumi-e*), linked verse (*renga*), the tea ceremony, and Zen-influenced garden design emerged during Ashikaga patronage and profoundly shaped the aesthetics of traditional Japanese culture. His shogunate thus represents a pivotal transition: the defeat of imperial restoration, the entrenchment of warrior rule, and the flowering of the artistic traditions that define Japan's classical cultural identity.
