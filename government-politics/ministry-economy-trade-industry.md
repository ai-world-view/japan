# Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI)

**Category**: Government & Politics
**Key figures**: Chalmers Johnson, Noboru Gotoh, Koichi Hamada, Yasuhisa Shiozaki

## Summary

The Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI; 経済産業省, *Keizai Sangyō Shō*) is Japan's principal government body responsible for economic policy, industrial development, trade, and resource management. METI originated as the Ministry of International Trade and Industry (MITI; 通商産業省, *Tsūshō Sangyō Shō*), established on June 4, 1949, during the Allied occupation under the Shoup Tax Mission recommendations and broader economic reorganization. In January 2001, MITI was reorganized into METI following a sweeping administrative reform of Japan's national government that merged the Small and Medium Enterprise Agency and restructured bureau jurisdictions. METI currently operates with approximately 2,300 civil servants in Tokyo and maintains offices throughout Japan and abroad, including a network of industrial promotion offices and overseas posts at embassies.

The ministry's mandate encompasses a vast portfolio: industrial policy and strategic sector development; trade negotiations and multilateral agreements (WTO, bilateral FTAs/EPAs); energy security and resource management (oil, natural gas, rare-earth minerals, nuclear energy); support for small and medium enterprises (SMEs), which constitute over 99% of Japanese businesses; intellectual property and technology transfer; environmental standards and industrial efficiency; and the regulation of certain industries. METI maintains specialized bureaus devoted to manufacturing industries, international trade, coal and energy policy, and resource security, as well as affiliated agencies including the Agency for Natural Resources and Energy, the Patent Office, and the Nuclear and Industrial Safety Agency (consolidated into the Nuclear Regulation Authority after 2012).

## Institutional History

### MITI's Founding and Early Mandate (1949–1955)

MITI was established on June 4, 1949, by merging the former Ministry of Commerce and Industry (Shōkōshō, established 1925) with the Board of Trade (Bōeki-chō), which had managed Japan's foreign trade under Allied occupation. The founding context was the Dodge Line austerity program (1949)—a stringent stabilization plan imposed by American advisor Joseph Dodge that cut government spending, balanced the budget, and introduced a fixed exchange rate of ¥360 per US dollar. The Dodge Line caused a sharp deflationary recession that eliminated thousands of weaker firms but rationalized Japan's industrial base, setting conditions for MITI's subsequent guidance role.

From its earliest years, MITI operated under a distinctive model of "administrative guidance" (*gyōseishidō*)—informal directives, recommendations, and consultations through which the ministry exercised influence over private firms without formal legal authority. Because administrative guidance operated outside the formal regulatory framework, it was difficult for courts or legislators to challenge, and firms typically complied to maintain relationships with the ministry controlling their access to foreign exchange allocations, import licenses, and government financing. This flexibility gave MITI an institutional nimbleness that purely regulatory agencies lacked.

### The Developmental State and Industrial Policy (1950s–1970s)

MITI's central contribution to Japan's [postwar recovery and economic growth](../economy/postwar-recovery-growth.md) was its systematic promotion of strategic industries through coordinated policies:

**Foreign Exchange Control**: Under the Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Control Law (1949), MITI controlled Japan's allocation of scarce foreign exchange, enabling it to decide which industries could import foreign technology and machinery. Industries targeted for promotion received priority access; sectors deemed non-strategic were denied. This concentration of resources accelerated technology transfer and capacity building in chosen sectors.

**The Basic Materials Era (1950s)**: MITI's early priorities were the "three priority industries" of coal, steel, and fertilizer—essential to Japan's industrial restart and food production. The ministry channeled Reconstruction Finance Bank (RFB) loans and later Japan Development Bank financing to these sectors, enabling rapid capacity expansion. Japan's crude steel output grew from approximately 5 million tons in 1952 to 22 million tons in 1960—a fourfold increase in eight years.

**Transition to Knowledge Industries (1960s–1970s)**: As Japan's comparative advantage shifted away from low-wage manufacturing, MITI recalibrated its strategy toward capital-intensive, technology-intensive industries. The Petrochemical Industry Act (1955) and subsequent special measures laws provided tax incentives, accelerated depreciation, and rationalization cartels for automobiles, machine tools, computers, and consumer electronics. MITI actively encouraged technology licensing agreements with Western firms (particularly American and European), acquiring production know-how for domestic industries at a fraction of original R&D costs. Under MITI guidance, Japanese automakers obtained technology licenses from US and European manufacturers in the 1950s, then systematically improved upon them—enabling Toyota, Nissan, and Honda to develop competitive domestic models by the mid-1960s. See [Technology & Manufacturing](../economy/technology-manufacturing.md).

**The DRAM and Semiconductor Strategy**: One of MITI's most celebrated industrial policy successes was the VLSI (Very Large Scale Integration) Technology Research Association (1976–1980), a government-sponsored R&D consortium of Fujitsu, Hitachi, Mitsubishi, NEC, and Toshiba, with MITI providing approximately ¥30 billion in direct subsidies. The VLSI project produced over 1,000 patents and enabled Japanese semiconductor manufacturers to achieve parity with, and then surpass, American DRAM producers in yield, defect rates, and cost by the early 1980s. By 1986, Japanese firms held approximately 80% of the global DRAM market—triggering intense US–Japan trade friction and the US–Japan Semiconductor Agreement of 1986 (establishing minimum price floors and a 20% foreign market share target in Japan). This trade confrontation marked the high-water point of MITI's developmental state model and the beginning of US pressure for deregulation.

**The Auto VER (Voluntary Export Restraints)**: In 1981, responding to Congressional pressure for import quotas on Japanese cars, MITI negotiated Voluntary Export Restraints (VERs) with the US under which Japan would limit its auto exports to the US to 1.68 million units per year (later raised). While the VERs reduced the volume of Japanese car exports, they paradoxically increased their value, as Japanese manufacturers shifted toward higher-margin vehicles (introducing luxury brands such as Lexus and Acura) and accelerated investment in US-based "transplant" factories (Honda in Ohio, 1982; Nissan in Tennessee, 1983; Toyota in Kentucky, 1988), partially circumventing the restrictions. The VER episode illustrated the contradictions of managed trade as an industrial policy tool.

### The Chalmers Johnson Thesis

The analysis of MITI's role was crystallized in the political scientist Chalmers Johnson's landmark work *MITI and the Japanese Miracle: The Growth of Industrial Policy, 1925–1975* (Stanford University Press, 1982). Johnson argued that Japan represented a "developmental state"—a capitalist state in which a bureaucratic elite of economic technocrats (epitomized by MITI) prioritized national economic development over consumption and welfare, using industrial policy to guide market outcomes toward strategic goals. Johnson's thesis—that Japan's economic success was neither pure free-market capitalism nor Soviet-style command planning but a distinct third model—became one of the most influential frameworks in political economy and development studies, spawning a large literature on developmental states in East Asia (South Korea's POSCO and heavy industry drive, Taiwan's ITRI, Singapore's Economic Development Board all drew comparisons). Johnson's thesis was critiqued and qualified by subsequent researchers (Marcus Noland, Daniel Okimoto, Edward Lincoln) who pointed to MITI failures (the computer industry's limited success against IBM, the supercomputer project's limited commercial impact) and the role of firms in driving their own growth, but it remains the foundational framework for understanding MITI's historical role.

### Bubble Era and Lost Decades (1980s–1990s)

During the [1980s Bubble Economy](../economy/bubble-economy.md), MITI's influence was both extended and complicated. The ministry remained central to Japan's export drive in automobiles, electronics, and precision machinery, but it had less control over the financial excesses driving asset inflation—a domain dominated by the Ministry of Finance and the [Bank of Japan](../economy/bank-of-japan.md). After the bubble's collapse in 1990–91, MITI shifted roles: it became an architect of industrial restructuring, supporting mergers in declining sectors (steel, petrochemicals, shipbuilding), administering small business bailout programs, and promoting deregulation in response to international pressure (particularly under US–Japan Structural Impediments Initiative talks, 1989–1990).

During the [Lost Decade](../economy/lost-decade.md), MITI and its successor METI struggled to identify new growth vectors comparable to the industrial successes of earlier decades. The ministry launched several technology initiatives (Fifth Generation Computer Systems project, the Sigma Project for software productivity) that proved costly failures, contributing to scholarly reassessment of MITI's track record. These setbacks reinforced arguments that industrial policy works best in "catching up" phases—identifying and adopting known technologies—and becomes less effective at the technological frontier.

### METI's Formation (2001) and Contemporary Mandate

The January 2001 reorganization of Japan's national government—the largest such restructuring since 1945—merged MITI with the Agency of Natural Resources and Energy (elevated to bureau status within METI) and the Small and Medium Enterprise Agency, creating METI with an expanded portfolio. The reorganization also consolidated several public corporations and regulatory functions, reducing agency proliferation in line with the administrative reform agenda of Prime Minister Hashimoto Ryūtarō.

Since 2001, METI's priorities have evolved in response to Japan's economic challenges and global shifts:

**Energy Security**: Following the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake and Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster, METI's energy policy underwent a complete reset. Japan's 50 nuclear reactors, which had provided approximately 30% of electricity generation, were progressively shut down for safety reassessment; by 2012, all were offline for the first time since 1970. METI managed the resulting energy gap through increased LNG imports (at significant cost), emergency conservation measures, and accelerated renewable energy development. METI introduced the Feed-in Tariff (FIT) system in July 2012, requiring utilities to purchase renewable electricity at guaranteed prices—leading to a rapid expansion of solar PV capacity (from approximately 5 GW in 2012 to over 70 GW in 2022, making Japan the third-largest solar market globally). METI's 2030 energy mix targets (as of 2023) call for renewables at 36–38%, nuclear at approximately 20–22% (if reactors restart), LNG at 20%, and coal at 19%—a mix that climate advocates criticize as insufficiently ambitious.

**Green Innovation Strategy**: In 2021, METI released the Green Growth Strategy (*Gurīn Seichō Senryaku*), a 14-sector industrial policy blueprint for Japan's decarbonization through 2050, targeting offshore wind (45 GW by 2040), green hydrogen, next-generation solar, storage batteries, and decarbonized steel, with ¥2 trillion ($15 billion) in annual fiscal support mechanisms. The strategy represented METI's most ambitious "strategic sector" initiative since the VLSI project, explicitly modeled on developmental state logic applied to clean energy.

**Semiconductor Renaissance**: The global semiconductor supply disruption of 2020–2021 prompted Japan to reassert a semiconductor industrial policy after two decades of relative decline. METI facilitated and subsidized the TSMC Japan factory (JASM 1, Kumamoto, operational 2024) with approximately ¥476 billion in government support, and announced a second TSMC plant (JASM 2) for 6nm-class chips. In 2022, METI organized a domestic chipmaker consortium, Rapidus Corporation, with a ¥700 billion government investment toward manufacturing 2nm-class semiconductors by 2027 in Chitose, Hokkaido—an extraordinarily ambitious target that would require Japan to leapfrog two generations of semiconductor technology. Critics have questioned the technical feasibility timeline, but the initiative reflects METI's return to bold industrial policy bets. See [Kyushu](../geography/kyushu.md) for the regional industrial context.

**Digital Transformation (DX)**: METI has led Japan's national Digital Transformation strategy, responding to Japan's persistent ranking near the bottom of advanced economies in enterprise digitization indices. The ministry's DX Report (2018, updated 2022) identified the "2025 Digital Cliff" (*2025-nen no gake*) — the risk that Japan's enterprises relying on obsolete legacy IT systems face catastrophic competitive disadvantage as system maintenance costs consume IT budgets and systems become unmaintainable. METI has used regulatory guidance, certification systems, and investment incentives to accelerate cloud migration, API standardization, and enterprise software modernization.

## Organization and Structure

METI's headquarters are in Kasumigaseki, the Tokyo government district, in proximity to other key ministries. Its major bureaus include:

- **Trade Policy Bureau**: Negotiates and administers Japan's bilateral and multilateral trade agreements, including the Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP), the Japan-EU EPA (effective 2019), and the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP, effective 2022)
- **Industrial Science and Technology Policy and Environment Bureau**: Manages R&D policy, intellectual property systems, and environmental standards for industry
- **Manufacturing Industries Bureau**: Covers automobiles, machinery, aviation/space, and chemical industries
- **Commerce and Service Industry Policy Group**: Covers retail, services, and the digital economy
- **Agency for Natural Resources and Energy**: Manages energy security, resource diplomacy, and nuclear regulation coordination
- **Small and Medium Enterprise Agency (Chushō Kigyōchō)**: Administers the most comprehensive SME support system among advanced economies, covering financing, business continuity, business succession, and startup promotion

The Minister of Economy, Trade and Industry is a Cabinet-level political appointment; the Vice-Minister (Jimujikan) is the career bureaucrat heading the ministry's administrative structure. METI's career bureaucrats traditionally graduate from top Japanese universities (predominately the University of Tokyo Law Faculty) and follow generalist career paths across the ministry's bureaus, rotating assignments every one to two years in accordance with Japanese bureaucratic tradition—a practice that builds broad policy knowledge while limiting deep specialization.

## *Keiretsu* Coordination and the Developmental State Model

A central mechanism of MITI's influence was its coordination with Japan's *keiretsu* (interlocking corporate family groups)—horizontal conglomerates such as Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo, Fuyo, Sanwa, and DKB organized around main banks, trading companies (*sōgō shōsha*), and manufacturers. The *keiretsu* structure, which replaced the prewar *zaibatsu* (dissolved by Allied occupation authorities in 1945–1950), provided stable cross-shareholding relationships and main-bank financing that enabled long-term industrial investment—sometimes at the cost of shareholder returns. MITI leveraged these relationships to channel its industrial policy recommendations through the corporate groups' internal coordination mechanisms, effectively translating ministerial guidance into coordinated private-sector action.

The *sōgō shōsha* (integrated trading companies)—Mitsubishi Corporation, Mitsui & Co., Sumitomo Corporation, Itochu, Marubeni, and others—served as crucial intermediaries in Japan's global trade networks, securing resource contracts, arranging export financing, and managing distribution channels across multiple industries simultaneously. METI maintained close relationships with these firms, which provided both market intelligence and execution capacity for the ministry's trade and resource security objectives. The trading companies' role declined somewhat from the 1980s onward as manufacturers developed their own international sales and procurement operations, but they remained significant actors, particularly in resource-commodity markets and infrastructure projects in developing economies. See [Liberal Democratic Party](liberal-democratic-party.md) for the political dimensions of MITI/METI's relationships with business.
