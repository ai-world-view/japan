# Supreme Court of Japan

**Category**: Government & Politics  
**Key figures**: Chief Justice Tanaka Kōtarō, Chief Justice Yokota Kisaburō, constitutional law scholars, the Justices' Conference

## Summary

The Supreme Court of Japan (*Saikō Saibansho*) is the nation's highest judicial authority and sole constitutional court, established under the **1947 Constitution** as the court of last resort for all civil, criminal, and administrative cases. Prior to 1947, Japan operated under the Meiji Constitution (1890), which created courts but subordinated them to the executive branch under the principle that justice was administered in the Emperor's name; the Great Court of Cassation (*Daishin'in*) could only review legal questions, not constitutionality. The 1947 constitutional framework fundamentally reordered the judiciary's role by vesting judicial power exclusively in courts and creating an independent Supreme Court with explicit authority under **Article 81** to review the constitutionality of laws, executive actions, and administrative proceedings. The Court comprises a **Chief Justice** and **fourteen associate justices**, appointed by the Emperor on the advice of the Cabinet and subject to a once-in-office popular referendum after each general election—a unique democratic check on judicial appointments known as the "people's review" (*kokumin shinsa*).

Japan's Supreme Court has struck down statutory provisions as unconstitutional in only about a dozen cases across more than seven decades of operation—a record of exceptional restraint compared with constitutional courts elsewhere. This restraint reflects both a traditionally deferential judicial posture toward the legislative and executive branches and the composition practice of appointing justices from career bureaucratic, prosecutorial, and diplomatic backgrounds alongside lawyers and academics. Landmark cases have nonetheless addressed fundamental constitutional questions about the pacifist **Article 9**, the scope of emergency powers, equality of citizens regardless of gender or birth legitimacy, and the balance between free expression and public order. The 1959 *Sunakawa* case upheld US military bases on Japanese soil; later reinterpretations under Prime Minister Abe in 2014 expanded the permissible scope of "collective self-defense" without formal constitutional amendment.

## Structure and Composition

The Supreme Court sits in **Tokyo** and is divided into three **Petty Benches** (*Shō Hōtei*) of five justices each, which handle the bulk of cases, and a **Grand Bench** (*Dai Hōtei*) of all fifteen justices convened for constitutionally significant matters or when a Petty Bench seeks to depart from precedent.

By established practice, the fifteen seats are allocated roughly as follows:
- **6 justices** from the career judiciary (drawn from the court system's judge track)
- **4 justices** from the legal profession (attorneys with private practice backgrounds)
- **2 justices** from the public prosecutor's office (*kensatsu*)
- **2 justices** from academia (law professors)
- **1 justice** from the diplomatic or administrative services

This allocation is not constitutionally mandated but has been maintained by convention since the Court's founding. The Chief Justice is designated by the Cabinet and formally appointed by the Emperor. All associate justices are appointed by the Cabinet without any parliamentary confirmation hearing, a process frequently criticized as lacking transparency.

Justices serve until age **70** (the mandatory retirement age) and are not subject to unlimited terms; a justice appointed at age 65 serves only five years. The average tenure on the Court is approximately six to seven years.

## Popular Review (*Kokumin Shinsa*)

Article 79 of the Constitution requires that each Supreme Court justice face a **popular review** by the electorate at the first general election of the House of Representatives following their appointment, and at subsequent elections held more than ten years later. Voters mark a ballot indicating whether they wish to dismiss any justice; a majority in favor of dismissal removes that justice from office. In practice, **no justice has ever been dismissed** through this mechanism since 1947. Voter participation in the review is typically low, and information about individual justices is limited; critics argue the system functions more as symbolic accountability than substantive democratic oversight. Scholarly proposals for pre-appointment hearings in the Diet have circulated but not been enacted.

## Constitutional Review Power (Article 81)

Article 81 of the 1947 Constitution explicitly vests in the Supreme Court "the power to determine whether any law, order, regulation, or official act is constitutional." Unlike Germany's Constitutional Court or the United States Supreme Court—both of which have frequently exercised the power to invalidate legislation—Japan's Supreme Court has exercised this power with extreme restraint, striking down statutory provisions in only around a dozen cases since 1947, beginning with the 1973 patricide ruling and extending through the 2013 inheritance and 2023 gender-recognition decisions.

Constitutional law scholars offer several explanations for this restraint:
- **Appointment pipeline**: Career judges who reach the Supreme Court have been socialized in a judicial culture emphasizing deference to legislative and executive judgment.
- **Majoritarian theory**: The Court has explicitly embraced a majoritarian reading of its role, holding that constitutional interpretation is primarily a political question reserved to the Diet and Cabinet.
- **Political context**: The LDP's near-continuous majority since 1955 has meant the political branches are largely controlled by a single party; judicial review might be seen as anti-democratic interference with repeated electoral mandates.
- **Article 9 sensitivity**: Any aggressive constitutional review could destabilize the carefully managed ambiguity around Article 9 and the Self-Defense Forces, which depends on executive interpretation rather than clear judicial holding.

## Landmark Cases

**Sunakawa case (*Sunakawa Jiken*, 1959)**: A Grand Bench ruling arising from protests against the expansion of the US Tachikawa Air Base. Defendants arrested under the US-Japan Security Treaty's criminal jurisdiction provisions argued the Treaty was unconstitutional under Article 9. Chief Justice Tanaka Kōtarō's majority opinion held that the constitutionality of the Security Treaty was a "highly political question" beyond the Court's ordinary jurisdiction—an explicit adoption of the political question doctrine. The ruling was significant for sanctioning US military presence and became the foundational precedent for deference on security matters.

**Patricide case (*Sonzoku Satsujin Jiken*, 1973)**: The Grand Bench ruled that Penal Code Article 200, which prescribed heavier punishment for killing a parent than for ordinary homicide, violated the **Article 14** guarantee of equality under the law. This was the first instance in which the Court held a specific statutory provision facially unconstitutional. The provision was eventually removed from the Penal Code in 1995.

**Electoral apportionment cases**: A series of rulings from the 1970s onward found that extreme malapportionment in Diet electoral districts (disparities of 5:1 or more between voter-to-seat ratios in rural versus urban constituencies) violated the equal-protection principle of Article 14. However, the Court stopped short of voiding election results, instead declaring elections "in a state of unconstitutionality" while allowing them to stand—a characteristically cautious formulation.

**Unmarried child inheritance case (2013)**: In a Grand Bench ruling, the Court held unconstitutional a Civil Code provision granting children born out of wedlock only half the inheritance share of children born within marriage, finding it violated Article 14 equality guarantees. The Diet amended the Civil Code within months.

**Transgender name-change requirement (2023)**: A Grand Bench ruling held that requiring transgender individuals to undergo surgical sterilization as a condition of legal gender change was unconstitutional, applying an evolving reading of dignity and personal autonomy under Article 13.

## Article 9 and the Self-Defense Forces

Article 9 of the 1947 Constitution famously renounces war as a sovereign right and prohibits maintenance of "war potential." The Supreme Court has never ruled directly on the constitutionality of the **Self-Defense Forces** (established 1954), leaving the question formally open while the executive branch maintains its own interpretation. The Court's *Naganuma Nike Missile Base* case (1973, Sapporo District Court level) briefly saw a lower court rule the SDF unconstitutional—a ruling quickly reversed on jurisdictional grounds by the Sapporo High Court. The Supreme Court affirmed the High Court's reversal without addressing the constitutionality question.

Prime Minister Abe's 2015 reinterpretation—allowing "collective self-defense" (coming to the aid of an ally under attack) through Cabinet resolution rather than constitutional amendment—relied on executive interpretation precisely because the Supreme Court had never established a constraining constitutional holding. The Court's continued silence on Article 9 thus enables considerable executive flexibility in security policy.

## Administrative and Criminal Jurisdiction

Beyond constitutional review, the Supreme Court functions as the court of final appeal in all criminal and civil matters. It reviews **jōkoku** appeals (legal error) and **tokubetsu jōkoku** appeals (grave injustice). The Court accepts a small fraction of petitions: it receives approximately **2,000–3,000** petitions annually and issues full written opinions in roughly **100–150** cases. The overwhelming majority are dismissed as not meeting the criteria for review.

Japan's **criminal justice system** features a conviction rate exceeding **99%**, largely because prosecutors decline to bring cases they are not certain to win. The Supreme Court has generally upheld this system, including practices such as extended pre-charge detention (*daiyō kangoku*—"substitute prisons" allowing police to hold suspects in police cells for up to 23 days before formal indictment) that international human rights bodies have criticized.

## Significance

The Supreme Court embodies Japan's transition from imperial autocracy to democratic governance under rule of law and represents a key institutional check on executive power—though its record shows significant restraint in exercising that power. The Court's interpretation of Article 9 and the constitutionality of the Self-Defense Forces has direct implications for Japan's defense posture and alliance relationships, particularly with the United States. Recent rulings on inheritance equality, transgender rights, and electoral apportionment suggest the Court is gradually, if cautiously, expanding its substantive rights jurisprudence. As Japan confronts demographic decline, generational shifts in constitutional values, and questions about imperial succession and gender equality, the Court's future decisions on marriage law, succession, and constitutional amendment procedures will shape the nation's civic identity and governance.
