# 1980s Bubble Economy

**Category**: Economy
**Key figures**: Yasushi Mieno (Bank of Japan Governor), Finance Minister Kiichi Miyazawa, Prime Minister Nakasone Yasuhiro, Satoshi Sumita (preceding Bank of Japan Governor)

## Summary

Japan's "Bubble Economy" (バブル景気) of the 1980s and early 1990s represented the most dramatic asset-price inflation in modern economic history. Following Japan's emergence as the world's second-largest economy in the late 1970s, the [Bank of Japan](../economy/bank-of-japan.md) pursued accommodative monetary policy from 1986 to 1989, maintaining historically low interest rates to counter the yen's sharp appreciation after the Plaza Accord of 1985. This liquidity explosion fueled unprecedented speculation in real estate and stock markets, briefly making Japan's financial system the largest in the world by capitalization.

## Origins: The Plaza Accord and Cheap Money

The bubble's roots trace to September 22, 1985, when finance ministers from the G5 nations (the United States, Japan, West Germany, France, and the United Kingdom) met at the Plaza Hotel in New York City and agreed to coordinated intervention to depreciate the US dollar. The Plaza Accord caused the Japanese yen to appreciate sharply—from roughly ¥240 per US dollar in 1985 to ¥128 by 1988. This rapid yen appreciation ("endaka," 円高) compressed Japanese exporters' profit margins and triggered fears of recession.

In response, the Bank of Japan—under Governor Satoshi Sumita—progressively cut the official discount rate from 5.0% in January 1986 to a postwar low of **2.5%** in February 1987, where it was held for an unprecedented two-and-a-half years. The Ministry of Finance simultaneously implemented fiscal stimulus. Awash in cheap credit, Japanese banks, corporations, and individuals redirected capital from productive investment into asset speculation, initiating a feedback loop of rising prices and expanded borrowing against inflated collateral.

## Asset Inflation: Stocks and Real Estate

Between 1985 and 1989, Japanese equity markets experienced a vertical rise. The **Nikkei 225** index surged from approximately 11,000 in 1983 to a historic peak of **38,915.87 on December 29, 1989**—a level not surpassed again until March 2024, a gap of 34 years. At peak valuation, Japan's stock market capitalization constituted roughly 40% of global equity market value, despite Japan producing only about 15% of world GDP.

Real estate inflation was even more extreme. By 1989–1990, the total assessed value of Japanese land was estimated at roughly **four times the value of all US land**—though the United States is 25 times Japan's geographic size. Commercial land in the Ginza district of central Tokyo reportedly reached prices of ¥30 million (approximately $240,000 at the time) per square meter. Residential land in central Tokyo's best neighborhoods traded at prices that would require a household to save its entire income for 70–100 years to purchase a modest home. Golf club memberships at prestigious clubs sold for over ¥300 million (approximately $2 million), and the Imperial Palace grounds in Tokyo were reportedly worth more on paper than all the real estate in California.

Banks and their affiliated non-bank lending subsidiaries (jusen companies, 住専) competed aggressively to extend mortgage and commercial real estate loans against this inflated collateral. Loan-to-value ratios loosened, and credit reached borrowers of declining creditworthiness.

## Monetary Tightening and Collapse

The Bank of Japan began reversing course on May 31, 1989, when Governor Satoshi Sumita raised the official discount rate for the first time in over two years. His successor **Yasushi Mieno**, who took office in December 1989 and became known as the "bubble buster," accelerated the tightening. In all, the official discount rate was raised five times in rapid succession:

| Date | Rate |
|------|------|
| May 1989 | 3.25% |
| October 1989 | 3.75% |
| December 1989 | 4.25% |
| March 1990 | 5.25% |
| August 1990 | 6.00% |

Asset prices collapsed almost immediately. The Nikkei 225 fell **38%** in 1990 alone and declined a further 10% into 1992, losing approximately ¥300 trillion in market capitalization. Real estate prices in Tokyo and Osaka fell **70–80%** from their peaks, a decline that proved slow and grinding rather than sudden—Japan's land market is far less liquid than equities, so losses compounded over the entire decade of the 1990s.

## The Banking Crisis

The collapse of asset prices left Japan's financial institutions—commercial banks, long-term credit banks, and the jusen companies—holding massive non-performing loans (NPL) against collateral worth far less than its pledged value. Japan's government and the Bank of Japan initially attempted to conceal the severity of the banking crisis, allowing insolvent institutions to roll over bad debts rather than write them off. This "evergreening" policy—known as "zombie lending"—prevented a dramatic acute crisis but prolonged the credit crunch.

By the mid-1990s, the scale of the NPL problem became undeniable:

- The **jusen companies** were liquidated in 1996 at a taxpayer cost of approximately ¥685 billion ($6 billion) in direct support, a politically explosive scandal.
- **Yamaichi Securities**, one of Japan's "Big Four" securities firms, collapsed on November 24, 1997, unable to meet ¥3.2 trillion in off-balance-sheet liabilities. Its CEO wept publicly at a press conference, declaring "Our employees have done nothing wrong."
- **Sanyo Securities**, **Nippon Credit Bank**, and **Long-Term Credit Bank of Japan** all failed in 1997–1998, requiring nationalization and restructuring at enormous public cost.
- Total non-performing loans across the banking system ultimately exceeded **¥100 trillion** by some estimates—equivalent to roughly 20% of Japan's annual GDP.

## Lost Decades: Economic and Social Consequences

The bubble's collapse ushered in Japan's **"Lost Decade"** (失われた十年)—a term later extended to "Lost Decades" (plural) as stagnation persisted through the 2000s. Key economic indicators tell the story:

- **GDP growth** averaged less than 1% annually through the 1990s, compared to 4–5% in the 1980s.
- **Consumer price deflation** became entrenched by the late 1990s, reaching negative annual rates and persisting until the 2010s.
- **Unemployment** rose to a postwar high of 5.5% in 2002, shattering the expectation of lifetime employment that had defined postwar Japanese corporate culture.
- The **Nikkei 225** sank to a post-bubble low around 7,600 in April 2003 — roughly 80% below its 1989 peak — and fell further still, to about 7,050, during the 2008–2009 global financial crisis.

The [Bank of Japan](../economy/bank-of-japan.md) pioneered emergency monetary tools now standard globally: it reduced the overnight call rate to **zero** in 1999 (the world's first Zero Interest Rate Policy, ZIRP) and launched **quantitative easing** in 2001, purchasing government bonds to inject liquidity directly into the banking system. These measures were insufficient to break deflationary expectations.

The social consequences were equally severe. The collapse of the lifetime employment model—as corporations restructured, shed workers, and reduced hiring—produced a generation of "freeters" (フリーター), workers who cycled through part-time and temporary employment without securing stable careers. Suicide rates rose sharply in the late 1990s, exceeding 30,000 per year from 1998 through 2011. Youth disengagement, declining birth rates, and growing economic inequality became entrenched features of Japanese society.

## Significance

The bubble economy and its collapse exposed structural weaknesses in Japan's financial system and governance that the prior era of rapid growth had concealed. It demonstrated that asset-price inflation, when financed through bank credit, creates self-reinforcing cycles that monetary policy can reverse only at catastrophic cost. The Bank of Japan's hesitation to raise rates in 1987–1988—under American pressure to keep Japanese rates low to support the global economy after the Black Monday crash of October 1987—exemplified how international macroeconomic coordination can override domestic prudential concerns.

The bubble remains a defining economic trauma in Japanese collective memory and the most instructive cautionary tale for global policymakers about the risks of prolonged monetary accommodation and credit-driven asset speculation. Its echoes shaped policy responses to the US housing bubble of 2008, the European sovereign debt crisis, and subsequent cycles of ultra-loose monetary policy worldwide.

The broader legacy connects to [postwar economic governance](../government-politics/postwar-constitution.md), whose stable political consensus enabled the aggressive lending culture of the 1980s and complicated decisive crisis response in the 1990s, and to Tokyo's metropolitan transformation—the [capital city's](../geography/tokyo.md) landscape was permanently reshaped by the bust, as construction projects halted mid-rise and office vacancy rates in central districts reached crisis levels.
