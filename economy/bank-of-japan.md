# Bank of Japan

The **Bank of Japan** (日本銀行, *Nihon Ginkō*, abbr. BoJ) is the central bank of Japan, responsible for monetary policy, currency issuance, and financial system stability. Established on **October 10, 1882** during the Meiji period and modeled closely on the Belgian National Bank, it operates with statutory independence under the 1997 Bank of Japan Act. The Governor is appointed by the Cabinet and confirmed by the National Diet for five-year renewable terms. The BoJ is the sole issuer of Japanese yen banknotes and serves as banker to the government and the commercial banking system.

## Founding and Early History

The BoJ was established in 1882 under its first Governor, Yoshihara Shigetoshi, as part of the Meiji government's sweeping financial modernization. Japan had introduced the yen as its national currency in **1871**, but monetary chaos persisted: a proliferation of inconvertible national bank notes and regional currencies undermined economic integration. Finance Minister **Matsukata Masayoshi** championed a central bank modeled on Western counterparts to unify the currency system, stabilize prices, and channel capital toward industrial development. Within a decade of its founding, the BoJ had achieved its initial goal of establishing a convertible silver-standard yen and consolidating note issuance.

During the Meiji and Taishō periods, the BoJ financed Japan's industrialization and its wars with China (1894–95) and Russia (1904–05), issuing war bonds and managing foreign loans in London. After World War I, the **1923 Great Kantō Earthquake** triggered a severe financial crisis: the BoJ provided emergency credit to prevent a banking collapse, but the episode exposed chronic weaknesses in the banking system—vulnerabilities that materialized as the **Bank Panic of 1927**, in which dozens of banks failed. These crises led to the Banking Act of 1928 and a gradual concentration of financial power that set the stage for wartime economic mobilization.

## Monetary Policy

The Bank of Japan's primary mandate—price stability—is embodied in a **2% inflation target** formally adopted in January 2013 under the Abenomics framework advanced by Prime Minister Shinzō Abe. It implements monetary policy through open-market operations (buying and selling Japanese Government Bonds, or JGBs), setting the uncollateralized overnight call rate (its benchmark policy rate), and adjusting reserve requirements for commercial banks.

Since the 1990s, the BoJ has pioneered a succession of unconventional tools:

- **Zero Interest Rate Policy (ZIRP):** Introduced in **February 1999**, cutting the policy rate to effectively 0% to combat deflation. The BoJ briefly and controversially raised rates in August 2000 before immediately reversing course.
- **Quantitative Easing (QE):** Launched in **March 2001** under Governor Masaru Hayami—the first central bank in the world to formally adopt QE—targeting the level of commercial bank reserves at ¥5 trillion rather than an interest rate target.
- **Quantitative and Qualitative Easing (QQE):** Launched in **April 2013** under Governor Haruhiko Kuroda, pledging to double the monetary base within two years through aggressive purchases of government bonds (JGBs), exchange-traded funds (ETFs), and real-estate investment trusts (REITs).
- **Negative Interest Rate Policy (NIRP):** Adopted in **January 2016**, charging banks −0.1% on excess reserves parked at the BoJ to incentivize lending.
- **Yield Curve Control (YCC):** Introduced in **September 2016**, pegging the 10-year JGB yield at approximately 0% while maintaining the short-term rate at −0.1%. The band around the 10-year target was widened from ±0.25% to ±0.5% in December 2022 and to ±1.0% in July 2023 as global inflation spread.

## Historical Context: The Bubble and Lost Decades

During the 1980s, financial deregulation, yen appreciation following the **1985 Plaza Accord**, and loose monetary policy inflated Japan's asset prices to unsustainable levels. The BoJ had cut its official discount rate from 5% (1985) to **2.5% (1987)** to cushion the yen's sharp appreciation, but the cheap money fueled speculative investment rather than productive activity.

The Nikkei 225 stock index peaked at **38,915** on December 29, 1989. Tokyo commercial land prices soared so high that, by some estimates, the land beneath the Imperial Palace alone exceeded the value of all real estate in California. Alarmed by inflation, the BoJ raised its discount rate five times between May 1989 and August 1990—from 2.5% to **6.0%**—puncturing the bubble.

The Nikkei fell 48% in 1990 alone. Land prices began a collapse that would last more than a decade. Banks found themselves holding vast portfolios of non-performing loans (NPLs) collateralized by now-worthless assets. The "zombie company" phenomenon emerged: banks extended credit to insolvent firms to avoid recognizing losses, paralyzed by a web of cross-shareholdings and implicit government guarantees. This credit misallocation suppressed business dynamism for two decades.

The BoJ responded with serial easing: the discount rate fell to **0.5% by September 1995** and near zero by 1999. Despite these measures, deflation proved persistent. The GDP deflator turned negative in 1994 and remained mostly below zero through the mid-2000s. Wages stagnated; consumers delayed purchases anticipating further price declines—the classic deflationary trap. This era of stagnation became known as the "Lost Decade," and as recovery failed to materialize, the "Lost Two Decades."

## Modern Operations and Policy Normalization

The BoJ holds approximately **54% of outstanding Japanese government bonds** as of 2024—a share that roughly tripled since 2012. Its balance sheet expanded to over **¥750 trillion** (~$5 trillion USD), approximately 130% of Japan's GDP, the highest ratio among major central banks globally. Through ETF purchases, the BoJ also became one of the largest single shareholders in the Tokyo Stock Exchange, owning roughly 7% of total market capitalization.

As global inflation surged in 2022–2023 and the yen weakened to **34-year lows** against the U.S. dollar, pressure mounted on the BoJ to follow other central banks in raising rates. Governor **Kazuo Ueda**, appointed in April 2023, undertook a gradual normalization:

- **March 2024:** BoJ raised its policy rate from −0.1% to **0–0.1%**, ending the world's last remaining negative-rate policy and formally abandoning YCC.
- **July 2024:** Rate raised to **0.25%**—the highest since 2008—triggering global market turbulence, including a brief unwinding of yen-funded carry trades that caused a 12% drop in the Nikkei over three days.
- **January 2025:** Rate raised to **0.5%**—the highest since 1995.

Ueda has framed further normalization as conditional on **wage-driven, sustained inflation** at the 2% target, emphasizing the importance of Japan's annual spring labor negotiations (*shuntō*) as a leading indicator. The 2024 *shuntō* round produced average wage increases of approximately 5.1%—the highest in three decades—supporting the tightening path.
