# Labor Market & Employment

Japan's labor market has undergone profound transformation since the postwar era, shifting from a system of lifetime employment (*shūshin koyo*) and seniority-based pay to a bifurcated market of permanent and precarious workers. This transition, accelerated by the 1990s [Lost Decade](lost-decade.md) and structural demographic decline, has reshaped wage inequality, gender participation, and social stability—while the 2019 Work Style Reform Law and 2022–2024 Shunto wage rounds marked the beginning of a tentative new chapter.

## Lifetime Employment & Seniority Wage System

The postwar model of lifetime employment emerged not as law but as corporate practice, a consequence of wartime labor mobilization, American occupation labor-law influence, and rapid high-growth industrialization that made workforce stability profitable. Under this system, workers—typically hired directly from high school or university through *shuukatsu* (job-hunting) simultaneous mass recruitment each spring—remained with a single employer until mandatory retirement at age 55–60, receiving wages tied to tenure (*nenko joretsu*, seniority-based order) rather than individual productivity.

The *kaisha no tame ni* (devotion to the company) ethic and the seniority wage system were mutually reinforcing: employers could demand long working hours, geographic transfers (*tanshin funin*, single-man postings away from family), and departmental rotation, while workers accepted lower cash wages early in their careers in exchange for back-loaded compensation and guaranteed security. Internal labor markets were dominant; most Japanese firms promoted managers and executives from within their own cohorts, hiring almost no senior talent externally until the 2000s.

Lifetime employment was never universal. Women, temporary workers, and rural migrants were historically excluded. Regional variation was substantial: large manufacturing exporters—[Toyota](technology-manufacturing.md), Sony, Matsushita (Panasonic), Nippon Steel—could sustain these practices across economic cycles, while small and medium enterprises (SMEs) relied on higher turnover and lower wages. Nonetheless, by the 1980s, approximately two-thirds of male workers in medium-to-large firms (those with more than 100 employees) enjoyed formal or de facto lifetime contracts, and the model defined Japan's labor market globally.

## The Lost Decade & Labor-Market Shift (1990–2005)

The 1990s asset-bubble collapse and the banking crisis forced Japanese corporations to abandon the economics of lifetime employment. Beginning in 1993–1994, major firms announced redundancy programs—euphemistically labeled *risutora* (from the English "restructuring")—that were the first mass layoffs in postwar Japan. Toyota, Honda, and other automakers shifted from layoffs of core workers to hiring freezes and aggressive use of agency temporary workers (*haken rōdōsha*).

By 2003, the proportion of precarious workers (part-time, fixed-term contract, agency temp, and *arubaito* casual) had risen from approximately 20% (1985) to more than 34% of the workforce. The 1998 Labor Standards Law revision weakened protections for *haken* (dispatched) workers, whose employment could terminate with 30 days' notice; the 1999 *haken* liberalization extended temporary staffing into manufacturing—previously prohibited—creating what critics termed a *haken gire* (dispatch cutoff) culture in which seasonal and cyclical workers could be shed instantly.

By the early 2000s, a structural "irregular worker" crisis had emerged: young adults entering the labor market during the 1993–2003 "ice age" of graduate hiring were shunted into temporary contracts, often earning ¥1.2–1.5 million annually—roughly one-third of what permanent workers with similar educational qualifications received—and lacking employer-provided health insurance, defined-benefit pensions, or training. The Takenaka structural reforms (2001–2006) under PM Koizumi explicitly encouraged flexibility as a competitive necessity; the 2003 Dispatched Workers Law expanded agency-temp coverage to all occupations except a narrow prohibited list. Retirement ages began rising under the 2006 Law on Stabilization of Employment of Elderly Persons, gradually moving the mandatory retirement floor from 60 toward 65 (fully phased in by 2025), driven not by improved old-age provision but by deteriorating pension dependency arithmetic.

## NEETs, Freeters & the Lost Generation

Two social categories emerged from the 1990s labor-market restructuring as diagnostic markers of structural failure:

**Freeters** (*furītā*, a portmanteau of "free" and the German *Arbeiter*/worker): young adults cycling through *arubaito* and part-time work without stable employment. Estimated at 2.1 million individuals (2003, government survey), freeters faced a compound disadvantage: each year outside regular employment made re-entry into the *shuukatsu* simultaneous-recruitment system harder, as firms preferred recent graduates. By the time the labor market tightened in the late 2000s, many freeters of the early 2000s were in their thirties, constituting a cohort without pension contributions, career ladders, or employer training—the core of what MHLW has called the "lost generation" (*ushinawareta sedai*).

**NEETs** (Not in Education, Employment, or Training): approximately 640,000 individuals (2003 peak, Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare count), concentrated in the 15–34 age group. The NEET designation distinguished those who had withdrawn from the labor market entirely—not looking for work—from the unemployed, and overlapped with the *hikikomori* (social-recluse) phenomenon in which individuals confine themselves at home for six months or more. Sociologists including Yūji Genda (see *A Nagging Sense of Job Insecurity*, 2001) attributed NEET formation partly to the collapse of clear social scripts for employment and life progression and partly to the psychological trauma of repeated rejection during the ice age.

Government responses included the 2003 Youth Employment Initiative (*Wakamonono Yatoi*), subsidized internship programs (*Job Café* consultation centers), and the 2005 Law to Facilitate Employment of Young People. Uptake was modest; the structural bottleneck was not information but entry-level regular positions, which firms were not creating.

## Shunto: Spring Wage Negotiations

The annual *Shunto* (春闘, "Spring Struggle") wage-negotiation round, instituted in 1955 by the Sōhyō labor federation, establishes a coordinated industry-by-industry benchmark for wage increases that cascades from large-firm settlements to SME wage-setting, making it Japan's primary wage-coordination mechanism. The pattern-setter has historically been the steel industry (in the postwar era) and later the automotive sector (particularly the Toyota agreement), whose settlements are announced in mid-March before smaller firms adjust.

Post-bubble Shunto results trace the history of wage stagnation and the tentative 2022–2024 reversal:

| Year | Major Firms' Wage Increase (base pay + bonuses) | Real Wage Change (approx.) |
|------|--------------------------------------------------|----------------------------|
| 1991 | +5.7% | +3.1% |
| 1998 | +2.7% | −0.7% (recession) |
| 2003 | +1.6% | +0.4% |
| 2013 | +1.8% | −0.2% |
| 2019 | +2.4% | +0.5% |
| 2022 | +2.1% | −1.0% (inflation spike) |
| 2023 | **+3.58%** (base pay +2.12%) | +0.7% |
| 2024 | **+5.10%** (base pay +3.84%) — largest since 1991 | +1.2% (partial year estimate) |

The 2023 and 2024 Shunto rounds broke three decades of near-zero nominal wage growth: the 2024 round's 5.1% average increase (Rengo, the major labor federation, official figure) reflected above-target inflation, government pressure under PM Kishida's "New Capitalism" agenda, and genuine labor-market tightening. However, the headline figures applied primarily to large firms covered by Rengo unions; non-unionized SMEs—accounting for roughly 70% of employment—saw average increases of 2.5–3.0%, and precarious workers saw minimal gains.

## Gender, Precarity & Womenomics

Women's labor-force participation, rising from ~50% (1980) to over 54% (2024) in the aggregate participation rate, has been shaped by gendered precarity and persistent motherhood penalties. The Equal Employment Opportunity Law (EEOL, Danjo Koyō Kikai Kintōhō), enacted 1985 and reinforced in 1997 and 2007, theoretically prohibited sex discrimination, yet informal barriers persisted. Major firms historically segregated women into clerical *ippanshoku* (general-track) positions, rarely extending lifetime employment or the managerial *sōgōshoku* (integrated career track) opportunity. Marriage and childbirth triggered the "M-curve" in female labor-force participation—a dip during the 25–35 childrearing years—and rarely led back to equivalent positions.

**Abe's Womenomics Initiative (2013–2020)**: PM Shinzō Abe's "[Women Shining](../people/abe-shinzo.md)" policy (*Joshi ga Kagayaku Shakai*) set quantitative targets of 30% women in leadership positions in government and large firms by 2020—a goal not achieved; the figure reached approximately 12% in the private sector by 2021 and 14.7% for corporate board members by 2024 (up from 6.2% in 2013). Concrete policy measures included:

- Expansion of licensed daycare (*hoikujo*) capacity: 268,000 additional slots created 2013–2019, reducing but not eliminating *taiki jidō* (waitlisted children)—still approximately 22,000 children waitlisted nationally in 2024.
- Subsidized employer childcare facilities and *ikuji kyūgyō* (childcare leave) incentives.
- The 2016 Law for Promotion of Women's Active Engagement required firms with ≥300 employees to publish numerical targets for women's representation in management and annual progress reports.

The 2023 gender wage gap measured at approximately 21.3% (women earning 78.7% of male wages for full-time workers, MHLW Basic Survey on Wage Structure). The gap reflects occupational segregation (women concentrated in lower-wage service and care sectors), the *sōgōshoku/ippanshoku* tracking system, and the lifetime-income cost of motherhood-driven career breaks. Women accounted for approximately 55% of all non-regular (precarious) workers as of 2024, a structural feature producing disproportionate income vulnerability.

## Karoshi: Overwork Deaths

*Karoshi* (過労死, "death from overwork") emerged as a recognized social phenomenon in the 1980s—initially in the finance and trading company sectors—and was formally defined under the 1987 Ministry of Labour guidelines as death from cardiovascular disease or stroke precipitated by overwork, or suicide caused by overwork-induced mental illness. Japan's labor attorneys recognized the first *karoshi* victims in the late 1970s; the first major corporate settlement came in 1983 when the family of a Mitsui & Co. trading company employee who died of stroke at age 43 received ¥2.5 million.

The most high-profile modern *karoshi* case was **Matsuri Takahashi** (1991–2015), a 24-year-old employee of the advertising giant Dentsu Inc. who died by suicide on December 25, 2015, after logging over 100 hours of overtime in October and November 2015. The case, whose cause of death was officially certified as *karoshi* (overwork-related suicide) in September 2016, triggered a national reckoning: Dentsu's president resigned in December 2016; the firm was fined ¥500,000 (the maximum under the existing Labor Standards Act) in October 2016; and the Takahashi case directly accelerated Diet passage of the 2019 Work Style Reform Law.

Government statistics on recognized *karoshi* deaths show consistent underreporting. Labour Bureau certifications (deaths where compensation was paid) numbered approximately 790–820 annually in 2020–2023, but independent estimates using excess mortality from overwork-linked conditions suggest actual *karoshi* deaths (including uncompensated cases) of 5,000–10,000 per year.

## Demographic Decline & Labor-Force Contraction

Japan's working-age population (15–64) peaked at approximately 87.2 million (1995) and has contracted to approximately 73.9 million (2024), a decline of 15% in three decades. The proportion of workers aged 65+ in the labor force rose from approximately 7% (1990) to 13.4% (2024). This demographic compression intersects with precarity: older workers laid off during the Lost Decade rarely re-entered comparable employment, and the *haken katsuyo* elderly—retirees taking agency-temp positions to supplement inadequate national pensions—became a fixture in convenience stores, distribution centers, and agricultural operations by the 2010s.

To offset labor-force decline, Japan has made cautious structural adjustments:

- The **2019 Specified Skilled Worker Visa** (特定技能, *Tokutei Ginō*) scheme created two categories: Type 1 allows up to 5-year sojourn in 14 designated sectors (care, construction, agriculture, food service, shipbuilding, etc.) without a family sponsorship right; Type 2 allows indefinite residence and family sponsorship in 9 sectors. The scheme had originally targeted 345,000 Type 1 workers by 2024—a target revised upward to 820,000 by 2029 under the June 2024 policy revision—though actual certifications reached approximately 200,000 by end-2023.
- The **2022 Refugee and Humanitarian Protection Law** marginally broadened protection grounds, and discussions on a comprehensive immigration policy framework gained political traction for the first time since the 1990s.

Japan remains far below immigration levels in Australia, Canada, or Germany relative to population; foreign workers constituted approximately 3.1% of the total labor force in 2023.

## Contemporary Labor-Market Structure (2024)

By 2024, Japan's labor market has stratified into three visible tiers:

1. **Core permanent employees** (~45–50% of workforce): receive lifetime-contract expectations, annual Shunto pay rises, employer-paid health insurance, defined-benefit or defined-contribution pensions, and training investment. Median annual cash earnings approximately ¥3.5 million (high-school graduates) to ¥4.2 million (university graduates); significant regional dispersion (Tokyo median ~¥500,000 above national average).

2. **Regular but non-lifetime workers** (~20–25%): fixed-term contracts (1–3 years, often renewed indefinitely under de facto practice) with lower wages (~¥2.5 million) and ambiguous pension and insurance coverage. Common in SMEs, professional services, and public-sector outsourcing.

3. **Precarious workers** (~25–30%): part-time, agency-temp, gig-economy (including delivery platform cyclists for Uber Eats and Demae-Can, ride-share drivers under the 2023 rideshare pilot, and online platform workers). Median annual income ¥1.5–2.2 million; minimal benefits; no job security.

The Gini coefficient for wage income rose from approximately 0.28 (1985, near postwar egalitarianism) to approximately 0.39 (2024), approaching OECD-average inequality. Relative poverty (income below 50% of median) affected 15.4% of the population in 2024, up from approximately 12% in 2000 and concentrated among single-parent households (most frequently headed by women), elderly women, and the precarious-employment tier.

## Work Style Reform & Well-being

The 2019 Work Style Reform Law (*Hatarakikata Kaikaku Kanren Hō*, a bundle of eight amended statutes) introduced: mandatory overtime caps (720 hours annually, with a 100-hour single-month cap under special agreements); equal pay for equal work requirements between regular and non-regular employees (effective 2020 for large firms, 2021 for SMEs); and flextime account-period expansion to 3 months. A separate high-income professional exemption (*kōdo purofesshonaru* system, the so-called *kō-puro*) effectively exempted those earning ¥10.75 million+ annually in designated knowledge-intensive occupations from all overtime restrictions.

COVID-19 (2020–2023) accelerated remote-work adoption, with approximately 30% of workers reporting hybrid or fully remote arrangements by 2024—double the pre-pandemic rate, concentrated in Tokyo metropolitan white-collar employment. The blurred home-work boundary created new stress management challenges, and MHLW-administered surveys in 2022 identified a rise in occupational stress-related illness claims among remote workers relative to the 2019 baseline.

Parental leave entitlements expanded significantly: paternal leave (*chichi no ikuji kyūgyō*) grew from an effectively unusable 1-week allowance in the 1990s, to 10 days of "postpartum paternal leave" under the June 2022 Child Care Leave Law amendment, to a proposed optional 4-week individual entitlement under post-2024 reform discussions. Father uptake of any parental leave rose from under 3% (2010) to approximately 17.2% (FY2022, MHLW), driven partly by normalization campaigns and employer mandates to inform each new father of their rights. Childcare infrastructure expanded: the proportion of children under 3 enrolled in daycare rose from approximately 28% (2000) to 55% (2024), yet urban costs (approximately ¥40,000–60,000 per month in Tokyo for under-subsidized slots) still constrain participation.
