# Noto Peninsula

**Category**: Geography  
**Key figures**: N/A

## Summary

The Noto Peninsula (*Noto Hantō*) extends northward into the Sea of Japan from central Honshu in Ishikawa Prefecture, forming one of Japan's most distinctive geographical features. Approximately 100 kilometers long and up to 30 kilometers wide at its broadest point, the peninsula is bounded by the Toyama Bay (*Toyama-wan*) to the east and the Sea of Japan to the north and west. The peninsula's coastlines are sharply differentiated in character: the eastern *Nanao Bay* coast (*Uchi-Noto*, "Inner Noto") is sheltered with calmer waters that historically supported fishing settlements and port commerce, while the western and northern coasts (*Soto-Noto*, "Outer Noto") face open Sea of Japan swells with dramatic cliffs, rock formations, and rugged headlands shaped by continuous wave action.

Lying at the junction of the Eurasian and North American tectonic plates, the Noto Peninsula sits within one of Japan's most seismically active regions. This tectonic setting creates not only earthquake hazard but also the varied geology — ancient sedimentary marine rocks, lifted coastal terraces, and scattered volcanic formations — that earned the peninsula designation as a **Japanese Geopark** (a national geopark under the Japanese Geoparks Network) in 2011 (revalidated 2016). Despite its relative remoteness from Japan's Pacific coast industrial corridor, the Noto developed rich cultural traditions including the renowned **Wajima lacquerware** (*Wajima-nuri*), distinctive maritime communities, the *Gojinjo Daiko* ("shrimp-fishers' drum") percussion tradition, and terraced rice paddies (*Senmaida*, "thousand-plot fields") recognized under the FAO's Globally Important Agricultural Heritage Systems programme (designated 2011).

## Geography and Climate

### Terrain

The peninsula's interior consists of a low mountain spine (*Noto Sanmyaku*) averaging 300–500 meters in elevation, with the highest points reaching approximately 565 meters. This terrain creates a gentle relief compared to Japan's more dramatic mountain ranges, but it effectively divided Noto's communities into distinct coastal zones with limited interior routes. The mountainous core, combined with the peninsula's limited flat land, restricted large-scale rice cultivation to narrow coastal plains and river valleys, shaping a landscape of small-plot agriculture and maritime subsistence that persisted well into the 20th century.

The **Senmaida terraces** near Wajima are the most celebrated expression of this agricultural adaptation: hundreds of small, irregular rice paddies carved into steep hillsides descending to the sea, maintained for over 700 years. At their peak, approximately 2,200 terraced plots occupied a single hillside overlooking the sea. Population decline from the late 20th century reduced the number of active plots, but community preservation efforts and tourism have sustained the tradition. The terraces were designated part of a Globally Important Agricultural Heritage System (GIAHS) by the FAO in 2011 alongside the peninsula's broader agricultural and fisheries traditions.

### Climate

The Noto Peninsula experiences Japan's Sea of Japan climate (*nihonkai gawa no kikou*) with heavy winter snowfall driven by cold monsoon winds picking up moisture over the Sea of Japan. Winter temperatures are cold but not extreme — average January temperatures hover around 4–6°C in coastal areas — but persistent cloud cover, rain, and snow characterize the season. Summer is warm with moderate humidity; the peninsula is largely sheltered from Pacific typhoons by the Japanese Alps. Annual precipitation averages 1,500–2,000 mm, with significant winter snow accumulation in the mountains.

This climate and the dramatic winter seas shaped the maritime culture of the outer Noto coast, where fishing communities developed seaworthiness skills, rhythmic drum traditions for calling schools of fish (the *Gojinjo Daiko*, a fierce percussion tradition reportedly originating in the 16th century), and distinctive religious practices centered on the sea.

### Coastal Features

The Outer Noto coastline (Sea of Japan side) presents spectacular geological formations:

- **Godzilla Rock** (*Gojira Iwa*) near Suzu: A basalt sea stack eroded into a shape resembling the film monster, now a popular photography destination.
- **Sosogi Coast** (*Sōsogi Kaigan*): A stretch of coastal rock formations created by contrasting geology — dark basalt intrusions cutting through lighter sedimentary layers — producing dramatic striped outcroppings visible at low tide.
- **Ganmon** ("rock gate"): A natural sea arch formed by wave erosion through a basalt headland near Monzen town; the site features a small cave accessible by small boat.
- **Cape Rokkozaki** (*Rokkōsaki*): The northernmost tip of the peninsula, a rocky promontory with an 1883 lighthouse that remains in operation. The cape's exposure to open sea conditions makes it a navigational landmark.

The Inner Noto (Toyama Bay side) is far calmer. Toyama Bay reaches depths of over 1,000 meters within close proximity to shore, creating an unusual upwelling phenomenon that brings cold, nutrient-rich water to the surface. This supports exceptional marine biodiversity, including abundant firefly squid (*hotaru ika*), yellowtail (*buri*), and other commercially valuable species.

## Historical Development

### Medieval and Early Modern Period

The Noto Peninsula's relative isolation — connected to the mainland only by the narrow neck at its southern base — fostered distinct local traditions and political independence. During the Sengoku period (see [Sengoku Period](../history/sengoku-period.md)), the Noto was controlled by the Hatakeyama clan until 1576, when Oda Nobunaga's forces under Shibata Katsuie seized it. After Nobunaga's death (1582), Noto passed through the hands of successive warlords before coming under the Maeda clan of Kanazawa, who ruled the domain (one of Japan's richest outside the Tokugawa heartland) throughout the Edo period.

### Wajima Lacquerware (*Wajima-nuri*)

The most significant cultural contribution of the Noto Peninsula is **Wajima lacquerware**, produced in and around the city of Wajima on the outer northern coast. Wajima-nuri is distinguished by its **multiple-layer lacquer technique** (*ji-no-ko*): powdered diatomaceous earth from the Wajima area is mixed into intermediate lacquer coats, building up an exceptionally hard, durable foundation layer. A completed piece typically requires **75 or more distinct steps** of lacquering, drying, polishing, and decoration, and the process for a single item may span months to over a year.

The tradition dates to at least the 15th century, with the earliest surviving documented examples from the 17th century. By the Edo period (see [Edo Period](../history/edo-period.md)), Wajima lacquerware had developed its signature distribution system: traveling salesmen (*gyōshō*) carried Wajima pieces throughout Japan, particularly to major Buddhist temples which were among the principal customers for lacquered ceremonial vessels. Wajima lacquerware supplied tea ceremony utensils — tea containers, serving trays, and confectionery boxes — to practitioners throughout Japan (see [Tea Ceremony](../culture-society/tea-ceremony.md)).

Wajima lacquerware was designated a **National Traditional Craft** (*dentō kōgei*) by the Japanese government in 1975. At its modern peak in the 1980s–1990s, over 1,000 craftspeople were employed in the industry. The Wajima Lacquerware Museum (opened 1992) preserves historical examples and hosts living craftspeople demonstrating techniques.

### Maritime Culture and Salt Production

The sea was the peninsula's primary economic resource. Fishing — for yellowtail, sea bream (*tai*), abalone (*awabi*), squid, and sea cucumbers (*namako*) — sustained coastal communities throughout recorded history. The *ama* (海女, sea-women) diving tradition, in which women free-dive without equipment to harvest shellfish and seaweed from the seafloor, remained active on the Noto coast into the 21st century. The Noto ama tradition is culturally linked to similar practices in the Ise-Shima region and in Korea's Jeju Island.

Salt production from sea water (*seien*) was another major Noto industry, particularly from the medieval period through the early Meiji era. The sheltered bays of Inner Noto provided suitable conditions for salt evaporation; the resulting *Noto salt* (*Noto no shio*) was traded widely throughout central Japan. The industry declined with the Meiji-era modernization of salt production but has been partially revived as an artisanal specialty product.

## The 2024 Noto Earthquake

On **January 1, 2024**, a magnitude 7.6 earthquake struck the northern Noto Peninsula at 4:10 PM local time, with an epicenter approximately 11 km northeast of Wajima City at a depth of about 16 km. It was the largest inland earthquake to strike mainland Japan since the 2016 Kumamoto earthquakes and the deadliest to strike Japan since the 2011 Great East Japan (Tōhoku) Earthquake.

The earthquake caused widespread destruction across the peninsula's northern communities:

- **Fatalities**: At least 241 deaths were confirmed (as of early 2024), with the majority in Wajima and Suzu cities.
- **Building destruction**: Over 13,000 buildings were totally or partially destroyed; densely built traditional wooden townscapes in central Wajima and Suzu suffered catastrophic collapse.
- **Tsunami**: Initial warnings of up to 5-meter tsunami waves were issued. Waves approximately 1.2 meters high struck the Wajima coast; higher run-up was recorded at isolated bays along the outer Noto coast.
- **Infrastructure**: Roads — many running along narrow coastal margins or through mountain passes — were severed by landslides and surface ruptures, cutting off dozens of coastal communities for days to weeks. The isolation of some villages required helicopter evacuation of residents.
- **Wajima lacquerware**: The earthquake caused severe damage to workshops, kiln facilities, and museum collections. A large fire that broke out in central Wajima's market district (*Asaichi*, the "morning market," one of Japan's three famous morning markets) destroyed approximately 200 buildings and further devastated the city's historic center.

The disaster drew national and international attention to the Noto's pre-existing demographic challenges: many of the most affected communities were elderly-majority villages already facing rural depopulation, and the destruction compounded the difficulty of rebuilding or resettling survivors. The earthquake also raised urgent questions about Japan's infrastructure policies in rural areas and disaster preparedness for isolated peninsular communities.

Recovery efforts through 2024–2025 involved national government funding, mutual aid from other Japanese municipalities (particularly via the *furusato nōzei* hometown tax system), and significant volunteer coordination. However, the scale of destruction relative to the peninsula's small and aging population made full recovery a long-term challenge.

## Geopark and Cultural Heritage

The **Noto Peninsula Geopark** (日本ジオパーク能登半島, a Japanese Geopark officially recognized 2011, revalidated 2016 and 2020) encompasses the entire peninsula and recognizes its geological diversity: marine sedimentary strata recording ancient ocean environments, submarine landslide deposits, coastal terrace formations lifted by tectonic activity, and the ongoing active tectonics visible in earthquake deformation. The geopark's interpretive network includes geological sites, traditional villages, and working agricultural landscapes.

In 2011, the peninsula was also awarded the FAO's **Globally Important Agricultural Heritage System** (GIAHS) designation for its "Noto's Satoyama and Satoumi" landscape — the integrated system of mountain villages (*satoyama*) and coastal communities (*satoumi*) linked by traditional land and sea management practices. This designation acknowledged the Noto's rice terraces, traditional fishing methods, and community forest management as living cultural landscapes of global significance.

## Significance

The Noto Peninsula represents the intersection of Japan's geological dynamism, cultural depth, and 21st-century demographic challenges. Its unique position on the Sea of Japan coast linked it to networks — Korean and Chinese trade, Sea of Japan fishing grounds, the Maeda domain's cultural patronage — distinct from the Pacific-facing development that drove Japan's modern industrialization. The region exemplifies Japan's environmental diversity (from tectonic hazards to productive marine ecosystems) and the cultural persistence of rural communities under modernization pressure.

The 2024 earthquake transformed the Noto from a celebrated traditional landscape into a symbol of rural Japan's vulnerability and resilience. Reconstruction choices — whether to rebuild traditional townscapes or pursue modern, earthquake-resistant alternatives — reflect broader national debates about heritage, rural revitalization, and the sustainability of Japan's dispersed traditional communities.
