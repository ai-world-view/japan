# Hokkaido

**Category**: Geography  
**Key figures**: Ainu indigenous peoples, Matsumae clan, Kuroda Kiyotaka, Horace Capron, William S. Clark

## Summary

Hokkaido is Japan's second-largest island and the northernmost of the four major Home Islands, covering approximately **83,456 km²** (about 22% of Japan's total land area) while holding only around **5.2 million people** (roughly 4% of the national population as of 2020). Known for its subarctic climate—heavy snowfall averaging 5–7 meters per year in inland mountain zones, cool summers, and temperatures reaching −30°C in the interior—Hokkaido remained sparsely inhabited during much of the feudal period, populated primarily by the indigenous **Ainu people** who developed distinct cultural practices centered on salmon fishing, deer hunting, bear ceremonies, and trade with Japanese merchants to the south. The Tokugawa shogunate exercised nominal control through the Matsumae clan's domain in the southwestern tip of the island, though the interior and northern regions remained largely outside Japanese administrative systems until the Meiji Restoration.

Hokkaido's transformation as a Japanese territory accelerated dramatically following the Meiji Restoration in 1868. The government established the Hokkaido Development Commission (*Hokkaido Kaitaku-shi*, 1869–1882) to colonize and modernize the island, bringing Japanese settlers (*tondenhei* farmer-soldier colonists), introducing mechanized agriculture and coal mining, and constructing transportation infrastructure. American agricultural advisor **Horace Capron** and educator **William S. Clark** (of Sapporo Agricultural College, now Hokkaido University, founded 1876) shaped early development along American frontier models. Colonization marginalized the Ainu people, whose traditional lands and fishing rights were restricted through legislation, leading to cultural suppression and demographic decline. The island became an agricultural breadbasket and industrial center, with coal mining in the Ishikari and Sorachi coalfields providing crucial energy resources for Japan's industrialization.

## Physical Geography

Hokkaido's terrain is dominated by a central mountain spine containing active volcanoes—**Mount Asahi** (*Asahidake*, 2,291 m), the highest peak in Hokkaido, crowns the *Taisetsuzan* volcanic group at the centerpiece of Japan's largest national park (Daisetsuzan National Park, designated 1934). The island is seismically active: the 1993 Southwest Hokkaido Earthquake (M 7.8) triggered a tsunami that killed 230 people; the 2018 Hokkaido Eastern Iburi Earthquake (M 6.7) caused widespread landslides and infrastructure failure. Three major watershed systems drain into the Sea of Japan to the west, the Sea of Okhotsk to the north, and the Pacific to the south and east. The **Soya Strait** (La Pérouse Strait), only 43 km wide, separates Hokkaido from Russian Sakhalin; the **Tsugaru Strait** to the south divides it from Honshu. The **Seikan Tunnel** (completed 1988), the world's longest undersea tunnel at 53.85 km, now connects Hokkaido to Honshu by rail.

The **Kushiro Wetlands** on the eastern coast, Japan's largest wetland at roughly 183 km², host the critically endangered **red-crowned crane** (*Grus japonensis*) and are designated a Ramsar Convention site. Lake Mashu and Lake Akan are notable volcanic caldera lakes in eastern Hokkaido's Akan-Mashu National Park.

## Ainu Indigenous History

The Ainu are the indigenous people of Hokkaido, the Kuril Islands, and southern Sakhalin, with cultural roots reaching back thousands of years before Japanese contact. Ainu material culture centered on salmon (*cep* in the Ainu language) as a dietary staple, supplemented by bear hunting, deer, and coastal marine resources. The **Iyomante** (bear-sending ceremony) was among the most significant ritual practices. Trade between Ainu and Japanese merchants through the Matsumae domain created deep interdependencies but also coercive labor extraction of Ainu fishers.

Ainu population estimates before sustained contact reach into the tens of thousands; by the early twentieth century, official counts fell below 20,000 as disease, displacement, and intermarriage reduced the community. The **Former Natives Protection Act of 1899** (abolished 1997) classified Ainu as "former natives" (*kyū dojin*), stripped fishing and hunting rights, mandated assimilation into Japanese schooling, and redistributed ancestral lands to Japanese settlers. These policies accelerated language loss: the Ainu language is now considered critically endangered, with fewer than ten native speakers remaining by the early 2000s.

Japan did not formally recognize the Ainu as an indigenous people until **2019**, when the **Ainu People's Support Act** passed the Diet, declaring the Ainu "an indigenous people" for the first time in law and creating support funds for cultural revitalization. The **Upopoy National Ainu Museum** opened in Shiraoi in 2020 as Japan's first national facility dedicated to Ainu culture and history.

## Meiji Colonization and Development (1868–1912)

The Meiji government renamed the island "Hokkaido" (literally "northern sea road") in 1869, replacing older names including *Ezo* and *Ezochi*. The **Hokkaido Development Commission** (*Kaitaku-shi*), administered initially under Kuroda Kiyotaka, oversaw colonization with urgency driven partly by concern over Russian expansion in the north. American advisor Horace Capron arrived in 1871 with a team of American agriculturalists, engineers, and naturalists who introduced mechanized farming, livestock breeding, potato cultivation, and Western-style city planning—most visibly in Sapporo, laid out on a grid system in 1871.

William S. Clark, president of Massachusetts Agricultural College, co-founded Sapporo Agricultural College in 1876 and introduced American agricultural science and a Protestant Christian ethic to early students, famously departing in 1877 with the words "Boys, be ambitious!" (*Shōnen yo, taishi o idake*). The *tondenhei* system (1874–1904) settled approximately 7,300 military farm-colonist households across Hokkaido, simultaneously populating frontier areas and providing rapid-mobilization military capacity.

Coal became the island's economic engine: the **Horonai Coal Mine** opened in 1879 and was connected to Otaru by Japan's first railway in Hokkaido (1880). By the early twentieth century, the **Ishikari coalfield** was among Japan's most productive, feeding steel mills and naval shipyards in Honshu. Population grew from an estimated 58,000 in 1869 to roughly **1.4 million** by 1905.

## Modern Economy and Demographics

Hokkaido's contemporary economy rests on four pillars:

**Agriculture**: Hokkaido produces approximately 15% of Japan's total agricultural output despite being one prefecture. It is the dominant producer of wheat (over 60% of national output), potatoes (80%), soybeans, sugar beets, dairy products (over 50% of national milk production), and beef. The Tokachi and Konsen plains in eastern Hokkaido contain Japan's most extensive mechanized farmland. Dairy cooperatives and food-processing industries around **Obihiro** and **Memanbetsu** underpin regional economies.

**Fisheries**: Hokkaido's coastal waters support major hauls of salmon, crab (snow crab and king crab from the Sea of Okhotsk), scallops, sea urchin (*uni*), and kelp (*kombu*). The port of **Kushiro** is one of Japan's major fishing hubs. Hokkaido kombu accounts for the majority of national kombu production and is an essential ingredient in Japanese dashi broth.

**Tourism**: The island draws domestic and international visitors for winter sports (Niseko, Furano, and Rusutsu are major ski resorts popular with Australian and Chinese visitors), summer wildflower tourism (Furano lavender fields), and nature tourism in national parks. **Sapporo** hosted the 1972 Winter Olympics, Japan's first, and submitted bids for future Winter Games.

**Mining and energy**: Coal mining has declined sharply since the 1960s—most Hokkaido collieries closed by the 1990s—but geothermal and wind energy development has expanded. The island receives proportionally more renewable investment than most Japanese regions given its land area and wind resources.

**Demographics**: Hokkaido's population peaked in the 1990s at roughly 5.7 million and has been declining since, reflecting national aging trends amplified by rural depopulation. Sapporo, the prefectural capital (population approximately 1.97 million city proper), has grown even as rural municipalities shrink dramatically. Many farming and fishing towns in eastern Hokkaido face existential population loss.

## Major Cities

- **Sapporo** (札幌): Prefectural capital and by far the largest city; home to Hokkaido University, the annual Sapporo Snow Festival (drawing millions of visitors since 1950), the Sapporo Dome, and the Nippon-Ham Fighters baseball team.
- **Hakodate** (函館): Historic port city in southern Hokkaido, the first Japanese city to open to Western trade under the 1854 Convention of Kanagawa; retains a distinctive blend of Western-style architecture and traditional townscape.
- **Asahikawa** (旭川): Inland manufacturing and agricultural service city; coldest major city in Japan.
- **Obihiro** (帯広): Center of the Tokachi agricultural plain; known for *Banei* horse racing, the only draft-horse racing in the world.
- **Kushiro** (釧路): Major fishing port and gateway to the Kushiro Wetlands and Akan Mashu National Park.

## Significance

Hokkaido's development represents a critical chapter in Japan's territorial consolidation and industrial modernization. As both a frontier for Japanese expansion and the ancestral homeland of the Ainu, the island embodies Japan's complex relationship with indigenous peoples and minority rights. The 1869–1882 colonization established land-use patterns, infrastructure networks, and agricultural systems that persist today, while simultaneously dispossessing the Ainu of their land base and cultural autonomy. Hokkaido's coal and agricultural output were essential inputs to Japan's industrialization and wartime mobilization; its fisheries have long shaped Japanese food culture. Today, Hokkaido's economy combines agriculture, fisheries, tourism, and emerging renewable energy, while its demographics reflect the ongoing population decline challenges facing Japan's regions. The island's recent Ainu cultural recognition and the 2020 opening of the Upopoy museum represent a partial reckoning with colonization's legacies.
