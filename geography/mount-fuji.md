# Mount Fuji

**Category**: Geography  
**Key figures**: Katsushika Hokusai, Utagawa Hiroshige, Yamabe no Akahito (*Man'yōshū* poet)

## Summary

Mount Fuji (*Fujisan*, 富士山), at 3,776.24 meters (12,389 feet), is Japan's tallest mountain and one of the world's most recognized natural landmarks. A near-perfect stratovolcano straddling the border of Shizuoka and Yamanashi prefectures, it rises in striking isolation from a surrounding plain roughly 100 kilometers southwest of [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md), visible on clear winter days from the capital's upper floors and western suburbs. The mountain is designated a **UNESCO Cultural World Heritage Site** (inscribed June 22, 2013)—notably as a cultural rather than natural landscape—in recognition of its role in Japanese art, religion, and national identity over more than a millennium.

## Geology and Volcanic History

Mount Fuji is the product of roughly 100,000 years of volcanic activity, built up in three distinct phases recognized in the geologic record:

- **Ko-Fuji** (Old Fuji, ~100,000–10,000 BCE): the ancestral cone, now largely buried beneath subsequent eruptions. Ko-Fuji eruptions were predominantly basaltic lava flows that established the mountain's broad foundation.
- **Chū-Fuji** (Middle Fuji, ~10,000–3,500 BCE): a transitional phase characterized by explosive eruptions and the deposition of scoria and ash layers.
- **Shin-Fuji** (New Fuji, ~3,500 BCE–present): the modern cone visible today, formed by effusive and explosive eruptions that built the nearly symmetrical profile. The Shin-Fuji phase includes all historically recorded eruptions.

Since approximately 781 CE, Japanese chronicles record approximately 18 eruptions, the most significant of which are:

| Date | Eruption Name | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| 864–866 | Jōgan eruption | Major lava flow created Lake Senoumi (now largely dry) |
| 1083 | — | Explosive eruption, significant ash fall |
| 1511 | — | Small eruption |
| 1707–1708 | **Hōei eruption** | Last and largest in recorded history |

The **Hōei eruption** (December 16, 1707 – January 1, 1708) remains the most consequential modern volcanic event in central Japan. It was preceded just 49 days earlier by the Hōei earthquake (October 28, 1707), a magnitude ~8.6 offshore earthquake — one of the largest in Japanese recorded history — which may have destabilized magma chambers beneath the mountain. The eruption opened a new vent (Hōei-zan) on Fuji's southeastern flank at approximately 2,700 meters elevation, producing sustained ash and tephra falls that blanketed Edo (modern [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md)) and surrounding farmland over sixteen days. While no direct deaths were recorded from the eruption itself, widespread crop failures and economic disruption followed; the estimated 700 million cubic meters of erupted material remains the benchmark for Japanese volcanic hazard planning.

Fuji's current dormancy — over 300 years — is considered anomalously long relative to its historical activity. The Japan Meteorological Agency classifies it as an **A-rank active volcano** (highest threat category) and maintains continuous seismic and deformation monitoring. Elevated seismicity in 2000–2001 prompted renewed hazard assessments but no eruptive activity occurred.

## Topography and the Fuji Five Lakes

The summit caldera is roughly 780 meters in diameter and 240 meters deep, with the highest point at **Kengamine** (3,776.24 m). Eight subsidiary peaks ring the caldera rim. The summit hosts the **Kusushi Shrine** (奥宮, the inner sanctuary of Fujisan Hongu Sengen Taisha) and a Japan Meteorological Agency observatory that operated at the summit from 1932 to 2004.

The **Fuji Five Lakes** (*Fujigoko*) occupy the northern flank, formed when historic lava flows dammed river valleys:

| Lake | Area (km²) | Max Depth (m) | Notes |
|------|-----------|--------------|-------|
| Yamanaka | 6.57 | 13.3 | Largest by surface area; nearest to summit |
| Kawaguchi | 5.70 | 14.6 | Most accessible; primary tourist hub |
| Sai | 2.10 | 71.7 | Deepest of the five |
| Shoji | 0.50 | 15.2 | Smallest; cleanest water quality |
| Motosu | 4.70 | 121.6 | Deepest by far; image used on 1,000-yen note |

A photograph of Fuji reflected in the calm water of Lake Motosu (the "Reverse Fuji," from Okada Kōyō's image) appeared on the reverse of the 1,000-yen banknote of the 2004 series, perpetuating the mountain's role in Japanese visual culture; the subsequent 2024 series instead placed Hokusai's "Great Wave" on the note's reverse.

## Pilgrimage and the Climbing Tradition

Worship of Mount Fuji as a sacred peak (*shintai*, the body of a deity) extends to at least the 7th century CE, with the mountain associated with the goddess **Konohanasakuya-hime** (木花之佐久夜毘売), a Shinto deity of blossoming flowers and volcanic fire. The primary shrine complex, **Fujisan Hongu Sengen Taisha** (富士山本宮浅間大社) in Fujinomiya, traces its founding to the 3rd century CE in legend, with more reliable records dating to the 9th century. Sengen shrines — there are over 1,300 branch shrines across Japan — were established to enshrine and appease the volcanic mountain.

By the Edo period (1603–1868), organized confraternities called ***Fujikō*** (富士講) had emerged in urban commoner society, particularly in Edo. These mutual-aid pilgrim societies organized annual group climbs and erected small artificial mounds (*fuji-zuka*) in city neighborhoods so that those unable to make the journey could symbolically ascend. At their peak in the early 19th century, *Fujikō* numbered in the hundreds across the Edo metropolitan area, with tens of thousands of participants annually. This popular religious enthusiasm was closely intertwined with the [Edo period's](../history/edo-period.md) merchant culture and the spread of literacy among commoners.

Modern climbing is organized along four official trails:
- **Yoshida Trail** (north; most popular, ~50% of climbers): starts at Fuji Subaru Line 5th Station (~2,300 m)
- **Subashiri Trail** (northeast): notable for descent through volcanic ash fields
- **Gotemba Trail** (east; longest, lowest starting elevation at 1,440 m)
- **Fujinomiya Trail** (south; highest starting 5th Station at 2,400 m, closest to summit)

Annual climbing-season visitors peaked at approximately **320,000 in 2023**, prompting Yamanashi Prefecture to implement a fee gate (¥2,000 per person) and daily climber cap (4,000) on the Yoshida Trail from 2024. Concerns about *fukushi tōzan* ("bullet climbing" — attempting the summit at night without rest or equipment) and waste disposal drove the restrictions.

## Art, Literature, and Cultural Symbolism

Mount Fuji appears in the oldest surviving Japanese poetry anthology, the ***Man'yōshū*** (c. 759 CE), where Yamabe no Akahito composed celebrated verses describing the eternal snows and sacred smoke of the mountain. It recurs throughout *waka* and *haiku* traditions as a symbol of permanence, purity, and the meeting of earth and sky.

The mountain achieved global visual recognition through two woodblock print series of the [Edo period](../history/edo-period.md):

- **[Katsushika Hokusai](../people/katsushika-hokusai.md)** — *Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji* (*Fugaku Sanjūrokkei*, c. 1830–1833): a suite of 46 prints (the series expanded beyond 36) depicting Fuji from varied distances and angles across the seasons. The most famous, *Under the Wave off Kanagawa* ("The Great Wave"), shows the mountain as a distant blue cone beneath a towering wave — an image that became a defining icon of Japanese art internationally and influenced the European *Japonisme* movement of the 1860s–1890s.
- **Utagawa Hiroshige** — *Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji* (*Fugaku Sanjūrokkei*, 1858): a companion series published late in Hiroshige's life, emphasizing atmospheric mood and seasonal color over Hokusai's dynamic compositions.

In the modern era, Fuji appears ubiquitously in Japanese film, advertising, and national iconography. The mountain forms the backdrop of the *Rising Sun* aesthetic and appears in countless corporate logos, travel posters, and cultural productions. The UNESCO citation (2013) explicitly acknowledged 25 component sites — including the Sengen shrines, *fuji-zuka* mounds in Tokyo, and the five lakes — reflecting the mountain's role as a living cultural landscape rather than merely a scenic natural feature. See also [Shinto religion](../culture-society/shinto-religion.md) for the spiritual framework underpinning Fuji worship.

## Contemporary Management and Hazard Preparedness

Following UNESCO inscription, both Yamanashi and Shizuoka prefectures established management plans governing visitor infrastructure, trail maintenance, and visual corridor protection (preventing high-rise construction that would block traditional viewsheds). The **Fujisan World Cultural Heritage Council** coordinates policy across 12 municipalities.

Japan's Cabinet Office volcanic risk scenario (revised 2020) models a repeat Hōei-scale eruption depositing ash across the Kanto plain, potentially disrupting road and rail networks serving [Tokyo](../geography/tokyo.md) and its 37 million metropolitan-area residents. The scenario estimates economic losses exceeding ¥2.5 trillion and requires evacuation of approximately 750,000 residents from hazard zones on Fuji's flanks. This geohazard context lends urgency to the ongoing seismic monitoring network and to public emergency preparedness exercises held in the surrounding municipalities.
