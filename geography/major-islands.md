# Major Islands

**Category**: Geography  
**Key figures**: N/A

## Summary

Japan is an archipelago of approximately **6,852 islands** (those with a coastline perimeter of 100 metres or more), stretching roughly 3,000 kilometres from northeast to southwest along the eastern edge of the Asian continent. The four main islands—Honshu, Hokkaido, Kyushu, and Shikoku—account for approximately **97% of Japan's total land area** of 377,975 km² and house nearly all of its population of about 125 million (2023 estimate). Japan lies along the Pacific Ring of Fire at the convergence of four tectonic plates—the Pacific, North American, Eurasian, and Philippine Sea plates—making it one of the most seismically and volcanically active countries on Earth, with **111 active volcanoes** representing roughly 10% of the global total.

The islands are oriented in a northeast–southwest arc, with the Japan Sea and East China Sea to the west and the Pacific Ocean to the east. This geographic position shaped Japan's history profoundly: close enough to mainland Asia for cultural exchange and commerce, but separated by sea lanes that provided natural defense and preserved cultural distinctiveness.

## Honshu

**Area**: 227,960 km² (largest island; 7th largest in the world)  
**Population**: approximately 103 million (about 82% of Japan's total)  
**Major cities**: Tokyo, Osaka, Kyoto, Nagoya, Hiroshima, Sendai, Yokohama

Honshu is the political, economic, and cultural heartland of Japan. The imperial capital shifted to Edo (renamed Tokyo) in 1869 following the [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md), cementing Honshu's centrality. The island contains Japan's highest peak, **Mount Fuji** (3,776 m), a dormant stratovolcano straddling Shizuoka and Yamanashi prefectures that last erupted in 1707–1708. The Kantō Plain surrounding Tokyo is the largest lowland area in Japan (roughly 17,000 km²) and the most densely populated.

The Chūbu region of central Honshu contains the **Japanese Alps** (the Hida, Kiso, and Akaishi mountain ranges), with peaks exceeding 3,000 m that create a sharp climatic divide between the Pacific coast and the Sea of Japan coast. This divide produces heavy snowfall (*yukiguni*, "snow country") on the western side—some areas receive over 3 metres annually—while Pacific-facing areas experience drier winters.

Honshu is connected to [Hokkaido](#hokkaido) by the **Seikan Tunnel** (53.85 km; opened March 13, 1988), the world's longest undersea rail tunnel, passing beneath the Tsugaru Strait. It is connected to [Shikoku](#shikoku) by three expressway-bridge corridors across the Seto Inland Sea (the Nishiseto, Seto-Chūō, and Kobe-Awaji-Naruto routes).

The Great East Japan Earthquake of **March 11, 2011** (magnitude 9.0–9.1), centered off Honshu's Tōhoku coast, generated tsunamis that killed approximately 19,759 people and triggered the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster, the worst nuclear accident since Chernobyl.

## Hokkaido

**Area**: 83,423 km² (second largest; 21st largest island in the world)  
**Population**: approximately 5.2 million  
**Major cities**: Sapporo, Hakodate, Asahikawa, Kushiro

Hokkaido occupies Japan's northern frontier and was the last major island to be systematically colonized by the Japanese state. The indigenous **Ainu people** inhabited Hokkaido and the Kuril Islands for millennia before Japanese settlement intensified in the Meiji era. The Meiji government established the **Hokkaido Development Commission** (*Kaitakushi*) in 1869, importing American and European agricultural expertise—including American agricultural scientist Horace Capron, who advised large-scale farming development—to settle and exploit the island's resources.

Hokkaido's climate is distinctly cooler and more continental than the other main islands, with average January temperatures in Sapporo of around −4°C and significant snowfall. **Mount Asahi** (2,291 m) in Daisetsuzan National Park is Hokkaido's highest peak. The island produces about 20% of Japan's agricultural output despite having only about 4% of the population—a legacy of Meiji-era land development that created large farms unlike the small plots typical elsewhere.

The **Tsugaru Strait** separating Hokkaido from Honshu is approximately 20 km wide at its narrowest point and historically was a significant barrier to integration. The Seikan Tunnel beneath it, completed after 24 years of construction and costing approximately ¥1.1 trillion (around $3.8 billion in 1988 values), represented a major national infrastructure commitment. A Shinkansen extension through the tunnel opened on March 26, 2016.

The island is also notable for its proximity to disputed territories. The four southernmost Kuril Islands (called the *Northern Territories* by Japan)—Iturup, Kunashir, Shikotan, and the Habomai group—were seized by the Soviet Union in August–September 1945 and remain a source of ongoing diplomatic dispute with Russia.

## Kyushu

**Area**: 36,782 km² (third largest)  
**Population**: approximately 13 million  
**Major cities**: Fukuoka, Kagoshima, Kumamoto, Nagasaki, Kitakyushu

Kyushu ("Nine Provinces") is historically Japan's gateway to the Asian continent and the outside world. The city of **Hakata** (now part of Fukuoka) was Japan's primary trade port with Song and Ming China and with Korea throughout the medieval period. Kyushu was also where Portuguese traders and Jesuit missionaries—including Francis Xavier, who arrived in **Kagoshima in 1549**—first made contact with Japan, introducing firearms (*tanegashima*) and Christianity before both were suppressed by the Tokugawa shogunate.

The island is volcanically active. **Mount Aso** in Kumamoto Prefecture contains one of the world's largest calderas (roughly 25 km north–south by 18 km east–west) and remains one of Japan's most active volcanoes. The **Shimabara Eruption** of 1792 triggered a massive collapse that generated a **mega-tsunami** (*the Shimabara Catastrophe*) killing approximately 15,000 people, one of Japan's deadliest volcanic disasters.

Nagasaki—Japan's sole officially designated port for foreign trade during the Edo period's *sakoku* isolation policy—hosted the Dutch East India Company (VOC) trading post on the artificial island of **Dejima** from 1641 to 1853, Japan's only sanctioned window to Western knowledge. The city was the second target of an atomic bomb on **August 9, 1945**, when a plutonium device ("Fat Man") killed an estimated 39,000–80,000 people, contributing to Japan's surrender and the end of World War II. Nagasaki's Hypocenter Park and the **Nagasaki Peace Memorial Hall** commemorate these events.

The **Kanmon Strait** (minimum width approximately 600 m) separates Kyushu from Honshu and is crossed by rail, road, and pedestrian tunnels as well as bridges.

## Shikoku

**Area**: 18,800 km² (smallest of the four main islands)  
**Population**: approximately 3.7 million  
**Major cities**: Matsuyama, Kochi, Takamatsu, Tokushima

Shikoku ("Four Provinces") is the smallest and least urbanized of Japan's four main islands, separated from Honshu and Kyushu by the **Seto Inland Sea** (*Seto Naikai*)—one of Japan's most historically significant waterways, a sheltered sea highway connecting the imperial heartland of Kansai with Kyushu. The Seto Inland Sea contains hundreds of smaller islands and was a major route for cultural and commercial exchange throughout Japanese history.

Shikoku is most famous for the **88-temple pilgrimage** (*Shikoku Junrei* or *Ohenro*) associated with the Buddhist monk Kūkai (Kōbō Daishi, 774–835 CE), who was born at Zentsuji, now one of the pilgrimage sites. The roughly 1,200 km circuit, traditionally completed on foot in 30–60 days, remains one of Japan's most important pilgrimage traditions, attracting approximately 100,000–200,000 participants annually.

The island's primary agricultural products include mandarin oranges (*mikan*) and the sweet citrus *yuzu*. Cape Muroto and Cape Ashizuri, Shikoku's two southernmost promontories, are exposed to Pacific typhoons, which historically limited the island's maritime access.

## Ryukyu Islands (Okinawa)

Beyond the four main islands, the **Ryukyu Island chain** extends approximately 1,200 km southwest toward Taiwan, comprising about 100 inhabited islands. The Ryukyu Kingdom, founded in 1429 and an important hub of maritime trade between Japan, China, and Southeast Asia, was annexed by the Satsuma domain in 1609 and formally incorporated into Japan as **Okinawa Prefecture** in 1879. The main island of Okinawa (1,207 km²) hosts the prefectural capital Naha and most of the population of approximately 1.47 million.

Okinawa was the site of the **Battle of Okinawa** (April 1–June 22, 1945), the largest amphibious assault in the Pacific War, in which approximately 12,000 American troops, 110,000 Japanese defenders, and 94,000–150,000 Okinawan civilians died. The island was under U.S. administration until **May 15, 1972**, when it was returned to Japan, though large U.S. military installations remain under the 1951 Japan–U.S. Security Treaty.

## Tectonic Activity and Natural Hazards

Japan's position at the convergence of four tectonic plates produces frequent and severe seismicity. The country experiences approximately **1,500 earthquakes annually** that are perceptible without instruments and accounts for roughly 20% of the world's magnitude-6.0-or-greater earthquakes. Major 20th- and 21st-century events include the **Great Kantō Earthquake** (September 1, 1923; magnitude 7.9; ~142,000 deaths), the **Great Hanshin Earthquake** (January 17, 1995; magnitude 6.9; ~6,434 deaths), and the **Great East Japan Earthquake** (March 11, 2011; magnitude 9.0–9.1; ~19,759 deaths and missing).

Volcanic eruptions pose ongoing risks. Mount Ontake's eruption on September 27, 2014 killed 63 hikers, the deadliest volcanic event in Japan since 1926. Sakurajima in Kagoshima Bay (Kyushu) erupts multiple times daily and is one of the world's most active volcanoes.

## Significance

Japan's archipelago geography—islands strung along a tectonic boundary with volcanic soil, limited flat arable land, and ocean access on all sides—shaped the nation's fundamental character: dense coastal settlement, maritime culture, reliance on imported resources, vulnerability to natural disaster, and periodic forced confrontation with the outside world that ultimately drove the [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md). The sacred geography of the islands—mountains, rivers, rocks, and forests identified as homes of *kami*—is the foundation of [Shinto](../culture-society/shinto-religion.md) and permeates Japanese aesthetic and spiritual traditions.
