# Osaka

**Category**: Geography  
**Key figures**: Toyotomi Hideyoshi, Tokugawa Ieyasu, Chikamatsu Monzaemon, Ihara Saikaku, Homma Munehisa

## Summary

Osaka is Japan's third-largest city by population (approximately 2.75 million within city limits, 2023 census) and the economic and cultural capital of the Kansai region, situated on the delta where the Yodo and Yamato rivers empty into Osaka Bay, an inlet of the Seto Inland Sea on the southern coast of Honshu. The metropolitan area of Greater Osaka encompasses roughly 19 million people across Osaka, Hyōgo (Kobe), and Kyoto prefectures — Japan's second-largest urban agglomeration after Greater Tokyo.

The city has played a central role in Japanese history since antiquity. The area known as Naniwa served as an imperial capital under Emperor Kōtoku (r. 645–654 CE), and again briefly under Emperor Shōmu in the 740s. Its significance as a port and transit node between the Korean peninsula, the Yamato heartland, and points west made it a political and commercial anchor long before the age of castle towns. Osaka Castle, built by Toyotomi Hideyoshi beginning in 1583, announced the city's emergence as the military and administrative center of late-feudal Japan; its fall to Tokugawa Ieyasu in the Summer Campaign of 1615 ended the Toyotomi lineage and inaugurated over two and a half centuries of Tokugawa supremacy. Yet the Tokugawa shogunate transformed rather than diminished Osaka: stripped of political power, the city became the undisputed commercial capital of Japan, earning the epithet *tenka no daidokoro* ("kitchen of the realm") for its role aggregating and redistributing agricultural commodities from across the country.

The Dōjima Rice Exchange, established in Osaka in 1697, operated as the world's first organized futures market, where merchants traded standardized contracts for rice delivery months in advance — a financial innovation that predated European futures markets by nearly a century. Osaka's merchant class (*chōnin*) generated a distinctive urban culture of Kabuki theater, Bunraku puppet drama, popular fiction, and street cuisine that became the foundation of modern Japanese popular culture.

Meiji industrialization and the World War I export boom turned Osaka into Japan's leading manufacturing city, with textiles (the Osaka Spinning Company, founded 1882, became a model for the industry), iron and steel, and chemicals. The city endured severe firebombing during World War II — the Osaka Air Raids of March–August 1945 destroyed much of the urban core — but recovered rapidly to become a major postwar industrial and financial center. Osaka hosted Japan's first World Exposition (Expo '70) in 1970, attracting 64 million visitors and signaling Japan's arrival as a global economic power. A second World Exposition, Expo 2025, opened on Yumeshima artificial island in Osaka Bay on April 13, 2025.

## Ancient and Early Medieval Period (to 1583)

The Naniwa area (present-day Osaka) first achieved national importance under Emperor Kōtoku, who established the Naniwa-no-Miya Palace and issued the Taika Reform edicts (645–646 CE) there — the foundational administrative reforms that restructured Japanese governance along Tang Chinese lines. A second palace, the Naniwa-no-Miya of Emperor Shōmu, operated briefly in the 740s before the capital shifted permanently to Nara (710–784) and then Kyoto.

Through the Nara, Heian, Kamakura, and Muromachi periods, the Naniwa/Settsu area functioned as a crucial transit port and trading post connecting the capital region to the Inland Sea routes leading west toward Kyushu and Korea. The port of Ōsaka-no-Tsu (Osaka Harbor) handled continental imports and tax rice shipments from western provinces. Buddhist temples — particularly Shitennō-ji, founded 593 CE and traditionally attributed to Prince Shōtoku, making it among the oldest Buddhist temples in Japan — gave the area spiritual significance.

## Toyotomi Hideyoshi and the Castle Town (1583–1615)

Toyotomi Hideyoshi began construction of Osaka Castle (Ōsaka-jō) in 1583 on the site of the Ishiyama Hongan-ji fortress, a stronghold of the Ikkō-ikki Buddhist militia that Oda Nobunaga had besieged for a decade before demolishing in 1580. Hideyoshi's castle was among the largest in Japan, featuring a five-story keep atop an eight-story base, surrounded by concentric moats, and constructed with the forced labor of daimyo across Japan over three years. Hideyoshi moved his administrative headquarters from Kyoto to Osaka, making the city the effective political capital of a unified Japan from roughly 1590 until his death in 1598.

The Battle of Sekigahara (October 1600) shifted power to Tokugawa Ieyasu, but Hideyoshi's heir Toyotomi Hideyori remained in Osaka Castle. Ieyasu eliminated the Toyotomi threat in two campaigns: the Winter Siege of Osaka (1614–1615) ended in negotiated settlement and the deliberate filling of the outer moats; the Summer Campaign of Osaka (May–June 1615) resulted in the castle's fall, the deaths of Hideyori and his mother Yodogimi, and the end of the Toyotomi line. Ieyasu subsequently rebuilt the castle under Tokugawa control, with the current surviving castle tower dating from a 1931 reconstruction (the original Tokugawa keep was destroyed by lightning in 1665).

## Tokugawa Commercial Capital (1615–1868)

Under the Tokugawa shogunate, Osaka lost political primacy to Edo (Tokyo) but gained unrivaled commercial dominance. The shogunate designated Osaka a *tenryō* (directly administered domain), governing it through shogunal magistrates, which paradoxically gave the merchant class (*chōnin*) greater operational freedom than in domains governed by potentially extractive daimyo.

The Dōjima Rice Exchange emerged in the Dōjima district by the 1690s. By 1730 the shogunate officially licensed standardized rice futures trading. Merchants issued forward contracts (*chō-ai-mai*) specifying delivery of defined rice quantities at future dates, enabling hedging, speculation, and price discovery. This made Dōjima arguably the world's first organized financial futures market — the Chicago Board of Trade would not open until 1848. The Exchange also generated the first candlestick charting techniques, attributed to rice trader Homma Munehisa (1724–1803), which remain foundational to technical analysis globally.

The city hosted major warehousing operations (*kurashiki*) where daimyo from across Japan stored rice revenues shipped via the Inland Sea for sale or conversion to cash. Osaka's *fudasashi* (rice brokers) became the de facto bankers of the feudal economy, extending credit to daimyo against future rice revenues. By the late Edo period (18th–19th century), many daimyo were chronically indebted to Osaka merchant houses.

Bunraku puppet theater (*ningyō-jōruri*) flourished in Osaka, with playwright Chikamatsu Monzaemon (1653–1725) writing dozens of plays for the Takemoto-za theater — including *Sonezaki Shinjū* (The Love Suicides at Sonezaki, 1703) — that defined the genre. Kabuki also thrived in the Dōtonbori entertainment district. The popular prose fiction genre known as *gesaku* — light, often satirical novels — centered on Osaka writers. Ihara Saikaku (1642–1693), though active in late 17th-century Osaka, established the merchant-class literary tradition that flourished through the Edo period.

Osaka's cuisine traditions developed in this era: the city's location at the confluence of rivers and the sea provided abundant fish, shellfish, and vegetables. The *dashi* (stock) tradition emphasizing delicate kombu (kelp) flavors rather than the stronger katsuobushi-dominant stocks of Edo became a regional hallmark. Street food culture around the Namba and Shinsaibashi areas produced prototypes of modern dishes including *oshi-zushi* (pressed sushi) and *takoyaki* (octopus balls, though the modern form appeared only in the 1930s).

## Meiji Industrialization (1868–1912)

The Meiji Restoration integrated Osaka into the new national economy with unusual speed. The government railway between Osaka and Kobe — among the earliest lines in Japan, following the pioneering Shimbashi–Yokohama line of 1872 — opened in 1874, and the Osaka–Kyoto line was completed in 1877. The Osaka Cotton Spinning Company (Ōsaka Bōseki Kaisha), founded 1882, became Japan's first large-scale mechanized textile enterprise, operating imported British ring-frame machinery around the clock with two shifts — an organizational innovation that transformed the industry. Within a decade, Osaka and its satellite cities (Sakai, Kishiwada) dominated Japanese textile production, exporting to markets across Asia.

Osaka's industrial development in the Meiji era extended beyond textiles. The Osaka Iron and Steel Works (later Sumitomo Metal Industries), chemical plants, and machinery manufacturers concentrated in the Tempozan waterfront and Nishimachi industrial districts. The city's population grew from approximately 270,000 in 1868 to over 1.2 million by 1912, making it Japan's most populous city for much of the Meiji period before being overtaken by Tokyo.

The 1903 Fifth National Industrial Exposition (Naikoku Kangyō Hakurankai), held in Tennoji Park, attracted 4.3 million visitors and showcased Japanese industrial achievements internationally — a forerunner of Expo '70.

## Taishō Boom and Interwar Period (1912–1945)

World War I generated enormous demand for Japanese manufactures, and Osaka's industries ran at full capacity from 1914 to 1919. The city expanded into surrounding communities; the 1925 municipal boundaries expansion incorporated surrounding townships, pushing the official population above 2 million and briefly making Osaka Japan's largest city.

The interwar period saw labor organizing among Osaka factory workers intersect with the broader Taishō Democracy movement. The Kawasaki shipyard strikes of 1921 and textile mill strikes in Kishiwada drew national attention. See [Taishō Period](../history/taisho-period.md) for the broader political context.

**The Osaka Air Raids (1945)** were among the most destructive of the Pacific War. Eight major incendiary raids between March 13–14 and August 14, 1945 dropped a total of approximately 4,000 tons of incendiary ordnance over the city. The March 13–14 raid alone — conducted by 274 B-29 Superfortresses — killed approximately 3,900 people and destroyed 21 km² of the urban core. In total the raids destroyed approximately 35% of Osaka's built-up area and killed over 10,000 civilians. Osaka Castle's historic warehouses (*yagura*) and inner citadel were destroyed; the main keep (a 1931 ferro-concrete reconstruction) survived.

## Postwar Recovery and Modern City (1945–present)

Postwar reconstruction proceeded rapidly. The Osaka port, critical to import-dependent Japanese industry, was rebuilt and expanded through the 1950s. The city's economy diversified from textiles into chemicals (Sumitomo Chemical), pharmaceuticals (Takeda Pharmaceutical, founded Osaka 1781), electronics, and services.

**Japan World Exposition 1970 (Expo '70)** held in Senri Hills in northern Osaka from March to September 1970 attracted 64.2 million visitors — a world record at the time — and introduced Japan's postwar industrial achievement to a global audience. The Tower of the Sun sculpture by artist Tarō Okamoto remains on the Expo site, now a public park.

**Kansai International Airport**, built on an artificial island in Osaka Bay (construction 1987–1994), opened on September 4, 1994 at a construction cost of approximately ¥1.5 trillion. Designed by Renzo Piano, the terminal is among the longest buildings in the world at 1.7 km. The airport's island has been subject to ongoing subsidence, sinking approximately 13 meters since construction — a significant engineering challenge addressed through hydraulic jacking of the terminal structure.

The **Great Hanshin Earthquake** (January 17, 1995, magnitude 6.9 Mw) struck the Kobe area approximately 30 km west of central Osaka. While Kobe suffered catastrophic damage (over 6,400 deaths, 250,000 structures destroyed), Osaka experienced severe ground shaking and infrastructure disruption but far less building collapse due to greater distance from the fault rupture. The disaster prompted Japan's most significant revision of earthquake-resistant building standards since the 1981 code.

**Expo 2025 Osaka**, held on the artificial Yumeshima island in Osaka Bay (April 13–October 13, 2025) under the theme "Designing Future Society for Our Lives," marked the second World Exposition hosted in Osaka. Construction costs exceeded ¥2 trillion and drew international participation from over 160 countries.

Contemporary Osaka's economy centers on services, commerce, tourism, pharmaceuticals (Osaka Prefecture hosts the headquarters of Takeda, Dainippon Sumitomo Pharma, and Shionogi), and manufacturing. The city is a major convention and exhibition hub; the Osaka-Kobe-Kyoto metropolitan region accounts for approximately 18% of Japan's GDP.

## Cultural Identity

Osaka maintains a strong and self-conscious regional identity distinct from Tokyo. Osakan dialect (*Osaka-ben*, a variant of *Kansai-ben*) is associated with directness, commercial pragmatism, and wit; the city claims the cultural heritage of Japanese stand-up comedy (*manzai*), which became the dominant form of Japanese television comedy through groups like Downtown (Hitoshi Matsumoto and Masatoshi Hamada) and the talent agency Yoshimoto Kogyo (founded Osaka, 1912). The Dōtonbori canal district, with its neon-lit signs and elevated restaurant façades, is among Japan's most photographed urban streetscapes.

Osaka's food culture (*kuidaore*, "eat yourself bankrupt") centers on *takoyaki* (octopus balls), *okonomiyaki* (savory pancake), *fugu* (pufferfish), and kushikatsu (breaded skewers). The city has the highest per-capita density of restaurants of any prefecture in Japan. The Kuromon Ichiba ("Kuromon Market"), operating since the 1920s, serves as both working food market and tourist destination.
