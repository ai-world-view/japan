# Tokyo

**Category**: Geography  
**Key figures**: Tokugawa Ieyasu (established Edo as shogunal capital, 1603), Meiji Emperor (relocated capital to Edo, renamed Tokyo, 1869), Goto Shimpei (architect of post-1923 reconstruction)

## Summary

Tokyo, originally known as Edo (江戸, "Bay-entrance"), is Japan's capital city and the largest metropolitan area in the world. Located on the Kanto Plain at the southern tip of Honshu — Japan's largest island — the city occupies roughly 2,194 square kilometers in the Tokyo Metropolis administrative unit. The Greater Tokyo Area (comprising Tokyo, Kanagawa, Saitama, and Chiba prefectures) holds approximately 37.4 million people, nearly 30 percent of Japan's total population, making it a global megacity without parallel in size. Tokyo's GDP (metropolitan area) is estimated at approximately $2 trillion, placing it among the top three urban economies in the world alongside New York and Los Angeles.

In 1868, during the Meiji Restoration, Japan's new government decided to relocate the imperial seat from Kyoto, which had served as the imperial capital since 794, to Edo. The city was officially renamed Tokyo (東京, "Eastern Capital") in September 1868, and the Meiji Emperor physically took up residence there in 1869. This geographic shift formalized Tokyo's role as Japan's administrative and symbolic center, consolidating political power in a city that had already surpassed Kyoto in population and economic weight under the Tokugawa shogunate.

## Historical Development

### Edo: The Tokugawa Capital (1603–1868)

The site that became Tokyo was a minor fishing settlement before Tokugawa Ieyasu chose it as his military headquarters in 1603 following his appointment as shogun. The choice was strategic: the flat, defensible Kanto Plain offered fertile hinterland; the Sumida and Arakawa rivers provided transportation and fresh water; and proximity to the Pacific coast facilitated sea trade. Edo Castle, begun in the early 15th century and massively expanded under the Tokugawa, became the world's largest castle complex. By the late 17th century, Edo had grown into the world's largest city, with an estimated population of 1 million by 1720 — outpacing London, Paris, and Beijing. The shogunate's *sankin-kōtai* (alternate attendance) system, requiring all daimyo to spend alternating years in Edo and their domains, drove constant economic activity and population growth in the city.

### Meiji Transformation (1868–1912)

The Meiji government's decision to center modernization efforts on Tokyo transformed the city's physical and institutional landscape. Western-style government buildings were constructed in Marunouchi; the first railway — the 29-kilometer Tokyo–Yokohama line — opened in 1872; and in 1878 the Tokyo Stock Exchange was established, formalizing Tokyo's role as Japan's financial capital. The University of Tokyo (founded 1877) anchored the city's role as Japan's intellectual center. By 1900, Tokyo's population had reached approximately 2 million.

### The 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake

On September 1, 1923, at 11:58 a.m., a magnitude 7.9 earthquake struck beneath Sagami Bay, approximately 90 kilometers southwest of central Tokyo. The earthquake, fires ignited by overturned cooking stoves during the lunch hour, and the subsequent tsunami killed approximately 105,000 people across the Kanto region — making it one of the deadliest natural disasters in modern history. Roughly 570,000 homes were destroyed, and most of downtown Tokyo and Yokohama was reduced to ash. Interior Minister Goto Shimpei led an ambitious reconstruction plan that introduced broader avenues, parks, and reinforced concrete construction, shaping the modern urban grid. The earthquake also triggered a wave of anti-Korean violence amid rumors and panic, a dark episode in Tokyo's history.

### World War II and Postwar Reconstruction

Between March and August 1945, the United States Army Air Forces firebombed Tokyo in a series of raids, the most devastating of which — Operation Meetinghouse on the night of March 9–10, 1945 — killed an estimated 80,000 to 100,000 civilians and destroyed approximately 267,000 buildings across 41 square kilometers of the city. The destruction was comparable in scale to the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki. Postwar reconstruction, assisted by American Occupation authorities and driven by Japan's rapid industrialization, transformed Tokyo into a modern metropolis within two decades. The 1964 Tokyo Olympics served as a symbol of this reconstruction, prompting the opening of the Shinkansen (bullet train) between Tokyo and Osaka and the construction of the Metropolitan Expressway network.

## Administrative Structure

Tokyo is classified as a *to* (都, metropolis) rather than a standard prefecture, giving it a unique administrative structure:

- **23 Special Wards (特別区)**: The core urban area is divided into 23 self-governing special wards (*tokubetsu-ku*) including Chiyoda (home of the Imperial Palace and central government), Shinjuku (government and entertainment hub), Minato (embassies and corporate headquarters), Shibuya (youth culture and tech), and Asakusa's host ward of Taitō. Combined population approximately 9.7 million.
- **26 Cities**: The western Tama area includes cities such as Hachioji, Tachikawa, and Musashino.
- **5 Towns and 8 Villages**: Including the distant Izu and Ogasawara islands, which are administratively part of Tokyo Metropolis despite lying hundreds of kilometers into the Pacific.

The Tokyo Metropolitan Government, headquartered in Shinjuku's iconic twin-tower complex (designed by Kenzo Tange, completed 1991), governs the entire metropolis.

## Geography and Natural Hazards

Tokyo lies on the Kanto Plain, a sedimentary lowland underlain by thick alluvial deposits from the Tama, Arakawa, Edo, and Sumida rivers. Much of the waterfront, particularly the Koto and Edogawa wards and Tokyo Bay reclamation zones (notably Odaiba), sits on artificial landfill at or below sea level, creating vulnerability to both earthquakes and tsunami.

The city sits near the convergence of three tectonic plates: the Pacific Plate, the Philippine Sea Plate (subducting beneath the Eurasian Plate along the Sagami Trough), and the North American/Okhotsk Plate. This triple junction makes the Kanto region one of the most seismically active zones in the world. Japanese seismologists estimate a 70 percent probability of a magnitude 7.0 or greater earthquake striking the southern Kanto region within 30 years (as of 2013 estimates by the Earthquake Research Committee).

Following the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake (magnitude 9.0, epicenter off the coast of Miyagi Prefecture), which caused strong shaking in Tokyo, the city's earthquake preparedness infrastructure was substantially upgraded. The Metropolitan Area Outer Underground Discharge Channel — a massive underground cistern and tunnel system in Saitama, completed 2006 — mitigates flood risk from typhoon-driven river overflow, protecting eastern Tokyo.

## Neighborhoods and Cultural Districts

Tokyo's distinct neighborhoods reflect layered historical development:

- **Marunouchi / Chiyoda**: Administrative core; the Imperial Palace grounds, the National Diet Building, ministries, and Tokyo Central Station.
- **Shinjuku**: Home to the busiest railway station in the world (approximately 3.6 million daily passengers), the Tokyo Metropolitan Government building, the Kabukichō entertainment district, and the Golden Gai bar alley.
- **Shibuya**: Global center of youth culture and fashion; the Shibuya Crossing — known as the "Scramble" — is one of the world's most photographed urban intersections.
- **Harajuku**: Fashion subculture (Takeshita Street) and the Meiji Jingū shrine, a major Shinto sanctuary set in 70 hectares of forested parkland.
- **Akihabara**: Electronics retail and anime/manga culture; became the global symbol of *otaku* (enthusiast) culture from the 1990s onward.
- **Asakusa**: Edo-era sensibility preserved around Sensō-ji, Tokyo's oldest temple (founded 645 CE according to tradition), and the Nakamise shopping street.
- **Roppongi**: Diplomatic residences, nightlife, and major art museums (Mori Art Museum, National Art Center).
- **Ginza**: High-end retail and galleries, comparable in commercial density to the Champs-Élysées.

## Transportation Infrastructure

Tokyo operates one of the world's most extensive and punctual mass transit systems:

- **Rail**: Over 158 railway lines serve the metropolitan area. JR East's Yamanote Line (30 stations, 34.5 km loop) serves as the central spine. Tokyo Metro and the Toei Subway together operate 13 subway lines totaling 304 kilometers. Average delay across the JR East network is under 1 minute.
- **Shinkansen**: Tokyo Station is the eastern terminus of the Tōkaidō Shinkansen (to Osaka in approximately 2 hours 25 minutes) and the gateway to the Tōhoku, Jōetsu, and Hokuriku Shinkansen lines.
- **Airports**: Haneda Airport (Tokyo International) handles approximately 87 million passengers per year (pre-pandemic), making it one of Asia's busiest; Narita International Airport serves major long-haul international routes.
- **Roads**: The Metropolitan Expressway network spans approximately 327 kilometers; Route 246 (Aoyama-dori) and other arterials are chronically congested despite extensive commuter rail usage.

## Economic Role

Tokyo functions as Japan's undisputed economic capital. Key institutions headquartered in the city include:

- **Tokyo Stock Exchange (TSE)**: Founded 1878; the TSE (now Japan Exchange Group following the 2013 merger with the Osaka Securities Exchange) is the third-largest stock exchange in the world by market capitalization, with approximately $5.6 trillion in listed market cap as of 2023.
- **Bank of Japan**: Headquartered in Nihonbashi since 1882; the Bank of Japan is Japan's central bank and the primary lender of last resort, responsible for monetary policy and yen stability.
- **Corporate Headquarters**: Japan's ten largest companies by revenue — including Toyota, Mitsubishi, Sony, and SoftBank — maintain their principal Tokyo offices in the metropolitan area.

The city generates approximately 19 percent of Japan's GDP within the 23-ward core alone, rising to approximately 37 percent for the broader metropolitan area.
