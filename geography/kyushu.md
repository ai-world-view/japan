# Kyushu

**Category**: Geography
**Key figures**: Takamori Saigō, Shimazu Nariakira, Francis Xavier

## Summary

Kyushu is Japan's third-largest main island and the southwesternmost of the four primary landmasses, covering approximately 36,782 square kilometers. With a population of roughly 13 million people, it anchors Japan's southwestern region and has historically served as Japan's primary gateway to East Asia. Geographically, Kyushu sits at the convergence of the Philippine, Eurasian, and North American tectonic plates, making it one of Japan's most seismically and volcanically active zones. The island encompasses seven prefectures—Fukuoka, Saga, Nagasaki, Kumamoto, Miyazaki, Kagoshima, and Oita—each with distinct economic, cultural, and historical identities.

The island's major cities include Fukuoka (population approximately 1.6 million city proper, over 2.5 million metropolitan), Kitakyushu (approximately 940,000), Nagasaki (approximately 405,000), and Kagoshima (approximately 590,000). Fukuoka is the fastest-growing large city in Japan and is frequently ranked among Asia's most livable cities. The island is home to significant industrial zones—particularly around Fukuoka and Kitakyushu, which developed as textile, coal, steel, and chemical manufacturing centers in the Meiji era—as well as agricultural regions producing rice, citrus (most notably in Saga and Nagasaki), sweet potatoes, and shochu (distilled spirits, with Kagoshima and Miyazaki producing the highest volumes in Japan). Kyushu's volcanic topography, marked by calderas and active volcanoes such as Sakurajima and Mount Aso, has shaped its settlement patterns and cultural traditions for centuries.

## Geography and Geology

Kyushu's terrain is dominated by the Kyushu Mountains (Kyūshū Sanchi), which run north–south through the center of the island, rising to their highest point at Sobo-katamuki at 1,756 meters. The island's volcanic character produces some of Japan's most dramatic geological features:

**Mount Aso**: The world's largest active caldera, with a rim approximately 120 kilometers in circumference. Aso's central cone has erupted repeatedly in recorded history, with major eruptions in 1980, 2016, and 2021. Aso County and Aso City within the caldera are inhabited by approximately 47,000 people, making it one of the world's most populated volcanic calderas.

**Sakurajima**: An active composite volcano in Kagoshima Bay, formerly an island that was permanently connected to the Ōsumi Peninsula by lava flows during the 1914 eruption. Sakurajima is one of Japan's most continuously active volcanoes, erupting hundreds of times per year in small events, and is the subject of ongoing volcanic monitoring by the Japan Meteorological Agency. Kagoshima city (across the bay, population ~590,000) receives regular ashfall.

**Beppu**: Located in Oita Prefecture, Beppu hosts the largest volume of hot spring water output in Japan (and the second-largest in the world after Yellowstone), producing approximately 130,000 kiloliters per day from over 2,900 springs. The area's famous "eight hells" (*jigoku*) are distinctive hot spring pools with vivid colorations from mineral content.

**Unzen**: Nagasaki Prefecture's Mount Unzen is an active volcanic complex; its 1792 eruption produced the largest landslide in Japanese history, triggering a tsunami that killed approximately 15,000 people—one of the deadliest volcanic events in Japan's recorded history. Unzen erupted again in 1991, causing renewed devastation.

The Tsushima Strait and Korea Strait separate Kyushu from the Korean Peninsula (approximately 200 kilometers at the narrowest point), while the Bungo Channel separates eastern Kyushu from Shikoku.

## Historical Significance

### Gateway to Asia: The Portuguese and Christian Period (1543–1637)

Kyushu's proximity to the Asian continent made it Japan's first point of contact with Europeans. In 1543, Portuguese traders arrived at Tanegashima, an island off southern Kagoshima, introducing firearms (*tanegashima*) to Japan—a technology that dramatically transformed Japanese warfare during the [Sengoku Period](../history/sengoku-period.md). Eight years later, in 1549, Jesuit missionary Francis Xavier arrived in Kagoshima to begin the Christianization of Japan, winning early converts among local daimyo. At its peak in the early 17th century, the Kyushu Christian community numbered an estimated 300,000 to 750,000 believers, concentrated in Nagasaki, the Amakusa Islands, and the Shimabara Peninsula.

Following Tokugawa unification, the shogunate perceived Christianity as a political threat and began systematic persecution. The Shimabara Rebellion (1637–1638), centered in Nagasaki Prefecture, was the last large-scale armed uprising before the [Edo Period](../history/edo-period.md)'s long peace; an estimated 37,000 Christian rebels were killed when the Hara Castle fell. Afterward, the Tokugawa shogunate implemented *sakoku* (closed country policy), and Nagasaki's Dejima island became the only permitted point of contact with foreign merchants (Dutch and Chinese), giving Kyushu and especially Nagasaki an outsized role in Japan's intellectual and commercial contact with the outside world for over two centuries.

### Meiji Restoration and the Shimazu Domain

The Shimazu clan (*han*: Satsuma, headquartered in Kagoshima) and the Mōri clan (Chōshū, in present-day Yamaguchi Prefecture at the tip of Honshu) were the principal architects of the [Meiji Restoration](../history/meiji-restoration.md). Shimazu Nariakira (1809–1858), the enlightened daimyo, pioneered industrial modernization in Kagoshima—building Japan's first Western-style factories and a reverberatory furnace for iron smelting—before his early death. His successors steered Satsuma into the Sonno-jōi ("revere the emperor, expel the barbarians") movement that ultimately unseated the Tokugawa shogunate in the 1868 Boshin War.

Takamori Saigō (1828–1877), born in Kagoshima, became one of the central figures of the Restoration and later one of Japan's most famous tragic heroes. After the new Meiji government spurned his proposal to invade Korea, Saigō resigned and returned to Kagoshima, where he established private academies for former samurai. In 1877, the Satsuma Rebellion—the last and largest of the samurai uprisings against Meiji modernization—erupted under his reluctant leadership. Approximately 40,000 Satsuma samurai fought the government's conscript army before the rebellion was crushed at the Battle of Shiroyama (September 24, 1877), where Saigō died. He was posthumously pardoned and has since become a revered symbol of honor and resistance.

### Industrialization: Coal, Steel, and Heavy Industry

From the 1880s onward, Kyushu became the crucible of Japan's industrial revolution. The island sat atop extensive coal deposits, particularly in the Chikuho Coalfield (Fukuoka Prefecture), which at peak production in 1941 yielded approximately 17 million tons per year and supplied much of Japan's industrial and naval fuel. In 1901, the Imperial Yawata Steel Works (八幡製鐵所, present-day Kitakyushu) commenced operations, becoming the largest integrated steel mill in East Asia and the foundation of the modern Japanese steel industry. By the 1910s, Yawata produced the majority of Japan's domestically manufactured steel and pig iron, vital for the expanding Imperial Navy and industrial infrastructure. The Yawata works and related Kitakyushu industrial complex became one of the targets in the first American B-29 raids on Japan's home islands (June 15, 1944).

In 2015, the Yawata-Tobata region of the Meiji Industrial Revolution sites (including Yawata Works) was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site under "Sites of Japan's Meiji Industrial Revolution: Iron and Steel, Shipbuilding and Coal Mining."

### World War II: Nagasaki

On August 9, 1945, three days after Hiroshima, the United States dropped the second atomic bomb ("Fat Man," a plutonium implosion device) on Nagasaki. The bomb was detonated at 11:02 a.m. at an altitude of approximately 503 meters over the Urakami district—a historically Catholic community and Japan's largest Christian congregation. The explosion and its immediate aftermath killed an estimated 40,000–80,000 people (estimates vary widely due to record destruction), with total deaths from radiation and injury reaching approximately 80,000 by the end of 1945. Nagasaki's hilly terrain and the bomb's displacement from its intended aim point (the Mitsubishi armament complex) somewhat limited the destruction compared to the Hiroshima bombing, but the human and physical devastation was catastrophic. The Nagasaki Peace Park and Atomic Bomb Museum now draw millions of visitors annually.

## Modern Economy and Development

Kyushu's modern economy has diversified considerably from its industrial base. Key sectors include:

**Semiconductor and Electronics Manufacturing**: The island has become known as "Silicon Island" due to the concentration of semiconductor fabrication plants, particularly in Kumamoto Prefecture. TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) announced in 2021 a major fabrication facility in Kikuyo, Kumamoto, with production of 22nm and 28nm chips beginning in early 2024—a landmark foreign investment in Japanese semiconductor manufacturing supported by METI subsidies exceeding ¥476 billion (approximately $3.5 billion). Sony, Renesas, and TSMC have collectively made Kyushu one of Asia's most significant semiconductor clusters. A second TSMC facility (JASM 2) with more advanced 6nm process technology was announced in 2024. See [Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI)](../government-politics/ministry-economy-trade-industry.md) for industrial policy context.

**Automotive Manufacturing**: Toyota, Daihatsu, Honda, and Nissan all operate major assembly plants in Kyushu. The island's automotive industry employs several hundred thousand workers directly and indirectly, and vehicle exports through Kyushu's ports—particularly Hakata (Fukuoka) and Kanda (Fukuoka, adjacent to the Toyota Kyushu plant)—constitute a major share of Japan's automotive export value.

**Agriculture**: Kyushu accounts for a substantial share of Japan's food production. Saga and Fukuoka prefectures are significant rice producers. The island leads in sweet potato production (Kagoshima, Miyazaki), tangerines and citrus (Saga, Nagasaki), and tea (Kagoshima's Kirishima brand is among Japan's most recognized). Miyazaki and Kagoshima produce premium branded beef (Miyazaki Wagyu, Kagoshima Kuro-buta pork) exported globally.

**Geothermal Energy**: Kyushu's volcanic endowment makes it the center of Japan's geothermal power industry. Oita Prefecture's Hatchobaru geothermal plant (approximately 112 MW) is Japan's largest geothermal power station. Japan holds the world's third-largest geothermal reserves, and Kyushu hosts the majority of operating capacity—a resource increasingly important to Japan's energy transition goals under the METI-led Green Innovation Strategy.

**Fukuoka as a Startup Hub**: The Japanese government designated Fukuoka as a "National Strategic Special Zone" (*kokka senryaku tokku*) in 2014 to attract startups and foreign talent, with relaxed regulations on immigration for foreign entrepreneurs and streamlined business registration processes. Fukuoka has subsequently emerged as one of Japan's most dynamic tech startup ecosystems, with firms in SaaS, fintech, and AI. Population growth in Fukuoka Prefecture has bucked the nationwide trend, increasing from approximately 5.07 million (2010) to 5.14 million (2020) as rural-to-urban internal migration offset national depopulation trends.

## Culture and Heritage

Kyushu's cultural landscape reflects its layered history as a contact zone between Japan and the wider world. Hakata (Fukuoka), one of Japan's oldest port cities, developed an urban merchant culture (*hakata-mono*) with distinct textile traditions: Hakata-ori silk weaving, a technique dating to the 13th century when a Hakata merchant brought loom technology from Song Dynasty China, producing the distinctive ribbed *kenjo hakata* fabric used in obi sashes worn with kimono.

Kyushu's Christian heritage is most visible in Nagasaki, where the Urakami Cathedral (rebuilt after 1945), the Oura Cathedral (designated a National Treasure), and the "Hidden Christian" sites of the Amakusa and Goto Islands preserve the legacy of Japan's clandestine Christian communities who maintained their faith for over two centuries of prohibition, until the Meiji government permitted religious freedom in 1873. The Hidden Christian Sites of Nagasaki were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2018.

The Yoshinogari archaeological site (Saga Prefecture), together with the Itoshima area of neighboring Fukuoka Prefecture, ranks among the most significant archaeological sites in Japan, containing evidence of the Yayoi period culture (c. 300 BCE–300 CE) that transitioned Japanese society from hunter-gathering to wet-rice agriculture. Yoshinogari is believed by some researchers to be the site of the ancient polity of Yamatai described in Chinese Wei Zhi chronicles, and the site's moated settlement and artifacts offer a rare window into Japan's early cultural formation.
