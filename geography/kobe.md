# Kobe

**Kobe** (神戸, "gateway to the divine") is Japan's sixth-largest city by population and one of the country's most important deep-water ports, situated on the northern shore of Osaka Bay at the foot of the Rokko Mountains in Hyogo Prefecture. The city proper had a population of approximately 1.53 million as of 2023, with the greater Hanshin metropolitan area (including Osaka and Himeji) exceeding 19 million people. Kobe is the prefectural capital of Hyogo and serves as a gateway city between the Kansai interior — Kyoto, Osaka, Nara — and the rest of the world. Its history of openness to foreign influence, combined with devastating catastrophes and remarkable recoveries, has given Kobe a distinct cosmopolitan character unique among Japanese cities.

## Early History: Medieval Hyogo

The area now called Kobe was long known as **Hyogo** (兵庫, "arsenal"), a name referring to the government armory established nearby in the early Nara period. The Hyogo estuary served as a transit port for vessels traveling the Seto Inland Sea as early as the Nara period (8th century). During the Heian period, **Taira no Kiyomori** (1118–1181), the most powerful figure in Japan before the rise of the warrior shogunates, undertook a major harbor development at Fukuhara (within modern Kobe) around 1168, dredging the bay and improving anchorage to facilitate trade with Song-dynasty China. Kiyomori briefly moved the imperial capital to Fukuhara in 1180 — an attempt to shift political power to the Taira heartland — before the plan was abandoned within months under pressure from his rivals and the court.

During the Muromachi period, Hyogo was a trading port for the Nanboku-chō rivals and later for the Sengoku daimyo, and it hosted a significant Chinese merchant community engaged in the lucrative Ming-trade (*kangō bōeki*) under licenses allocated through the shogunate.

## The Treaty Port and the Meiji Opening (1868–1900)

Kobe's modern history began with Japan's forced opening. Under the 1858 **Ansei Treaties** (the Harris Treaty with the United States and its counterparts with Britain, France, Russia, and the Netherlands), Hyogo/Kobe was designated one of Japan's five new treaty ports. The port opened on **January 1, 1868** — the same year the Meiji Restoration began. Within weeks, the new Meiji government replaced the old Tokugawa administration, and Kobe became from its very inception a city identified with the new Japan rather than the feudal order.

Foreign merchants, diplomats, missionaries, and engineers rapidly established a **Foreign Settlement** on land reclaimed from the bay just east of the Ohama district. The settlement was organized along Western town-planning lines, with straight streets, European-style buildings, consulates, and churches. By the 1890s, more than 26 foreign consulates operated in Kobe, representing the city's role as Japan's principal gateway to the Western world. Foreign residents also settled in the hillside **Kitano-cho** district above the settlement, building Western-style residential mansions (*ijinkan*, 異人館). Over 200 *ijinkan* were built between 1868 and the 1930s; approximately 30 survive today as museums and tourist attractions, including the Danish-style "Windmill House" (*Kaze no Fuki Nuki Kan*), the English-style "Ben's House," and the German-style "Gothic House."

The city's strategic position on the Seto Inland Sea and its proximity to Kyoto (the imperial capital until 1869) and Osaka made it Japan's primary commercial and diplomatic port during the early Meiji era. It was at Kobe's port that the first shipment of Japanese raw silk for export reached foreign merchants in 1869, beginning the silk trade that would underpin early Meiji industrial development.

## Meiji Modernization and Industrial Growth (1868–1923)

The Meiji government invested heavily in Kobe's infrastructure. The **Osaka–Kobe Railway** opened on **May 11, 1874**, connecting Kobe to Osaka along Japan's second modern railroad (the first was the 1872 Shimbashi–Yokohama line). By 1886, the line had been extended to Osaka's Nanba area, and in 1889 the Sanyo Railway pushed westward from Kobe toward Hiroshima. The Kobe–Kyoto line opened in 1877.

The harbor was deepened and expanded throughout the 1870s–1890s. **Kawasaki Dockyard** was founded in Tokyo in 1878 by Kawasaki Shozo and relocated its major operations to Kobe in 1896, establishing the shipyard that became **Kawasaki Heavy Industries**, one of Japan's premier shipbuilders and manufacturers of aircraft, trains, motorcycles, and industrial machinery. **Mitsubishi Shipbuilding Kobe** established major facilities in 1905; both firms would become cornerstones of Japan's naval construction programs. By the late Meiji era, the harbor area between Kobe Station and Minatogawa included steel works, sugar refineries, flour mills, and spinning mills.

Kobe also became a center of the sake-brewing industry. The **Nada-Gogō** district (five villages of Nada, now part of the eastern ward of Nada-ku and neighboring Higashinada-ku) emerged as Japan's premier sake production area, benefiting from the mineral-rich "miyamizu" groundwater, the proximity of high-quality Yamadanishiki rice grown inland in Hyogo Prefecture, and the cool ocean breezes flowing through the Rokko Mountains. By the early 20th century, Nada produced approximately 30% of all sake sold in Japan, a share it largely maintains today.

**Kobe beef** emerged as a prized product during this period. Wagyu cattle (*Tajima-ushi*) were raised in the Tajima region of northern Hyogo Prefecture and brought to Kobe for slaughter and export. Foreign residents at Kobe encountered the marbled beef and spread its reputation internationally; the name "Kobe beef" attached to this cattle breed's premium product, which is now one of Japan's most globally recognized luxury food exports. Authentic Kobe beef must meet strict criteria: born and raised in Hyogo Prefecture, a Tajima-breed steer or virgin cow, slaughtered at licensed abattoirs in the prefecture, and meeting specific meat-quality and marbling grades (*BMS* of 6 or higher).

By 1900, Kobe had surpassed Yokohama as Japan's busiest port by cargo tonnage, handling over 40% of Japan's total import-export volume. Insurance companies, trading houses (*shōsha*), and banking institutions clustered downtown. Kobe's Sannomiya district developed as the commercial and retail core of the city.

## The Interwar Period (1923–1945)

The **1923 Great Kantō Earthquake** (magnitude 7.9), which devastated Tokyo and Yokohama and killed approximately 105,000 people, indirectly benefited Kobe. Several industries and financial institutions relocated operations to the Kansai region, which was undamaged, and reconstruction contracts for the east strengthened Kobe's industrial base. By the late 1920s, Kobe's port throughput had recovered and expanded.

The interwar period also saw significant growth in the city's **Chinese community**. Kobe's **Nankinmachi** (南京町, "Nanjing Town") Chinatown, initially established in the 1860s foreign settlement era, expanded through the early 20th century and today covers several city blocks in the former settlement area. It is one of Japan's three major Chinatowns (alongside those in Yokohama and Nagasaki), with over 100 Chinese restaurants, food stalls, and shops operating around its central **Gaito-mon** gate.

By the 1930s, Kobe's port was heavily integrated into the Japanese imperial war economy. Military procurement, naval shipbuilding, and logistics through the harbor expanded dramatically after the start of the Second Sino-Japanese War in 1937.

## World War II and Destruction (1945)

Kobe was one of Japan's most heavily bombed cities during World War II. The United States Army Air Forces' 20th Bomber Command (B-29 Superfortresses based in the Mariana Islands) mounted repeated raids on Kobe's port, shipyards, and industrial facilities beginning in June 1944. The **March 16–17, 1945 raid** — coordinated with raids on Tokyo, Nagoya, and Osaka — was the most destructive: 331 B-29s dropped approximately 2,300 tons of incendiary bombs on Kobe's densely packed wooden residential and commercial districts. The firestorm killed approximately **8,000–8,841 civilians** (estimates vary), destroyed over 65,000 buildings, and left roughly 170,000 people homeless. Over the course of the air campaign, more than a third of Kobe's urban area was reduced to ash.

Despite the devastation, Japan's military-industrial infrastructure in Kobe — the dockyards and heavy manufacturing — partially survived and continued operations until August 1945. The atomic bombings of Hiroshima (August 6, 1945) and Nagasaki (August 9, 1945) accelerated Japan's surrender, and Kobe ceased hostilities on August 15, 1945, with American occupation forces arriving in September.

## Postwar Recovery and the Economic Miracle (1945–1990)

Kobe's recovery began under the Allied Occupation (1945–1952). American engineers and Japanese workers collaborated to rapidly restore port infrastructure. The **Kobe Port** was the first major Japanese port to resume commercial operations after the war, reopening to limited traffic in 1947 and to full operations by 1950. The Korean War procurement boom (1950–1953) generated massive orders for steel, ships, and heavy equipment from Kobe's manufacturers.

By the 1960s, **Kawasaki Heavy Industries** was producing locomotives, aircraft components, and motorcycles alongside ships. Kobe became a center for **cement manufacturing** (Taiheiyo Cement, then Onoda Cement), **rubber** (Kobe Steel began rubber operations), **medical technology**, and **precision instruments**. The high-speed **Shinkansen** reached Shin-Kobe station in 1972 (though the station was placed high on the hillside — notoriously inconvenient relative to the downtown area — due to topographic constraints).

The **Portopia '81 Exposition** (March 20 – September 15, 1981) showcased Kobe's modern aspirations. Held on the purpose-built **Port Island** — an artificial island in Osaka Bay whose construction began in 1966 and was completed in 1981 — the exposition attracted 16.2 million visitors over six months. Port Island inaugurated a new model of urban development: large-scale land reclamation to create flat industrial and residential space in a city constrained by mountains. **Rokko Island** (construction 1972–1992) was a second such project, adding 580 hectares of reclaimed land east of the existing port.

Throughout the 1980s bubble economy, Kobe's real estate and commercial development expanded dramatically. The **Harborland** mixed-use development adjacent to Kobe Station and the **Meriken Park** waterfront opened in 1992, transforming formerly industrial waterfront into retail, cultural, and residential space.

## The 1995 Great Hanshin Earthquake and Reconstruction

At **5:46 a.m. on January 17, 1995**, the **Great Hanshin Earthquake** (Hanshin Daishinsai) struck with a Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA) magnitude of **7.3** (moment magnitude Mw 6.9). The epicenter was in Akashi Strait, approximately 20 kilometers southwest of Kobe city center. The shallow depth (16 km) and proximity to dense urban areas made it catastrophic.

The earthquake killed **6,434 people**, the great majority of them in Kobe. Approximately 102,000 buildings collapsed completely, and a further 80,000 were partially destroyed. The **Hanshin Expressway**, an elevated urban freeway running through the city, collapsed over a stretch of approximately 635 meters, with the road deck toppling sideways at a dramatic angle. The **Rokko Liner** automated guideway and **Portliner** systems were knocked out of service. In the Port, virtually all of the berths were rendered inoperable; the container terminal — at the time one of Asia's busiest — was completely shut down.

Fires broke out in dozens of locations in the immediate aftermath, burning through water-main breaks rendered fire-fighting nearly impossible. Approximately 7,500 fires were reported; the Noda-Minami district in Nagata-ku burned for over two days.

The death toll, while catastrophic, could have been far higher: the JMA's seismograph network had only limited early-warning capability in 1995, but Japanese building codes adopted in 1981 after the 1978 Miyagi earthquake proved their worth — buildings constructed after 1981 standards performed significantly better than older stock.

### Recovery

Reconstruction proceeded with remarkable speed. The National Government's extraordinary budget allocations (over ¥9 trillion in reconstruction spending through 2000) funded port rehabilitation, highway repair, and housing reconstruction. By the **end of 1996**, the Hanshin Expressway was repaired; by **December 1997**, Kobe Port's container terminal had been rebuilt to exceed its pre-quake throughput capacity. Many neighborhoods were rebuilt with new anti-seismic construction standards. The **Disaster Reduction and Human Renovation Institution** (DRI, opened 2002) — jointly funded by the national government and Hyogo Prefecture — became an international center for disaster-risk education, attracting experts from across the world.

The earthquake also prompted fundamental reforms. The disaster response failures — the Japanese Self-Defense Forces took over 17 hours to be formally requested, the government's initial response was critically slow — led to revisions in Japan's Disaster Relief Law, the introduction of a Disaster Management Bureau in the Cabinet Office, and new protocols for international disaster-assistance acceptance. Kobe's reconstruction became a model for post-disaster cities worldwide, informing responses to earthquakes in India, Turkey, China, and Haiti.

## Modern Kobe (1995–Present)

Kobe's economy diversified substantially in the post-1995 period. The city's traditional strengths in **shipbuilding**, **steel**, and **heavy machinery** were supplemented by growth in:

- **Medical and life sciences**: Kobe was designated a **Medical Industry City** (*Kobe Medical Industry Development Project*) in 2000, attracting pharmaceutical companies, medical device firms, and research institutes to Port Island. The **Riken Center for Biosystems Dynamics Research** opened in 2018.
- **Fashion**: Kobe has a historically significant fashion industry, rooted in its export-oriented textile trade and foreign-influenced taste. The **Kobe Collection** biannual fashion show is one of Japan's major fashion events.
- **Tourism**: The Kitano ijinkan district, Nankinmachi Chinatown, and Meriken Park waterfront are major domestic and international tourist destinations. The city receives approximately 30 million visitors annually.

The port today handles approximately **2.7 million TEUs** (twenty-foot equivalent units) annually, making it one of Japan's busiest container ports. Kobe's port specializations include automobile exports (Kobe handles shipments from the Toyota Kyushu and other western Japan assembly plants), raw-materials imports (coal, iron ore, grain), and chemical products.

The **Akashi Kaikyō Bridge** — opened April 5, 1998, connecting Kobe to Awaji Island and onward to Shikoku via the Naruto Bridge — held the record as the world's longest suspension-bridge central span (1,991 meters; total length 3,911 meters) from its 1998 opening until 2022, when Turkey's 1915 Çanakkale Bridge surpassed it. Its final span length is a direct legacy of the earthquake (its construction had been underway when the quake struck; the earthquake shifted the two towers slightly, requiring recalculation of the final span). The bridge reduced transit times across the Kii Strait and integrated the Kansai island chain into the national expressway network.

**Kobe Airport**, on an artificial island adjacent to Port Island, opened in 2006 as a reliever airport to Osaka's Itami and Kansai International airports, handling domestic routes.

## Geography and Climate

Kobe occupies a narrow coastal strip between the Rokko Mountains (highest point: Rokko-san at 931 m) and Osaka Bay. The mountains rise sharply from the coast, limiting the buildable area to a corridor 2–5 kilometers wide in most places, which explains the city's linear urban form and its aggressive land-reclamation projects. The geology is complex: the Median Tectonic Line (one of Japan's major fault systems) runs nearby, and the city sits within the broader Nankai Trough seismic zone.

The climate is **temperate oceanic** (*Köppen Cfa*): mild winters (average January low approximately 3°C, mean 6°C), warm to hot summers (average August high approximately 31°C, mean 28°C), and a distinct rainy season (*tsuyu*) in June–July. Annual precipitation averages approximately 1,300 mm. Typhoons occasionally make landfall or pass close during the Pacific typhoon season (June–November), sometimes bringing heavy rains to the Rokko slope catchments and causing localized flooding.
